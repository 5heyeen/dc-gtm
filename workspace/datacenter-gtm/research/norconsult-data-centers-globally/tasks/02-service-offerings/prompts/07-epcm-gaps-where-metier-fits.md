## The EPCM Oversight Gap: Where Independent PM Advisory Fits

### 1. What EPCM Does NOT Do — The Structural Scope Gap

The EPCM model places the owner in a paradoxical position: the owner retains all construction contracts, all cost risk, and all liability for project outcomes — yet delegates the management of those contracts to a reimbursable third party whose professional liability is capped at "reasonable skill and care," not results. This creates several hard scope gaps.

**Owner's governance is not EPCM's job.** The EPCM contractor manages construction workstreams on the owner's behalf, but does not manage the owner's internal decision-making, reporting to investors, stage-gate discipline, or capital authorization processes. These functions fall in a vacuum between the EPCM scope and whatever in-house capability the owner has. The PwC analysis of EPCM models explicitly flags this: the EPCM contractor's scope of services must be "aligned with its level of accountability and the risks it is assuming" — and accountability for investor reporting, board approval of CAPEX variations, and portfolio governance simply does not sit with the EPCM contractor.

**Strategic and contract packaging decisions are pre-EPCM.** The EPCM firm is typically engaged after FEED or at least after the project development strategy has been set. The critical decisions about contract packaging (how to split civil, MEP, power, and IT fit-out into separate direct contracts), supplier qualification, and risk allocation are made before the EPCM contractor is on board in full capacity. An owner with no independent advisor making these decisions is essentially asking the EPCM firm to advise on a contract structure from which the EPCM firm will benefit — structurally problematic.

**Commissioning handover and operational readiness** are a perennial EPCM gap. DC commissioning requires an owner-side technical representative with independent authority to issue acceptance and punch-list sign-off. The EPCM firm, which managed the construction, cannot credibly certify its own work to the owner — yet this is precisely the role that is often left unfilled.

---

### 2. Where the Owner Is Most Exposed — Lifecycle Vulnerability Map

**Pre-FEED (Highest structural risk):** This is where concept decisions, power sizing, phasing strategy, contract packaging, and site infrastructure choices are locked in. Errors here compound through every subsequent phase. The owner is most exposed here because the EPCM firm either is not yet engaged or has a conflict in advising on a scope that will determine its own future revenue. PwC's 2024 energy transition report confirms that EPCM/Delivery Partner engagement during early feasibility "requires careful governance to avoid added complexity or scope creep."

**During Construction (Cost and change order exposure):** The reimbursable structure of EPCM contracts means the EPCM firm's revenue scales with project complexity and duration. As Mastt notes explicitly: "there could be conflicts of interest if the contractor prioritizes increasing project costs to maximize their fees." A practical illustration: "if a Construction Manager is calling for 25 site staff, the reality is that the project may only require 17 site staff." Without an independent advisor validating change orders and staffing claims, the owner has no check on this dynamic.

**At Commissioning (Contractual handover risk):** The EPCM firm manages punch-list and acceptance processes on the owner's behalf — but the owner needs an independent technical authority to certify acceptance, particularly when that acceptance is a contractual trigger for lender draw disbursement or investor milestone certification. The Lender's Independent Engineer function "issues drawdown certificates — arguably the most financially significant part of the job. Without this certificate, lenders do not release funds." Bulk's €410M senior secured loan from ABN Amro, Citibank Europe, and Nordea almost certainly carries construction progress and milestone conditions requiring independent technical certification — conditions the EPCM contractor cannot self-certify.

---

### 3. The EPCM Oversight Gap — The Structural Conflict of Interest

Freshfields' 2025 International Arbitration Trends Report documents the structural problem directly: "The Shift from EPC to EPCM: A Recipe for More Complex Arbitrations?" identifies the EPC-to-EPCM shift as creating "increasingly complex disputes" because "the more complex allocation of liability inherent in EPCM structures" produces contested cost recovery and delay attribution claims.

PwC states this more directly: EPCM creates a situation "verging on an alliancing style contract without taking the final step of openly creating a 'no blame' environment. It becomes increasingly difficult to apportion blame and pursue a Contractor for breach of contract in an integrated team approach where representatives of the Owner and the Contractor work together and make decisions jointly."

**The EPCM oversight gap in its structural form: when a project gets into trouble, the EPCM firm's interest is in protecting its own cost recovery and professional liability position — not in giving the owner an unvarnished assessment of what went wrong and what it will cost to fix it.** An independent advisor owes no duty to the EPCM firm and has no interest in the EPCM firm's fee recovery — making it the only party positioned to give the owner a genuinely impartial view.

FIDIC's own analysis confirms this: "the EPCM Contractor is not liable for delivering the project but rather is liable for delivering its services in accordance with contract requirements to the standard of skill and care required" — meaning cost overruns fall entirely to the owner, and the EPCM contractor bears professional liability only, not outcome liability (Loots, FIDIC 2007).

### 4. What Metier's Independence Makes Possible

Four specific capabilities an EPCM contractor structurally cannot provide:

**Independent cost estimate validation.** The EPCM firm produces cost estimates for its own workstreams and for subcontractor packages it manages. An EPCM firm cannot credibly challenge its own estimates. Metier, with no contractual relationship to Norconsult or any construction subcontractor, can benchmark EPCM cost estimates against comparable Norwegian and European DC projects and validate (or challenge) change order claims without any conflict. → Metier Service 1 (Contract, Procurement & Equipment Planning) and Service 2 (Project Execution & PM with independent cost control).

**Independent milestone certification for investors and lenders.** Bulk's €410M loan facility carries draw conditions tied to construction milestones. The lenders will require independent technical certification — a Lender's Independent Engineer function — that the EPCM contractor cannot perform for itself. The Independent Engineer's role is explicit: "the key is to be independent so that opinions on the technical aspects of the project are not biased either in favor of the lenders or the developer/owners." → Metier Service 4 (Owner's Engineer/PMC).

**Governance and investor reporting.** Bulk's institutional lenders (ABN Amro, Citibank Europe, Nordea) will require standardized progress reporting, variance analysis, and milestone tracking at a level of rigor the EPCM contractor neither produces nor is contractually obligated to produce in investor-facing format. → Metier Service 5 (Portfolio & Project Owner Governance), directly addressing "high reporting burden" as a primary funding-driven pain point.

**Strategic challenge of EPCM scope and contract packaging.** Before or during an EPCM engagement, an owner needs to challenge whether the EPCM's recommended package structure, procurement sequence, or staffing model is genuinely optimal. A competitor like Norconsult does not advise clients to reduce Norconsult's own role. Metier can.

### 5. Documented Cases: When EPCM-Led Projects Suffered from Absent Independent Advisory

**FOAK processing facility (Troutman Pepper mega-project failure analysis):** A reimbursable EPCM services contract for a first-of-a-kind processing facility revealed "FEED work-product was poor and incomplete, with inaccurate scale-up of the owner's proprietary process and missed or under-designed utility systems." The EPCM contractor had produced and validated its own FEED — with no independent review. Absence of an owner's engineer at FEED stage allowed fundamental engineering errors to propagate into construction.

**Mega-project systemic pattern:** Large international engineering projects "typically exceed their budgets by 20–45% and lag behind schedule by 40–60%." The structural arbitration evidence in Freshfields 2025 is itself documentation of the gap — the disputes reaching arbitration are precisely those that well-structured independent owner advisory would have intercepted early (change order validation, delay attribution, cost recovery claims).

---

### Implications for Metier's Positioning Against Norconsult

Norconsult occupies the EPCM role on the Bulk N01 project — which means it **cannot** offer Bulk (or any future client with a similar EPCM structure) the following without structural conflict: independent cost review, investor/lender milestone certification, change order challenge, or unbiased procurement advice on contract packaging.

Metier's competitive position against Norconsult is therefore most credible in exactly these areas — not by competing to run construction management, but by occupying the independent advisory layer that sits above it.

**The Bulk N01 context is particularly compelling:** a €410M institutional loan on a live construction project, with a reimbursable EPCM contractor managing the works, and — as far as public sources reveal — no documented independent technical advisor or owner's engineer on the owner side. **That is the gap.**
