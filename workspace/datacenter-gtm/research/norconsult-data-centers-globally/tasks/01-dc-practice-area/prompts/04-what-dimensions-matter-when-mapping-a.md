# Prompt 04: what-dimensions-matter-when-mapping-a

> **Prompt:** What dimensions matter when mapping a competitor's practice area: division name, geographic ownership, headcount, leadership seniority, client base, and revenue attribution? How do you weight these dimensions, and what does each reveal about practice maturity?

---

## Framework for Assessing a Competitor's Practice Area Maturity: Seven Diagnostic Dimensions

### Dimension 1: Organizational Identity and Division Name
**Weight: Medium** | **Observability: High**

Reveals whether the practice is a named, ring-fenced unit or an informal cluster. Observable from: website service pages, LinkedIn division names, job postings. Sweco UK has an explicitly named "Mission Critical" division with a named Division Manager (Claes Andersson). Ramboll retains "EYP Mission Critical Facilities, part of Ramboll" brand.

*Blind spot:* Names can be aspirational without real resourcing behind them.

### Dimension 2: Headcount and Specialization Depth
**Weight: High** | **Observability: Medium**

Scale, delivery capacity, and specialist-to-generalist ratio. Observable from: LinkedIn title searches, job postings, acquisition press releases. Ramboll grew from near-zero to "hundreds of dedicated DC experts" through two acquisitions (EYP MCF 2022, i3 Solutions 2024). WSP acquired kW MCE at 175 employees in 2021.

*Blind spot:* LinkedIn overcounts generalists listing DC as a sector; acquisition figures include overhead roles.

### Dimension 3: Leadership Seniority and External Visibility
**Weight: High** | **Observability: High**

Whether practice leaders are recognized sector experts who speak at DCD/Uptime events and publish in specialist press. Observable from: conference speaker lists (DCD Nordics, Data Centre World), published bylines, LinkedIn, award shortlists.

*Blind spot:* High-visibility leaders may be rainmaker-only; check if they are billable.

### Dimension 4: Geographic Ownership and Delivery Footprint
**Weight: Medium-High** | **Observability: High**

True multi-geography capability vs. single-market boutique. Observable from: service pages citing geographies, case studies, CoE announcements (WSP South Africa CoE), country-specific specialist team pages (Sweco Finland: "Finland's only interdisciplinary DC design team").

*Blind spot:* Single engineers listed as "presence" vs. 10-person resident teams — look for project references in claimed geography.

### Dimension 5: Client Base Composition and Reference Projects
**Weight: High** | **Observability: Medium**

Whether practice wins hyperscaler/wholesale mandates or smaller enterprise/colo work. Also signals value-chain position: design-only vs. owner's engineer vs. full EPCM. Observable from: website case studies, award entries, procurement prequalification documents.

*Blind spot:* Flagship references may be from teams that have since departed (post-acquisition brain drain is common). Check if named project leads are still at the firm.

### Dimension 6: Revenue Attribution and Practice Contribution
**Weight: Highest when observable** | **Observability: Low**

Financial weight of the practice. Observable from: annual reports, acquisition announcements (Ramboll cited "$60M 2025 DC revenue target" post-EYP — a rare, highly diagnostic disclosure), BD+C Giants 400 DC billings rankings.

*Blind spot:* Most engineering consultancies don't break out practice-level P&L publicly.

### Dimension 7: Service Line Breadth and Value-Chain Position
**Weight: Medium-High** | **Observability: High**

End-to-end capability (site selection → feasibility → design → owner's engineer → commissioning → ops advisory) vs. point solution (MEP design only). Observable from: service pages, job posting skill requirements, case study scope descriptions.

*Blind spot:* Breadth of listing ≠ depth of delivery. Compare service claims against case study evidence.

---

### Weighting Summary

| Dimension | Diagnostic Weight | Observability |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue attribution | Highest | Low |
| Client base / references | High | Medium |
| Headcount / specialization | High | Medium |
| Leadership visibility | High | High |
| Service line breadth | Medium-High | High |
| Geographic footprint | Medium-High | High |
| Division name / identity | Medium | High |

**Most actionable read:** Start with leadership visibility (easiest) and acquisition history (reveals strategic intent). Triangulate with client references. Revenue attribution — when available — trumps everything.

---

### Applied to Norconsult

Norconsult's "data center" capability is not explicitly branded as a named practice area. Its Norconsult Digital subsidiary (~300 employees) focuses on software/data services for AEC — a different value chain from DC engineering design. This signals either a nascent capability embedded in broader building/energy engineering, or deliberate under-branding. Diagnostic moves: check Finn.no/LinkedIn for MEP/mission critical job postings, look for named staff with DC project histories, search for Norconsult in Norwegian DC operator press releases.
