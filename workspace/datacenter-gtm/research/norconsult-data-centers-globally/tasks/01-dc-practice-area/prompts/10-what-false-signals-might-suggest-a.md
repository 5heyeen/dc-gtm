# Prompt 10: what-false-signals-might-suggest-a

> **Prompt:** What false signals might suggest a named data center practice exists at an engineering consultancy when in fact it does not? What tests distinguish genuine practice depth from marketing artifacts?

---

## False Signals of a Named Data Center Practice: A Diagnostic Framework

### Catalogue of Common False Positives

**1. Single High-Profile Reference Project**
A consultancy wins one large DC project through a general engineering or MEP bid. It appears prominently in case studies, award submissions, and LinkedIn posts. The project is real, but a practice requires a portfolio, not a project.

*Test:* Ask for two or three additional DC references. If the firm struggles, the first project was likely opportunistic.

**2. Individual Employee Expertise, Not Organisational Capability**
One or two engineers with prior DC experience join the firm. Their CVs and LinkedIn profiles list DC competencies. The firm gains genuine knowledge assets, but they are person-dependent — if those individuals leave, the capability disappears.

*Test:* Check LinkedIn tenure and background of named DC staff. If they joined recently from a DC-specialist firm, the "practice" may be those two people.

**3. Reactive Service Page or Marketing Layer**
DC demand surges (as it has since 2022–2024 in Norway due to AI/power infrastructure investment). A marketing team adds a "Data Centers" tile, pulls quotes from one reference project, republishes. No underlying methodology, no named team, no thought leadership, no specialist recruitment behind it.

*Test:* Check page creation/last-modified date vs. the DC investment wave. Check if it links to methodology documents, white papers, or specialist team bios. Shallow pages have none.

**4. Award Nominations and Press Mentions**
A single project generates award entries and press coverage, creating a searchable public record that aggregators treat as evidence of ongoing practice.

*Test:* Count distinct projects behind the mentions. If every mention traces back to the same project, the breadth is illusory.

**5. Partnership or Framework Agreement**
A consultancy joins a framework (Statsbygg, Forsvarsbygg, or a hyperscaler vendor panel) that includes DC scope. Membership signals eligibility, not experience.

*Test:* Ask which DC projects have actually been called off the framework.

---

### Application to Norconsult

**Potentially misleading signals:**
- Norconsult's power and energy division does legitimate work on grid connections for DC sites. This is real engineering — but it is **grid consultancy that happens to serve a DC client**, not a DC practice. The same team does identical work for factories, offshore installations, and hospitals.
- Individual Norconsult engineers appear on LinkedIn with DC project mentions. Given ~5,000 employees, this is statistically expected even with zero organizational focus on DC.
- "Industry and technology buildings" in their website capability statements is broad enough to include DC without specialization.

**What would confirm genuine practice depth:**
- Named practice group or competence center with a dedicated head (visible on org chart or in job postings)
- Repeat DC clients across multiple distinct projects (not the same client/project family)
- Published technical guidance specific to DC (PUE optimisation, Tier classification, cooling redundancy design)
- Active recruitment for DC-specific roles (finn.no + LinkedIn for `norconsult datasenter`)
- Participation in DC industry bodies (DCD, DCOS, Green Mountain advisory groups)

**What would refute it:**
- No dedicated DC job postings over the past 18 months
- No named DC practice lead findable via LinkedIn or company website
- All DC project references trace back to one or two projects in the same geographic cluster
- No subsidiary or division name associated with DC on proff.no/brreg.no

**Key diagnostic question:** Is Norconsult's DC work a **downstream effect of their power grid and MEP dominance in Norway** (supplier relationship), or have they built **forward-looking DC-specific processes** (practice competitor to Metier's PM offering)? The former competes on engineering design; the latter would compete on PM, governance, and owner-side advisory.
