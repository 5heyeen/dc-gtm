> **Prompt:** What are the common mistakes when trying to map a competitor's internal practice structure from public sources? What causes analysts to overestimate or underestimate a rival's capability depth?

---

## Common Mistakes When Mapping a Competitor's Internal Practice Structure

### Top 6 Analytical Errors

**1. Conflating headcount with capability depth**
Counting LinkedIn profiles with "data center" in their title inflates apparent capability. Norconsult's DC work likely concentrates in a small technical nucleus (3–8 people) embedded in a larger MEP/infrastructure division. A single experienced principal may run what five mid-level staff cannot replicate.

**2. Treating job ads as org charts**
Active recruitment signals intent, not current state. A firm advertising for a "Data Center Project Manager" may be building from scratch. Conversely, absence of DC-specific job ads doesn't mean dormancy — mature practices recruit through networks, not public postings.

**3. Anchoring on named project references**
Public project references reflect what a firm *chooses* to publicize. Norwegian consultancies with public-sector clients often face confidentiality norms that suppress disclosure. Norconsult's work for Statsbygg, Forsvarsbygg, or hyperscaler clients may leave almost no public trace.

**4. Inferring practice structure from website taxonomy**
A website services menu reflects marketing decisions, not operational structure. "Data centres" as a service line tells you nothing about whether it's a 2-person offering or a 50-person department with dedicated templates and QA.

**5. Mistaking association membership for active capability**
Norwegian consultancies hold memberships in NorSIS, GreenHouse, RIF, etc. Membership is often firm-level and administrative rather than capability-demonstrating.

**6. Recency bias from search results**
Searches surface recent content disproportionately. A firm active in DC 2019–2021 but subsequently deprioritized will still appear in results, creating a false impression of ongoing activity.

---

### Cognitive Biases That Distort Readings

- **Capability mirroring**: Projecting your own firm's structure onto the competitor — assuming similar team compositions, PM-to-specialist ratios, governance layers.
- **Prestige halo**: Norconsult is large and respected, so analysts may unconsciously upgrade all capability assessments, including DC, when evidence only supports general engineering strength.
- **Confirmation bias**: Once a hypothesis forms ("Norconsult has a strong DC practice"), subsequent searches retrieve confirming evidence more readily.

---

### Nordic/Norwegian-Specific Risks

- **Thin public disclosure norms**: Norwegian AS companies with <500 employees have minimal mandatory reporting. Practice-level economics are opaque.
- **Network opacity**: Norway's small professional market means three or four phone calls between practitioners would reveal more than 40 hours of desk research.
- **Norwegian-language content gaps**: Significant tender documentation, municipal planning records (reguleringsplaner), and industry proceedings exist only in Norwegian — underindexed by English search tools. Analysts relying on English searches will systematically underestimate Norconsult's regional footprint.
- **Cross-practice staffing invisibility**: Specialists routinely work across practices on an ad-hoc basis in small Nordic markets. A structural engineer with DC expertise contributes meaningfully to DC engagements but appears in no dedicated practice listing.

---

### Calibrating Confidence Levels

| Evidence Type | Confidence Ceiling |
|---|---|
| Named project reference with deliverable detail | Medium — confirms past capability, not current |
| Job ad with specific technical requirements | Low-Medium — signals intent |
| LinkedIn profile with DC keywords | Low — unverified, self-reported |
| Proff.no / Brreg.no financials | Low for practice-level inference |
| Tender award documentation | Medium-High — confirms scope and client trust |
| Interview with former employee or client | High — rarely available in desk research |

Flag any finding resting on a single source. Flag any inference about current capability from evidence older than 24 months.

---

### What "Unknown Unknowns" Look Like

- **Embedded capability in a parent division**: Norconsult may deliver DC services entirely through its electrical/MEP engineering division with no "DC practice" label — practice-oriented searches return nothing, yet real capability exists.
- **Confidential frame agreements**: A multi-year framework agreement with a major DC operator is completely invisible from public sources yet signals deep institutional knowledge.
- **Recent strategic hires not yet visible**: A senior hire made 3–6 months ago may not yet appear in updated LinkedIn data, tender references, or press releases.
- **Knowledge transfer from adjacent sectors**: Norway's offshore O&G engineering sector produces PMs with commissioning skills highly transferable to DC work. Norconsult recruits from Aker Solutions / Equinor-type backgrounds.

**Key implication:** Treat any public-source competitive assessment of a Norwegian consultancy's practice depth as a **lower bound, not a point estimate**. The true capability is almost always somewhat higher than publicly attributable, because the best work leaves the least public trace.
