# Prompt 07: what-do-norconsults-publicly-named-service

> **Prompt:** What do Norconsult's publicly named service lines and industry sectors reveal about how they segment their market? Is "data center" named explicitly, and if so, under which parent service/division?

---

## Norconsult's Service Taxonomy and Data Centre Positioning

### Overall Service Structure

Norconsult organises its market-facing services into ~seven top-level domains on norconsult.com:

1. **Infrastructure** — roads, railways, airports, ports
2. **Building and Property** — residential, commercial, healthcare, educational
3. **Industry** — process-intensive and power-intensive industrial sectors
4. **Renewable Energy** — hydropower, wind, solar, power systems, EPCM
5. **Digitalisation** — digital services, BIM, AEC tools
6. **Transport**
7. **Water/Environment**

Norconsult segments primarily by **end-use sector and physical asset type** — not by service type (PM, engineering design are cross-cutting capabilities, not top-level labels).

---

### Data Centres: Named Explicitly Under "Industry"

**URL:** `https://norconsult.com/services/industry/data-centres/`
**Hierarchy:** Home > Services > Industry > Data Centres

Data Centres is one of ~11 named sub-sectors under Industry, alongside: Aquaculture and Fisheries, Food Industry, Pharmaceutical, Logistics, Mining, Green Industry (hydrogen/CCS/PtX), Mechanical Industry, Metallurgical/Chemical, Oil and Gas, Pulp/Paper/Forestry.

The DC page describes the offering as: *"interdisciplinary expertise and experience from power-intensive industrial facilities and associated security requirements,"* covering feasibility/location analysis through Pre-FEED, FEED, detailed design, and construction follow-up.

---

### What the Placement Signals

**1. Framed as industrial plant, not a building project.** Industry division DNA is EPCM for complex, process-intensive facilities (O&G plants, pharma, metallurgy). DC is positioned in the same category — power-dense industrial infrastructure — not as commercial real estate. Opposite of a building-centric/architect-led framing.

**2. Cross-disciplinary, single-source delivery.** The service description stresses interdisciplinary expertise spanning structural, electrical, power supply, mechanical, process, safety, and sustainability — one team covering all engineering streams.

**3. Power and grid infrastructure is a differentiator.** The Bulk Infrastructure N01 Støleheia EPCM (2023–2026) confirms: Norconsult designed two 420 kV/145 kV transformer stations and new 420 kV transmission lines. This shows their DC model bundles heavy power infrastructure with site/building engineering — a capability most pure Building and Property consultants lack.

**4. Green/sustainability narrative is embedded.** References "finding green solutions" for sustainable DCs, consistent with Norwegian hyperscale demand driven by cheap renewable hydro power.

**5. No dedicated "mission critical" label.** Unlike Ramboll (standalone "Data Centres & Mission Critical Facilities" under Technology), Norconsult bundles DC within the broad industrial plant universe. Strategically coherent for the Norwegian market (DC clients like Bulk Infrastructure are industrial land developers, not IT companies) but may limit ability to signal specialist depth to hyperscalers expecting a dedicated mission-critical practice.

---

### Summary Judgment for Metier

Norconsult's DC delivery model is driven by **process engineering and EPCM teams**, not project management or building consultancy teams. Their strength is in EPCM (which bundles design authority with PM), whereas Metier's offering is **independent PM/PMO without design responsibility**. They are not direct substitutes and can potentially co-exist on the same project.
