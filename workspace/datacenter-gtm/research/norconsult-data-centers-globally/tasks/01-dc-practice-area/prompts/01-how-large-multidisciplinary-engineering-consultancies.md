# Prompt 01: how-large-multidisciplinary-engineering-consultancies

> **Prompt:** How do large multidisciplinary engineering consultancies typically organise specialised practices like data centers — as standalone business units, centres of excellence, sector overlays, or informal communities of practice?

---

## How Large Multidisciplinary Engineering Consultancies Organise Data Center Practices

Large engineering consultancies have converged on four main organisational models for specialist practices like data centers. Each produces different market-facing visibility and competitive outcomes. The DC sector's hypergrowth since 2021 has accelerated structural choices that previously evolved slowly.

---

### The Four Main Models

**1. Standalone Business Unit (or Named Practice with Dedicated P&L)**

This is the highest-commitment model: a named, branded practice with its own leadership, headcount, and sometimes a separate P&L. Arup is the clearest example among the multidisciplinary majors. Arup's Data Centres practice is explicitly branded on its website, with named regional business leaders — Gareth [surname withheld] as UKIMEA Data Centre Business Lead, Stephen Griffin in Europe, Stefan Sadokierski in APAC, and Jennifer DiMambro as Americas Science, Industry & Technology Business Leader. The practice has its own service catalogue ("All data centre services"), thought leadership pipeline, and client-facing identity. Ramboll went further through acquisition: it absorbed EYP Mission Critical Facilities (founded 1996, over 70 million sq ft of critically powered space designed) as a semi-autonomous unit, which still operates under the brand "EYP Mission Critical Facilities, Part of Ramboll" with its own website (eypmcfinc.com), while being integrated into Ramboll's ~300-expert global DC network.

*Competitive advantage:* Maximum market visibility, clear client entry point, ability to attract and retain specialist talent, and coherent proposition to hyperscalers and colocators. This model produces the most recognisable DC brand in the market.

*Risk:* High fixed cost; can create tension with the broader matrix organisation; siloing risk if the unit becomes isolated from the firm's other disciplines (civil/structural/environmental) that add value in DC campus projects.

**2. Centre of Excellence (CoE) with Global Delivery Model**

WSP is the most explicit example. In 2021, WSP acquired kW Mission Critical Engineering (175 staff, Troy NY), retaining it as a specialist CoE that operates under the "Global Mission Critical Network" umbrella. WSP simultaneously operates a separate CoE in South Africa — 26 multidisciplinary engineers — explicitly positioned to support global DC clients with lower-cost, high-BIM-capability delivery. Their South Africa team reduced design delivery from ~6 months to 8 weeks through standardised supermodels. The CoE model allows WSP to concentrate expertise in nodes while drawing on the wider firm for structures, civils, and environmental permitting.

*Competitive advantage:* Knowledge concentration, consistent methodology, cost-effective delivery at scale. Particularly effective for hyperscale programmes where a client repeats the same design across multiple sites.

*Risk:* CoEs can become internally focused and lose client-facing identity. If the CoE is perceived as a back-office function rather than a market-facing practice, it underperforms commercially. WSP mitigates this by prominently naming kW MCE in client materials.

**3. Sector Overlay (Matrix Model within a Global Business Area)**

Arcadis operates primarily through this model. Its DC work sits within the "Places" Global Business Area and the "Technology" sector — a matrix overlay that cuts across Arcadis's geographic regions and service lines (Design & Engineering, Programme/Project Management, Cost Management). Arcadis has 229 active DC projects globally and claims support for over 15 GW of capacity, making it one of the largest players by project volume. In early 2025, Arcadis acquired a German DC design business to deepen the overlay in a high-demand market. Mott MacDonald similarly operates through a sector/market overlay without a prominently branded standalone DC practice, with DC work distributed across its Buildings and Infrastructure sectors.

*Competitive advantage:* Maximises cross-selling; allows the firm to bundle DC design with permitting, environmental, and infrastructure services (power grid, water). Well-suited for clients (e.g. hyperscalers) who need one firm to manage planning permission, grid connection, and building design simultaneously.

*Risk:* Weakest market-facing visibility. A sector overlay is hard to brand externally. Clients who want "data centre specialists" may perceive the firm as a generalist. Internal competition for resources (DC projects competing with other sectors for the same structural engineers) can slow response times.

**4. Informal Community of Practice (CoP)**

Sweco and, to some extent, Ramboll pre-acquisition operated this way. Sweco has named DC pages (sweco.co.uk, sweco.fi) and is active in the German Data Center Association, but its DC work appears to be delivered through multidisciplinary regional teams rather than a globally branded standalone unit. Sweco Finland's LUMI supercomputer work represents a high-end technical cluster, but it is not structured as a formal CoE with its own P&L or dedicated headcount ring-fenced for DC work. Jacobs operates in the opposite direction — ENR's #1 Data Center Design Firm, with 17 million sq ft and $30bn in construction value over ten years — suggesting a more formalised practice, but Jacobs markets it primarily as part of its Advanced Manufacturing and High-Tech sector rather than a named DC practice.

*Competitive advantage:* Flexibility; low overhead; allows the firm to staff projects dynamically.

*Risk:* Lowest market visibility, hardest to scale, no dedicated career path for DC specialists, and vulnerable to talent attrition to firms with more structured practices.

---

### Which Model Produces the Most Visible, Market-Facing DC Practice

The **standalone business unit / named practice model** consistently produces the strongest external brand. Arup's DC practice and Ramboll/EYP MCF are routinely ranked among the Top 10 data centre M&P firms by Data Centre Magazine and Data Centre Dynamics. Building Design + Construction's "Top 60 Data Center Engineering Firms for 2024" rankings confirm that firms with named, dedicated practices dominate visibility. WSP's CoE model (via kW MCE) also performs well because it retains the acquired firm's brand identity.

---

### Recent Restructuring Examples

- **Ramboll / EYP MCF acquisition (c. 2022–2023):** Ramboll formalised its DC practice by acquiring a specialist firm rather than building from scratch, retaining the EYP brand as a sub-brand.
- **WSP / kW Mission Critical Engineering acquisition (February 2021):** Transformed WSP's DC practice from a sector overlay into a named CoE with dedicated headcount and market identity.
- **Arcadis / German DC design business acquisition (February 2025):** Deepened the sector overlay model in the DACH market without creating a standalone unit.
- **Arup / CFT partnership (announced 2024–2025):** Arup partnered with CFT to augment its design practice with construction-phase advisory.

---

### Summary Assessment

The spectrum runs from **Arup** (standalone practice, highest visibility) through **WSP** (CoE model, strong specialist brand) to **Arcadis** (sector overlay, highest project volume but lower specialist identity) to **Sweco/Mott MacDonald** (informal to moderate, lower market-facing visibility).
