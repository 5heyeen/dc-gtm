# Prompt 06: what-would-a-competitive-intelligence-analyst

## Competitive Intelligence Framing: Mapping Norconsult's Data Center Practice

### The Diagnostic First Question

The single orienting question a competitive intelligence analyst asks first is:

**"Is this a practice area, or is this a project?"**

This deceptively simple question is the master key. It separates a firm that has built a repeatable, resourced capability from one that has opportunistically taken on a few data center engagements because a client asked. The answer restructures every subsequent line of inquiry.

A secondary diagnostic question follows immediately: **"Who owns it — and are they selling it?"** Meaning: is there a named practice lead with a public mandate, a dedicated team, a go-to-market identity, and internal P&L accountability? Or is "data center expertise" a label retroactively applied to project experience that lives inside a generalist engineering or architecture group?

---

### Why These Questions Come First

These questions reveal three things simultaneously:

1. **Strategic intent.** A formal practice with named leadership signals a board-level decision to compete in the segment. An ad-hoc cluster of projects signals opportunism. The competitive threat is categorically different in each case.

2. **Capability depth.** A true practice accumulates proprietary tools, templates, procurement frameworks, and benchmarks. A project-by-project firm re-learns each time. Knowing which camp a rival occupies tells you where their bids will be strong (integrated delivery, system-level design) and where they will be thin (PMO rigor, cost benchmarking, multi-site rollout consistency).

3. **Hiring signal.** If the rival is building a practice, they are hiring for it. Job ads are the earliest public evidence of strategic build-out — often appearing 12–18 months before a formal market announcement.

---

### The Information Cascade

Knowing whether Norconsult's DC work is a practice vs. projects immediately unlocks:

- **Organizational mapping:** Who are the 3–5 people who carry this knowledge? Are they senior enough to own client relationships, or junior delivery staff?
- **Client base inference:** Practice-stage firms pitch for anchor clients and publish references. Project-stage firms keep individual engagements quiet.
- **Partnership structure:** Are they acting as lead consultant, subcontractor to a tier-1 EPC, or design-only? Each implies a different competitive surface.
- **Pricing posture:** A practice competes on differentiation; a project team competes on availability and relationships.

- No published named "Data Center Practice" with a dedicated go-to-market identity found.
- LinkedIn/Finn.no searches for Norconsult + datasenter/data center roles show generalist infrastructure engineers rather than DC-specialist hires — consistent with project-stage positioning.

**Gap for Metier:** Norconsult competes on technical design depth. Metier's differentiation should emphasize owner-side project management, milestone governance, and investor-facing reporting — capabilities Norconsult is not structured to provide and does not market.
