## How Leading DC Advisory Firms Handle the Engineering-vs-Advisory Boundary

Turner & Townsend and Mitchell McDermott resolve the boundary through vertical integration: they carry both PM/cost advisory and DC-specific MEP engineering in-house. T&T's 500+ DC project track record (10+ GW) includes cost management, scheduling, and Owner's Engineer roles — but they have dedicated MEP engineers who can interrogate cooling topology and power redundancy designs. Mitchell McDermott goes further with wholly owned subsidiaries (G.T Nordics for electrical switchboards, NordicEPOD for prefabricated power modules), effectively becoming a hybrid advisory/EPC firm. COWI operates differently: it is primarily an engineering consultancy that has expanded into PM advisory — on the Skygard OSL1 NOK 2.4B project, COWI holds the byggherreombud role precisely because its PM capability is backed by in-house fire engineering, power systems, and cooling design. The common pattern is **engineering credibility as the license to lead advisory**: the firm that can technically interrogate the engineering can credibly manage the owner's interests.

## Standard Partnership Model: PM Consultancy + Engineering Firm

The industry-standard model for a PM consultancy without embedded engineering is **integrated delivery with clearly documented scope splits**, not loose referral:

- **PM consultancy leads**: contract strategy, programme controls, cost management, governance, stakeholder management, investor reporting, and procurement strategy. The PM firm holds the client relationship and is accountable for delivery.
- **Engineering firm follows as named sub-consultant or consortium partner**: provides technical due diligence, design review, constructability verification, and specialist deliverables (power system studies, cooling CFD, fire engineering, grid connection studies). The engineering firm is contracted through or alongside the PM firm.
- **Role division in practice**: On day-to-day engagements, the PM firm chairs client meetings and owns the programme; the engineering firm provides technical sign-off. For lender's technical advisory (a mandatory independent role), the engineering firm often takes the lead with PM backstop — because technical credibility is what lenders are buying.

The critical governance rule: **whoever faces the client must hold final accountability**. Ambiguity about this is the single most common source of client confusion and inter-firm conflict.

## Metier + Norconsult: Recommended Structure

The dc_services_all.md data shows 20+ services marked "Potential for Norconsult: Yes" — from grid connection strategy and power systems design to fire engineering, BREEAM, geotechnical surveys, and cooling systems. This is too pervasive for a referral model; it requires a structured arrangement.

**Recommended: Integrated Joint Pitch with Contractual Scope Protocol**

1. **Joint pitch, Metier as lead**: Both firms appear in the proposal. Metier is the prime contractor; Norconsult is a named sub-consultant. Client has one contract and one point of accountability.
2. **Pre-agreed scope allocation matrix**: Before any engagement, a written scope matrix assigns each service line to either Metier-led or Norconsult-led. Engineering deliverables (power studies, cooling design, fire engineering, EIA) are Norconsult-led; governance, programme, cost, and commercial services are Metier-led.
3. **Avoid formal JV**: A JV creates shared liability, governance overhead, and fee-pooling complexity for a market that is still being developed. Use sub-consultancy or consortium agreements instead, with clear IP and liability allocation.
4. **Referral-only is insufficient**: Without a structured arrangement, Metier loses visibility on the engineering work scope (and vice versa), fees get carved up unpredictably, and clients receive inconsistent advice at the boundary.

## Risks of the Partnership Model

**1. Client confusion about accountability**: If Norconsult gives a grid connection timeline and Metier builds the programme around it, a slip creates a blame dispute. Mitigation: single PM lead owns all schedule commitments; engineering inputs are flagged as "subject to Norconsult's technical confirmation."

**2. Fee dilution**: On a 2–5% advisory fee for a €500M project, splitting with an engineering partner can halve Metier's net revenue. Mitigation: price the partnership into the proposal from the start; avoid situations where Norconsult is brought in mid-engagement at Metier's cost.

**3. Conflict of interest if Norconsult is also a design firm on the same project**: Norconsult cannot simultaneously be Metier's Owner's Engineer sub-consultant (advising the owner on design quality) and the design engineer for the same facility. This is the independence trap. Mitigation: clarify at pitch stage whether Norconsult is in design or advisory mode — never both on the same project.

**4. Competitive displacement**: Norconsult's growing DC ambition (EPCM role on Bulk N01, references at Lefdal Mine, Telenor, DigiPlex) means it could pitch DC projects independently and position Metier as unnecessary overhead. Mitigation: formalize the partnership with a teaming agreement that defines exclusivity for Norwegian DC advisory — ensuring neither party undercuts the other.
