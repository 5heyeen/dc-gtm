# Chunk 02: buyer-psychology-objections
> **Topic:** DC operator buying process and objection handling
---

## 1. How DC Operators Buy Advisory Services

**Direct appointment** is the dominant route, especially at early stages. A CEO or VP Construction who knows the firm — or has been referred by a board member, investor, or peer operator — calls directly. No tender, no RFP. This is how most first engagements happen in the Nordic market.

**Referral** is the trigger for direct appointment. PE sponsors and infrastructure funds are particularly powerful referral nodes: they impose advisors they trust across portfolio companies. Board-level relationships and warm introductions from contractors or equipment suppliers are secondary referral channels.

**RFP/competitive tender** applies when the operator is institutional, has governance provisions requiring it (CAPEX above a threshold, investor board approval), or is procuring a large, sustained engagement (e.g., full PMO or multi-site Owner's Engineer). These are won or lost on sector references, day-rate competitiveness, and the perceived seniority of the team proposed.

**Framework agreements** are rare at project level among Norwegian DC operators today, but are emerging as larger operators (Green Mountain, DigiPlex) standardize supply chains. Positioning Metier on a framework requires existing relationships and at least one delivered project as proof.

**BD implication:** The realistic entry path for Metier is referral → direct appointment for a scoped, time-limited engagement (feasibility support, procurement advisory on a single package, governance sprint). RFP wins without prior references are very low probability. The BD strategy must prioritize relationship-building with PE sponsors, board members, and project directors already known to Metier from other sectors — not cold outreach to procurement teams.

## 2. Real Decision-Makers by Service Type

| Service | Primary Decision-Maker | Secondary Influence | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Owner's Engineer / PMC | CEO or CFO (for first-time developers); Board (when investor-required) | PE sponsor / board member | PE sponsors often mandate OE; internal champion is CEO |
| Project Execution & PM | VP Construction / Project Director | CEO | Operational hire — decided by whoever owns delivery |
| Contract & Procurement | VP Construction / CPO | CFO (for high-value contracts) | Technical decision, but CFO signs off on cost exposure |
| Portfolio Governance | CFO or COO | Board / PE investor | Driven by investor reporting requirements — PE sponsor is the real pressure point |
| Early-Phase Advisory | CEO | Board / investors | Strategic decision; CEO owns it but needs board alignment |
| Strategy Advisory | CEO / CCO | Board | C-suite decision; sometimes board-initiated |
| Competence Development | HR Director / VP Construction | CEO | Delegated operational decision; smaller budget |

**Key pattern:** For PE-backed operators, the PE sponsor (often not present in the room) is the true buyer for governance, reporting, and OE services — they impose the requirement, and the operator's CEO executes it. Pitching governance to a CEO without connecting it to investor expectations will fail.

## 3. Top 4 Objections and Counters

**Objection 1: "You have no data center references."**
Counter: Reframe the value — DC projects fail on project management fundamentals (scope control, contractor management, cost control), not on data center knowledge. Metier has delivered NOK 800+ billion in complex capital projects across energy, defence, and infrastructure — environments with comparable MEP complexity, long-lead equipment, and tight commissioning windows. The DC-specific knowledge sits with the operator's technical team; what they lack is the execution backbone. Offer a scoped, de-risked first engagement (e.g., procurement strategy for one package) to prove it.

**Objection 2: "We'd rather hire a specialist DC consultancy" (Turner & Townsend, Mitchell McDermott, Arcadis).**
Counter: International DC specialists bring DC pattern recognition but limited Nordic market depth — Norwegian regulations, contractor market, grid connection processes, and security clearance requirements are where local expertise matters. Metier has the regulatory and contractor relationships; the DC overlay is learnable and can be supplemented by DC-specialist subcontractors where needed. For Norwegian operators, a strong local PM backbone beats a global DC brand with no Norwegian footprint.

**Objection 3: "We're building an internal team — we don't need external PM."**
Counter: The internal team will take 6–18 months to reach full effectiveness, and the critical procurement and early construction decisions happen now. Metier can accelerate the internal team rather than replace it — providing governance frameworks, templates, and mentoring that build lasting internal capability. This is not a dependency play; it is a competence transfer play.

**Objection 4: "This is too early — we're still in the planning phase."**
Counter: The planning phase is exactly when advisory input has the highest leverage and lowest cost. Decisions made in early-phase (contract packaging strategy, phasing approach, site selection trade-offs) are 10–100x cheaper to correct before procurement than after. Early-phase advisory also creates a natural on-ramp to execution-phase work, without requiring a new procurement cycle.

## 4. Latent Pain Points — What DC Operators Need But Don't Say

**Investor translation burden.** Operators find it exhausting to translate technical project status into the financial and milestone language PE sponsors and lenders require for board packs and covenant compliance reports. They rarely ask for help with this — but they spend disproportionate time on it. A consultant who can produce investor-grade reporting as a byproduct of normal project controls is solving a real pain.

**Contractor leverage.** First-time DC developers don't know if they're getting a fair price or if their EPC contractor is exploiting their inexperience. They won't admit this directly, but it drives anxiety about every major commitment. Procurement advisory that independently benchmarks proposals and stress-tests contractor risk allocation directly addresses this without requiring the operator to confess ignorance.

**Schedule credibility gaps.** Operators announce timelines driven by investor commitments or customer LOIs, then quietly realize their current team and contractor lineup cannot deliver them. They need someone to either validate the timeline (with a credible methodology) or create the narrative for why a revised timeline is acceptable — without triggering investor alarm. Project controls and realistic planning serve this need.

**Governance theatre avoidance.** Many operators implement governance structures because investors require them, then find those structures consume management time without improving decision quality. They want governance that satisfies investors but doesn't slow them down — lightweight, investor-legible, and operationally unobtrusive. This is a design problem, not a compliance problem.
