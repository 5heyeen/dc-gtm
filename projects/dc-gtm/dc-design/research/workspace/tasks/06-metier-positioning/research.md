# Metier Positioning in the DC Engineering & Design Value Chain

## Executive Summary

Metier cannot and should not try to replicate what CTS Nordics, Norconsult, or Schneider Electric do. Each of those firms occupies a distinct position in the DC engineering value chain — design-build contractor, engineering consultancy, and equipment vendor respectively. Metier's natural position is **on the owner's side of the table**, providing the project management backbone that enables DC operators to make informed decisions, manage their contractors and consultants effectively, and maintain control of schedule, cost, and quality.

This synthesis identifies **five specific service opportunities** where Metier can insert itself in the DC engineering and design process, mapped to specific moments in the project lifecycle and specific operator archetypes.

---

## 1. The DC Engineering Value Chain — Who Does What

Based on the research across all five subtasks, the DC engineering value chain has four distinct layers:

| Layer | Players | What They Do | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Equipment Vendors** | Schneider, Vertiv, Huawei, Eaton, ABB | Provide reference designs, modular components, pre-fabricated power/cooling units | Schneider EcoStruxure reference designs |
| **Design-Build Contractors** | CTS Nordics, Fluor, DPR, Turner & Townsend | Integrated design + construction, sometimes with in-house manufacturing | CTS NordicEPOD + design-build for Bulk |
| **Engineering Consultants** | Norconsult, COWI, Multiconsult, Sweco, Ramboll | MEP design, structural/civil engineering, EPCM services | Norconsult EPCM on Bulk N01; COWI total advisor on Skygard |
| **Owner-Side Advisors** | PM consultants, owner's engineers, owner's reps | Project management, requirements management, design review, procurement advisory, quality oversight | NS Nordics PM on 4 Norwegian DC projects |

**Metier belongs in Layer 4 — Owner-Side Advisory.** This is not a gap in the market; it is a growing need as more PE-backed and first-time developers enter the Norwegian DC market without in-house project execution capability.

## 2. Five Service Opportunities for Metier

### Opportunity A: Owner's Project Requirements (OPR) + Delivery Model Advisory

**When:** Before any engineering or contractor engagement (Phase 1: Planning)

**What:** Help the operator define their technical and commercial requirements, then advise on the right delivery model (Design-Build, EPC, EPCM, multi-prime) based on the operator's experience level, risk appetite, and project pipeline.

**Why Metier:** Most Norwegian DC operators are new to the industry. The choice between totalentreprise (NS 8407), EPC, and EPCM is consequential — it determines the entire project structure. Metier's strength in contract strategy and early-phase advisory (VPU/Verdistyrt Prosjektutvikling) maps directly to this need.

**How it works with engineering partners:** Metier leads the OPR workshops and delivery model selection. If the operator chooses EPCM, Norconsult (or similar) would be the EPCM provider. If the operator chooses Design-Build, Metier helps evaluate contractors (including CTS). If the operator uses vendor reference designs (Schneider), Metier coordinates the gap analysis.

**Target operators:** Finance-first developers (Pattern A), first-time operators, PE-backed companies.

---

### Opportunity B: Basis of Design Management (with Norconsult)

**When:** Transition from planning to design (between Phase 1 and Phase 2)

**What:** Package a "Basis of Design as a Service" offering where Metier manages the OPR-to-BoD process and Norconsult produces the engineering content. The BoD covers power architecture, cooling strategy, redundancy levels, tier targets, and compliance requirements (EN 50600, Uptime Institute).

**Why Metier:** The BoD is not just an engineering document — it requires translating business requirements (investor expectations, phasing strategy, tenant mix, budget constraints) into technical parameters. This translation is project management, not engineering.

**Commercial model:** Fixed-fee engagement, typically 1-3% of estimated construction cost. For a 10-50 MW project (NOK 500M-2B), this is NOK 5-30M. The BoD becomes the procurement document for tendering design-build or EPC contracts — giving Metier a natural pathway into the next project phase.

**Target operators:** All archetypes. The BoD is a universal entry-point service.

---

### Opportunity C: Design Management & Review (30/60/90% Gates)

**When:** During Phase 2 (Design), regardless of delivery model

**What:** Provide independent design review at the 30%, 60%, and 90% milestones. Ensure the evolving design remains aligned with the OPR and BoD. Manage cross-discipline coordination. Challenge design decisions that increase cost or schedule risk.

**Why Metier:** In a Design-Build or EPC model, the contractor controls the design. The owner needs independent eyes to validate that what's being designed actually meets their requirements — especially when using standardized/modular designs from CTS or Schneider that may not perfectly match the operator's needs. The research on standardized design failure points (already in the repo) documents exactly this gap.

**How it works:** Metier's team (potentially with Norconsult technical support for deep MEP review) attends design review meetings, reviews drawings and specifications at each milestone, flags deviations from the BoD, and manages the design change process.

**Target operators:** Operators using Design-Build or EPC contractors. Especially relevant when the contractor is CTS (design-build with standardized approach) and the operator wants to ensure their specific requirements are met.

---

### Opportunity D: Procurement & Long-Lead Item Management

**When:** Phase 2 (late design) through Phase 3 (construction)

**What:** Manage procurement strategy, tender processes, supplier qualification, and long-lead equipment tracking. Critical MEP equipment lead times — transformers (16-40 weeks), generators (20-40 weeks), UPS (12-30 weeks), switchgear (12-52 weeks) — anchor the entire project schedule.

**Why Metier:** Equipment procurement is the single biggest schedule risk in DC construction. A transformer ordered 2 months late can delay the entire project by 2 months. Metier's procurement expertise (already a core service) is directly applicable. In EPCM models, procurement is explicitly part of the scope. In Design-Build models, the owner often retains procurement of critical items (OFCI — Owner Furnished Contractor Installed) to maintain control.

**Target operators:** All archetypes, but especially PE-backed operators where delay = IRR erosion.

---

### Opportunity E: Multi-Vendor / Multi-Contractor Program Management

**When:** Phase 3 (Construction) through Phase 4 (Commissioning)

**What:** Manage the interfaces between multiple contractors and vendors on a DC campus. Coordinate the design-build contractor (CTS), the civil contractor (Skanska), the grid connection contractor (Eltel/Statnett), module vendors (Schneider/Vertiv), and specialist sub-contractors. Manage the 5-level commissioning process.

**Why Metier:** Large DC campuses (like Bulk N01 or Google Skien) use multi-prime contracting with 3-10+ direct owner contracts. Someone must manage the interfaces, resolve conflicts, track progress, and coordinate commissioning. When the EPCM consultant (Norconsult) handles this, it's bundled. When the owner uses separate design and construction contracts, the PM coordination role is open.

**Target operators:** Multi-site operators (Pattern D), large campus developers, operators using multi-prime contracting.

| **CTS Nordics** | Design-build contractor (sits on the contractor side) | Metier sits on the owner's side — independent oversight of CTS's delivery |
| **Norconsult** | Engineering consultant / EPCM provider | Metier provides PM when Norconsult is design-only; or independent PM overlay when Norconsult is EPCM |

## 4. The Critical Insight: When Norconsult is EPCM, Metier's Role Shrinks

The research reveals an important tension: when an engineering consultancy like Norconsult takes an EPCM contract (as on Bulk N01), they absorb project management, procurement, and construction management — leaving less room for a separate PM consultant.

**Metier's strongest opportunities are therefore in scenarios where:**

1. The owner has **not yet selected** a delivery model — Metier advises on the right approach (Opportunity A)
2. The owner uses **Design-Build or EPC** — the contractor handles design/construction, but the owner needs independent oversight (Opportunities C, D, E)
3. The owner uses an engineering consultant **for design only** (not EPCM) — Metier provides the PM and procurement management separately (Opportunities B, C, D)
4. The owner wants **independent assurance** even over the EPCM consultant — Metier provides portfolio governance and investor reporting (relevant for PE-backed operators)
5. The project is in **early phase** before any engineering or contractor is engaged — Metier defines the project and helps select the delivery team (Opportunities A, B)

**The early phase is Metier's strongest entry point.** Once an EPCM consultant or Design-Build contractor is engaged, they tend to absorb management scope. Metier needs to be in the room before those decisions are made.
