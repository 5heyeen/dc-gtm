# Who Is Investing in Data Centers in Norway?

## Comprehensive Investor Landscape Research

**Date:** April 2026
**Scope:** Major investors, investment firms, PE funds, sovereign wealth funds, infrastructure funds, and other capital providers actively investing in or developing data centers in Norway.

---

## Executive Summary

Norway's data center market has emerged as one of Europe's fastest-growing digital infrastructure hubs, attracting an estimated **€1.8–2.7 billion per year** in investment. The market was valued at approximately **USD 1.15–1.65 billion in 2024–2025** and is projected to reach **USD 2.08–2.97 billion by 2030** (CAGR 10–13%).

As of March 2026, **103 data centers** are registered in Norway (up from 58 in July 2025), with **3.5 GW of reserved grid capacity** and an additional **5.4 GW in the queue** — representing demand ~30x current consumption levels.

### Investor Categories Active in Norway

1. **Hyperscalers** (Google, Microsoft/OpenAI, TikTok) — direct builds and long-term leases
2. **PE/Infrastructure Funds** (IPI/STACK, 3i Infrastructure, BentallGreenOak) — platform acquisitions and growth equity
3. **Israeli Real Estate** (Azrieli Group) — acquisition and expansion of Green Mountain
4. **Norwegian Utilities & Corporates** (Hafslund, Telenor, Statkraft, Aker) — JVs and partnerships
5. **Norwegian Entrepreneurs** (Peder Nærbø/Bulk, John Fredriksen/Geveran) — founding and growth capital
6. **Canadian Pension Funds** (CPP Investments) — large-scale platform acquisitions
7. **Energy PE** (HitecVision) — JV participation
8. **Renewables Investors** (Magnora) — emerging entrants

### Notable Absences

- **Norway's $2.1T Sovereign Wealth Fund (NBIM)**: Explicitly stated data centers will NOT be a focus of their next strategy (Bloomberg, Dec 2025)
- **Meta/Facebook**: Chose Sweden (Luleå) and Denmark (Odense) over Norway
- **Apple**: Chose Denmark over Norway, partly due to lower energy taxes
- **Amazon/AWS**: No significant Norwegian DC presence identified
- **Gulf SWFs (ADIA, Mubadala, QIA)**: Active globally in DCs but no specific Norwegian deals found

## 1. Hyperscaler Direct Investments

### 1.1 Google — Skien Data Center

**Investment:** €600 million ($646M)
**Location:** Gromstul, Skien (~130 km south of Oslo)
**Capacity:** 240 MW initial (applied for 840 MW total)
**Status:** Under construction, operations expected 2026

- Google's **first data center in Norway**, announced 2022, ground broken 2024
- 200-hectare site; phased build: 20 MW → 100 MW → 120 MW (second stage toward 2030)
- Construction partner: **Skanska** (NOK 1.1B contract for groundwork); **ZAUNERGROUP** for fit-out
- 99% carbon-free energy; net-zero target by 2030
- ~2,000 construction jobs, ~100 permanent positions
- Deloitte estimates €590M in value creation (2024–2025)
- Google's global CAPEX: ~$91B in 2025, projected $175–185B in 2026 (mainly AI DCs)

- [DLA Piper — Construction of Google's EUR600M DC in Norway](https://www.dlapiper.com/en/insights/publications/2024/05/construction-of-googles-eur600-million-hyperscale-data-centre-in-norway)
- [Datacenter Forum — Google started building 240MW DC](https://www.datacenter-forum.com/google/google-started-building-240mw-data-center-in-norway)

### 1.2 Microsoft / OpenAI / Nscale / Aker — Stargate Norway (Narvik)

**Investment:** $6.2 billion (5-year compute deal)
**Location:** Kvandal, Narvik, Northern Norway
**Capacity:** 230 MW initial, expandable to 520 MW
**Status:** Targeting completion Q4 2026

- Landmark partnership announced from Oslo: Microsoft, **Nscale**, and **Aker** for renewable AI infrastructure
- Joint venture between Nscale and Aker launched July 2025 as **"Stargate Norway"**
- Target: **100,000 NVIDIA GPUs** by end of 2026
- 100% renewable energy powered
- Estimated Norwegian investment: **NOK 15B+** (~NOK 7.5B to Norwegian suppliers)
- Operational phase: ~400 FTEs annually, NOK 1.5B annual value creation
- Microsoft also has **existing Azure data center regions** in Norway (Norway East and Norway West, launched 2019–2020)

**Sources:**
- [DCD — Microsoft signs $6bn deal to rent AI compute from Nscale](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/microsoft-signs-6bn-deal-to-rent-ai-compute-from-nscale/)

### 1.3 TikTok / ByteDance — Hamar Campus (via Green Mountain)

**Investment:** Estimated €1B+ (part of €12B European commitment under "Project Clover")
**Location:** Hamar, Norway
**Capacity:** 90 MW initial (3 × 30 MW buildings), expandable to 150 MW (5 buildings)
**Status:** All three buildings online as of April 2025

- TikTok's **largest data center in Europe**, built by Green Mountain
- Part of **Project Clover** — TikTok's EU data sovereignty initiative
- First building handed over December 2023; full campus operational 2025
- European user data now stored in Norway, Ireland, and dedicated EU facilities
- 100% renewable energy (hydropower); PUE of 1.2
- ByteDance has spent ~$13B on European data protection infrastructure
- NCC Group provides independent security oversight

**Sources:**
- [DCD — TikTok migrates European data to Norwegian data center](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/tiktok-migrates-european-data-to-norwegian-data-center/)
- [TikTok Newsroom — Project Clover Update](https://newsroom.tiktok.com/en-eu/project-clover-update-data-migration-to-tiktoks-norwegian-data-centre-has-begun)

### 1.4 Other Hyperscalers

**Meta/Facebook:** No Norwegian DC. Operates in Luleå (Sweden) and Odense (Denmark).

**Apple:** Chose Denmark (Viborg) over Norway. Key factor: lower Danish energy tax (0.5 øre/kWh vs. 12.39 øre/kWh in Norway at the time). However, Apple's Danish DC connects to Norwegian hydropower via 1.5 GW HVDC line at Tjele.

**Amazon/AWS:** No significant Norwegian DC presence identified. AWS operates Nordic regions in Stockholm (Sweden) and has edge locations but no announced Norwegian data center builds.

### 2.6 Kolos / HIVE Digital — Ballangen (Cautionary Tale)

**A failed mega-project turned crypto mining site.**

- **2017:** Kolos planned world's largest DC in Ballangen (Arctic Circle) — 1,000 MW, $500M project
- Raised "several million" from undisclosed Norwegian private investors; working with Headwaters MB (US investment bank) for additional funding
- **2018:** Failed to secure full funding; acquired by **HIVE Blockchain (Canada)** for just **$9.9M**
- HIVE pivoted to crypto mining; planned 120 MW capacity
- **2024–2025:** HIVE shifted operations to Sweden, Canada, and Paraguay; Norway no longer listed as active region
- Norway's increased electricity taxes for DCs in 2024 may have contributed to HIVE's exit

**Lesson:** Speculative mega-projects without pre-committed tenants struggle to attract capital. Grid reservations without confirmed offtake are high-risk.

## 3. Domestic Norwegian Investors

### 3.1 Norwegian Sovereign Wealth Fund (NBIM / Oljefondet)

**Status: NOT investing in data centers.**

Norway's $2.1 trillion Government Pension Fund Global (managed by NBIM) has **explicitly stated** that data centers will not form a major part of its next investment strategy. NBIM's head of real estate has "no active plans to invest there," citing high volatility and technology risk.

However, NBIM does hold public equities in DC-related companies (Equinix, Digital Realty, etc.) through its passive index-tracking strategy. The fund made $247 billion in 2025, driven by tech holdings.

**Sources:**
- [Bloomberg — Norway's $2.1 Trillion Wealth Fund Shuns Data Centers](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-10/norway-s-2-1-trillion-wealth-fund-keeps-away-from-data-centers)

### 3.2 Norwegian Utilities as DC Investors/Partners

**Statkraft (State-owned, Europe's largest renewable energy producer):**
- Active in **power supply** to data centers, not as a DC equity investor
- Operates dedicated [Data Center Solutions](https://www.statkraft.com/what-we-offer/data-center-solutions/) business offering PPAs and site support
- Offers data center sites on its properties; entered cooperation agreements to attract DCs to Norway (since 2019)
- Strategic role as **power enabler** rather than equity participant

**Hafslund (100% owned by Oslo municipality):**
- Direct equity investor via **Skygard JV** (31.7% stake; see Section 2.5)
- Partnership with **Hafslund Oslo Celsio** for waste heat reuse from DCs into district heating
- Positioned as the Oslo region's key utility-DC partner

**Other regional utilities:**
- Research did not find specific DC equity investments from Lyse, BKK, Agder Energi, or other regional power companies, though they may be providing power supply agreements

**John Fredriksen / Geveran Trading:**

### 3.4 Norwegian Government & State Entities

- Goal: attract DC investments while creating local value and positive synergies

**Investinor / Innovation Norway / Siva:**
- State investment and innovation bodies
- No specific direct DC investments found, but they provide the **ecosystem support** (venture capital, grant programs, business attraction)
- Investinor has NOK 8B total assets, focuses on venture/growth; merged with Innovation Norway (2020)
- **Business Norway** (Invest in Norway) actively promotes Norway's DC proposition internationally

## 5. Investment Thesis: Why Norway?

### 5.1 Energy Advantages

- **Hydropower dominance:** ~88% of Norway's energy mix is renewable hydropower (2024). Annual production ~137–149 TWh.
- **Power pricing:** Among the lowest wholesale electricity prices in Europe. Northern Norway (NO4) particularly attractive for cost-sensitive workloads.
- **PPA availability:** Statkraft and other producers offer long-term Power Purchase Agreements providing cost certainty
- **Grid reservations:** 3.5 GW reserved for DCs + 5.4 GW in queue (total demand ~45 TWh, ~30x current DC consumption)
- **Reservoir storage:** Norway holds ~half of Europe's total hydropower reservoir storage — provides flexible, reliable baseload

### 5.2 Climate & Efficiency

- Average summer temperatures 20–25°C, winter -4°C — enables **year-round free cooling**
- PUE ratings of 1.2 or better achievable (vs. 1.4–1.6 in Southern Europe)
- Reduced cooling CAPEX and OPEX directly improves investor returns
- Seawater and fjord water cooling (Green Mountain DC1 uses 8°C fjord water)

### 5.3 Political & Regulatory Environment

- World's **first national data centre strategy** (2018, updated 2021, new version expected 2025)
- **Reduced electricity tax** for data centers as an incentive
- Stable, transparent regulatory environment; NATO member; GDPR-compliant
- **Risks:** Growing political debate about DC power consumption vs. other industrial needs; 2024 electricity tax increase for DCs; NVE concession process can be slow (5–10 year waits for high-voltage grid connections); security clearance requirements for some locations

### 5.4 Connectivity

- Submarine fiber cables connecting to UK, Continental Europe, and North America
- Low latency to major European markets
- Regional fiber networks (Bulk Infrastructure is a major fiber operator)

### 5.5 Market Sizing

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Norway DC market value (2025) | USD 1.15–1.65 billion |
| Projected (2030) | USD 2.08–2.97 billion |
| CAGR | 10–13% |
| Nordic DC market value (2024) | USD 7.16 billion |
| Nordic projected (2030) | USD 14.9 billion (CAGR 12.8%) |
| Nordic DC construction market (2024) | USD 2.21 billion |

| Registered DCs in Norway (Mar 2026) | 103 (up from 58 in Jul 2025) |
| Grid reservations for DCs | 3.5 GW reserved + 5.4 GW in queue |

### 5.6 Risk Factors

1. **Grid connection delays:** 5–10 year wait for high-voltage feeds; gap between reservation and energized connection is the biggest risk discount
2. **Political risk:** Growing opposition to DC power consumption; potential for more restrictive policies
3. **Energy tax changes:** 2024 increase in DC electricity tax; Apple cited Norwegian tax as reason for choosing Denmark
4. **Labor market:** Limited pool of skilled DC construction and operations workers in Norway
5. **Currency risk:** NOK volatility for international investors
6. **Competition:** Sweden (Luleå, Strängnäs), Finland (Helsinki region, TikTok), Denmark, Iceland all competing aggressively for DC investment
7. **Speculative demand risk:** Many grid reservations may not convert to built capacity

## 6. Deal Structures & Investment Vehicles

### 6.1 Typical SPV/Holding Structures

Data center investments in Norway typically use multi-layered SPV structures:

- **TopCo/HoldCo** → overarching ownership entity
- **BidCo** → acquisition vehicles
- **FinCo** → debt management entities
- **PropCo** → physical property ownership
- **JV SPVs** → partnership vehicles (e.g., Skygard)

Norwegian **AS** (aksjeselskap / private limited company) is the standard vehicle. Cross-border structures often use Luxembourg, Netherlands, or UK holding entities for tax efficiency.

### 6.2 Fund Vehicle Types Active in Norway

| Type | Examples | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| Core/core-plus infra funds | 3i Infrastructure, Columbia Threadneedle ESIF | Long-term stable yield; operational DCs |
| Value-add PE | IPI Partners (ICONIQ/Iron Point) | Platform build and expansion |
| Real estate PE | BentallGreenOak (Sun Life) | DC as real estate play; growth equity |
| Digital infrastructure PE | DigitalBridge (broader STACK platform) | Hyperscale-focused platform aggregation |
| Energy PE | HitecVision | Energy-to-digital transition |
| Family offices | Smedvig (sold), Fredriksen/Geveran, Azrieli | Long-term strategic ownership |
| Pension funds | CPP Investments | Direct co-investment and platform JVs |
| Listed infra | 3i Infrastructure (LSE) | Listed vehicle acquiring DC assets |

### 6.3 Debt Instruments

- **Project finance:** Bulk's €410M senior secured facility for N01 campus
- **Corporate debt:** Green Mountain's €371M financing
- **Acquisition finance:** $4.2B package for atNorth (European + Canadian lenders)
- **Green bonds:** Trend toward sustainability-linked instruments for DC financing, but no specific Norwegian DC green bond issuance was prominently featured in research
- **Revenue model:** Long-term, availability-based contracts linked to inflation provide predictable cash flows that support leverage

### 6.4 Joint Venture Structures

JVs are increasingly common in Norwegian DC:
- **Utility + telco + PE:** Skygard (Hafslund + Telenor + HitecVision)
- **Industrial + AI compute:** Stargate Norway (Aker + Nscale, leasing to Microsoft)
- **DC operator + industrial landowner:** Green Mountain + Norske Skog (Halden)
- **Pension fund + REIT:** CPP Investments + Equinix (atNorth)
- **DC operator + power utility:** Green Mountain + KMW (Frankfurt JV)

## 7. Implications for Metier

### 7.1 Where the Capital Is Flowing

1. **Hyperscale build-to-suit** (Google Skien, Stargate Narvik, TikTok Hamar) — massive CAPEX, construction management needs
2. **Platform acquisitions and expansion** (Green Mountain, STACK, Lefdal, atNorth) — post-acquisition integration and growth capital deployment
3. **Greenfield development** (Bulk N01 expansion, Kalberg, new entrants) — project development from scratch
4. **JV formation** (Skygard, Aker/Nscale) — complex governance, multi-stakeholder projects

### 7.2 Investor-Driven Pain Points (Metier Service Opportunities)

| Investor Type | Key Pain Points | Relevant Metier Services |
|---|---|---|
| PE/Infra funds (BGO, 3i, IPI) | Milestone-gated capital; IRR pressure; rapid scaling needs | Project management, PMO, owner's engineering |
| Hyperscalers (Google, Microsoft) | Speed to market; grid connection execution; local execution risk | Construction management, procurement |
| Institutional (CPP, Azrieli) | Portfolio integration; ESG reporting; asset optimization | Project controls, ESG/sustainability advisory |
| Norwegian utilities/JVs (Skygard) | Multi-stakeholder governance; first-time DC execution | Governance frameworks, project management |
| Emerging developers (Magnora, Norske Skog) | Limited DC experience; need to de-risk for investors | Feasibility studies, project development |
