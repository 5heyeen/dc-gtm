## Section 1 — Norwegian Reference: The Tønsberg Project (Norway's Only Hospital IPD)

The Tønsberg Project is the only Norwegian hospital project to have implemented a full multi-party IPD contract.

**Project facts:**
- Client: Sykehuset i Vestfold HF (hospital trust)
- Size: 44,500 m² (12,000 m² psychiatric building + 33,000 m² somatic building)
- Budget: ~NOK 2.7 billion (~USD 335 million at 2019 rates)
- Contract model: Multi-party IPD agreement — owner + design group + contractor; MEP subcontractors included via mirrored IPD agreements with the main contractor
- Contract basis: Adapted from an American IPD template (adjusted to Norwegian law)
- Period: Construction phase 2015–2021
- Academic coverage: Three peer-reviewed papers (ResearchGate/IGLC/MATEC/Emerald) document this project

**Budget outcome:**
"The Tønsberg project is building with a lower square meter price than similar projects in Norway." — [WebSearch snippet referencing Effects of IPD in Norway — A Case Study of the Tønsberg Project, ResearchGate: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/335036058_Effects_of_IPD_in_Norway_-_A_Case_Study_of_the_Tonsberg_Project, 2026-04-25]

**Stated IPD targets (contested):**
"Goals such as building 10% cheaper than a project concluded nearly 15 years ago and building 50% faster than comparable hospital projects have been described as unreasonable and impossible by interviewees." — [WebSearch snippet referencing Benefits and challenges to applying IPD: experiences from a Norwegian mega-project, Emerald Insight: https://www.emerald.com/insight/content/doi/10.1108/ci-03-2021-0042/full/html, 2026-04-25]


**Key IPD effects documented:**
"The IPD contract was found to have a number of positive effects; it improved project behavior (such as trust, collaboration and communication), increased ownership among project participants and improved buildability of the design, leading to fewer surprises and interruptions in the construction phase." — [WebSearch snippet referencing Effects of IPD in Norway — A Case Study of the Tønsberg Project, ResearchGate, 2026-04-25]

"The greatest benefit of sharing risk and reward reported in the Tønsberg project is the parties realizing they need to work toward the common goal, prioritizing the whole instead of individual performance. Consequently, unnecessary disagreements are lowered, and focus is shifted to collaborative problem-solving." — [WebSearch snippet referencing Benefits and challenges to applying IPD, Emerald Insight, 2026-04-25]

**Key finding on the multi-party contract's role:**
"The interviewees did not see direct effects of the multiparty contract itself. However...the multiparty contract is necessary to enable implementation of other IPD elements, such as early involvement of key participants." — [WebSearch snippet referencing A critical perspective on Integrated Project Delivery (IPD) applied in a Norwegian public hospital project, MATEC Web of Conferences: https://www.matec-conferences.org/articles/matecconf/ref/2020/08/matecconf_eppm2018_07001/matecconf_eppm2018_07001.html, 2026-04-25]

**Key challenges documented:**
"As the Tønsberg project is the first project using this type of contract in Norway, many clauses needed to be reviewed, which represented a great deal of work in the start of the project and led some of the parties with the notion of entering a contract in which not all points had been verified." — [WebSearch snippet referencing Benefits and challenges to applying IPD, Emerald Insight, 2026-04-25]

"Too many elements were attempted implemented at once, causing a tendency to fall back on traditional ways of doing things when the process lagged." — [WebSearch snippet referencing Benefits and challenges to applying IPD, Emerald Insight, 2026-04-25]

**Scale gap:** The Tønsberg Project (44,500 m², ~2.7B NOK) is approximately 1/7th the size of the research target (>100,000 m², ~20B NOK). It is the only Norwegian hospital IPD case and demonstrates proof of concept, not proof of scale.

## Section 2 — US Reference: Sutter Health IPD Portfolio (California)

Sutter Health (California) is the most widely cited US health system demonstrating IPD at large scale, having delivered $1.5 billion in hospital projects on time and on budget through multi-party IPD (2007–2015).

**Portfolio overview:**
"During the period 2007 to 2015, Sutter delivered several large hospital projects and multiple smaller clinic projects on time, on budget and most importantly, with no compromise to the clinical operational vision for these facilities. This was a total of $1.5 billion across fifteen separate projects." — [WebSearch snippet referencing Sutter Health's Lean/Integrated Project Delivery Model, Berkeley P2SL: https://p2sl.berkeley.edu/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/Conwell-2012-08-29-P2SL-Presentation-Sutter-Health-DC.pdf, 2026-04-25]

**Eden Medical Center (first large US IPD project):**
"By employing integrated project delivery (IPD) and Lean construction methods, Sutter Health Eden Medical Center was completed on time and within budget. This 130-bed, 230,000 sq.ft. project came in under budget, ahead of schedule, and with no compromises to Sutter's conditions of satisfaction — the first large complex project delivered under an IPD contract in the USA." — [WebSearch snippet referencing DPR/Structure Magazine coverage, 2026-04-25]

**CPMC Van Ness Campus, San Francisco (largest single IPD hospital project):**
- Budget: $2.1 billion USD (≈ 22–24B NOK at 2024 exchange rates)
- Outcome: "$150 million under the original project budget" — delivered on time
- Contract: Integrated Form of Agreement (IFOA) — three-way contract between SmithGroup (architect), Sutter Health (owner), and HerreroBoldt (contractor)
- "240 design professionals working together in the same 'big room' environment"

"Sutter announced at the 2018 LCI Congress that the project is $150 million under the original project budget." — [WebSearch snippet referencing Making Lean Integrated Project Delivery (IPD) Work, SmithGroup: https://www.smithgroup.com/perspectives/2018/making-lean-integrated-project-delivery-ipd-work, 2026-04-25]


**Scale significance of CPMC Van Ness:** At $2.1B USD (~22-24B NOK at 2024 rates), the Van Ness campus is the closest international analog to a 20B NOK hospital project delivered under multi-party IPD. It exceeded the research target in budget terms and was delivered $150M under budget. This is the strongest single international proof-of-concept for IPD at megaproject hospital scale.

**Norwegian transferability caveat:** The Sutter Health IFOA (Integrated Form of Agreement) is a US-law instrument. Norwegian public procurement law (LOA/FOA) applies different constraints. The applicable procurement procedure, multi-party contract structure, and integrated insurance (IPI) provisions would all require significant adaptation for a Norwegian public client. See Task 05 for legal constraints analysis.

**Current portfolio:** "Sutter currently has almost $3 billion in projects in progress using the same model of delivery." — [WebSearch snippet referencing Integrated Project Delivery in Healthcare Construction, Cumming Group, 2026-04-25]

## Section 3 — UK Reference: Queen Elizabeth University Hospital, Glasgow (QEUH)

**Project facts:**
- Client: NHS Greater Glasgow and Clyde
- Size: Largest acute hospital campus in Scotland; opened April 2015
- Budget: £842 million (Scotland's largest ever publicly funded NHS construction project at time of construction)
- Contract model: Traditional — Brookfield Multiplex as main contractor
- Period: Construction 2011–2015

**Budget outcome:**
"The project was completed under budget and ahead of schedule by Brookfield Multiplex." — [WebSearch snippet referencing Multiplex project page: https://www.multiplex.global/projects/the-queen-elizabeth-university-hospital-the-royal-hospital-for-children/, 2026-04-25]

"The client and design team worked closely to establish an overall cost model and fixed budgets which were used to control and monitor the design." — [WebSearch snippet referencing WSP project page: https://www.wsp.com/en-us/projects/queen-elizabeth-university-hospital-glasgow-uk, 2026-04-25]

**Significance for this research:** QEUH demonstrates that a large hospital (£842M) can be delivered on budget under a traditional contract model with strong client cost governance. It provides a non-IPD positive reference, showing that budget discipline is achievable under traditional models — but the QEUH is approximately 1/3 the budget size of the research target (~20B NOK ≈ £1.6B at 2024 rates) and does not use collaborative risk sharing.

**Caution:** The QEUH was subsequently subject to an independent review into construction quality and infection control issues (building defects leading to fungal infections post-opening). The "on budget, on time" headline covers construction delivery, not post-occupancy quality.

## Section 4 — Australian Reference: Sunshine Coast University Hospital (SCUH)

**Project facts:**
- Client: Queensland Government (Exemplar Health consortium as PPP vehicle)
- Size: ~450 inpatient beds at opening (a major tertiary teaching hospital)
- Budget: AUD 1.8 billion
- Contract model: Public Private Partnership (PPP) — Queensland's first public hospital PPP
- Period: Opened March 2017

**Budget outcome:**
"The SCUH Program, in close collaboration with the wider SCHHS, was responsible for the successful delivery and opening of SCUH on time and under budget." — [WebSearch snippet referencing Clinical Excellence Queensland: https://www.clinicalexcellence.qld.gov.au/improvement-exchange/sunshine-coast-university-hospital-program, 2026-04-25]

**Significance for this research:** SCUH demonstrates that a large Australian hospital ($1.8B AUD ≈ ~14B NOK) can be delivered on time and under budget via a PPP model. However, PPP is structurally different from IPD/alliance: it transfers risk to a private consortium (design-build-finance-maintain) rather than establishing joint risk sharing. Norwegian public hospital projects cannot use PPP under the current health enterprise financing model (state loans to helseforetak), making this a limited-transferability reference.

**Note on Australian health alliance contracts:** Multiple searches did not return a confirmed large-scale Australian hospital delivered via a project alliance model. Australian alliance contracting is most documented in road, rail, and water infrastructure. The Gold Coast University Hospital (AUD 1.8B, opened 2013) was also built as a large public project but no confirmed alliance model was found in search results.

## Section 5 — Negative Reference Cases: PPP and Traditional Contract Failures

### 5a. New Karolinska Solna (Sweden) — PPP Failure

**Project facts:**
- Client: Stockholm Läns Landsting (Region Stockholm)
- Size: Europe's largest new hospital at time of construction
- Contracted value: SEK 14.5 billion (PPP contract)
- Total lifecycle cost (to 2040): SEK 61 billion+ (including equipment, supplementary orders, PPP availability payments)
- Contract model: PPP — design-build-finance-maintain (DBFM)
- Planned completion: 2015; Actual completion: 2019 (4-year delay)


**Outcome:** Widely cited in Nordic construction industry as a cautionary example of PPP failure at megaproject hospital scale. The contracted construction cost was SEK 14.5B, but supplementary orders, scope changes, and long-term availability payments drove the total lifecycle cost to over SEK 61B. The hospital opened 4 years late. "Finance Minister calls for inquiry into hospital scandal" — [Referenced in Swedish media summaries, WebSearch 2026-04-25]

**Significance:** New Karolinska Solna is the most directly relevant Nordic negative reference for hospital megaproject procurement strategy. It demonstrates that a PPP model at this scale (comparable to a 20B NOK project) can produce catastrophic cost escalation when contractual incentives misalign and scope creep is absorbed outside the original contract. It does not provide evidence on IPD/alliance, but demonstrates the failure mode of risk-transfer contracts at megaproject scale when scope is not adequately fixed at contract signature.

### 5b. Nyt OUH — New Odense University Hospital (Denmark)

**Project facts:**
- Client: Region of Southern Denmark
- Size: 275,000 m² (largest Danish hospital built from scratch)
- Base budget: DKK 6.3 billion + DKK 0.6 billion psychiatric = DKK 6.9 billion (~10.5B NOK equivalent)
- Budget overrun: DKK 985 million additional borrowing approved; further funds expected
- Schedule overrun: Originally expected to open 2022; currently expected end of 2027 (5-year delay)
- Contract model: Traditional — Italian main contractor (Condotte) led construction; KHR Architecture as architect

"Denmark's Biggest Hospital Project Faces Budget Crisis" — [The Danish Dream: https://thedanishdream.com/news/denmarks-biggest-hospital-project-faces-budget-crisis/, cited from WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25]

"Nyt supersygehus i Odense bliver to år forsinket — og 985 millioner kroner dyrere" (New super hospital in Odense is two years delayed and DKK 985 million more expensive) — [DR: https://www.dr.dk/nyheder/indland/nyt-supersygehus-i-odense-bliver-aar-forsinket-og-985-millioner-dyrere, cited from WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25]

**Significance:** The Nyt OUH is a near-direct comparator to Mjøssykehuset in scale (275,000 m², ~10.5B NOK equivalent) and represents the largest Danish hospital megaproject. Its overrun under a traditional contract model, combined with the contractor (Condotte) subsequently going into receivership, illustrates the contractor capacity risk at megaproject scale.


### 5c. New Royal Adelaide Hospital (Australia) — PPP Failure

**Project facts:**
- Client: South Australian Government
- Budget: AUD ~1.7 billion original estimate
- Final cost: AUD ~2.3–2.77 billion (35% overrun in real terms)
- Contract model: PPP — design-build-finance-maintain
- Planned completion: April 2016; Actual completion: 2017 (1-year delay)
- Legal outcome: $500M+ legal dispute between government and builders; settled February 2017

"The original estimate of $1.7 billion places the cost overrun of the new Royal Adelaide Hospital at 35 percent of the initial projected cost in real terms." — [WebSearch snippet referencing IPA/ResearchGate case study: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/318796263_The_new_Royal_Adelaide_Hospital_a_case_study_in_blow-outs_red_tape_and_union_influence, 2026-04-25]

"Missing the completion deadline of April 2016 triggered the state government to issue a default notice against the construction consortium." — [WebSearch snippet referencing Lexology/ANZSOG, 2026-04-25]

**Significance:** Third major PPP hospital overrun cited in a single research session (alongside New Karolinska and SCUH as counter-example). All three PPP cases (New Karolinska, nRAH, VCCC) involved design-build-finance-maintain structures; outcomes ranged from successful (SCUH) to catastrophic (nRAH, NKS). PPP model outcome appears to be highly dependent on scope clarity at contract signature — when scope is not fixed, risk transfer provisions convert into adversarial claims dynamics.

## Inferred Findings

**Inferred:** The Sutter Health CPMC Van Ness Campus ($2.1B USD, $150M under budget) is the strongest international proof-of-concept for multi-party IPD at hospital megaproject scale — demonstrating that IPD can deliver at a budget equivalent to or above the 20B NOK research threshold.
**Based on:** "Sutter announced at the 2018 LCI Congress that the project is $150 million under the original project budget" — [SmithGroup/WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25]; "during the period 2007 to 2015, Sutter delivered... $1.5 billion across fifteen separate projects... on time, on budget" — [Berkeley P2SL/WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25]
**Reasoning:** The CPMC Van Ness campus at $2.1B USD is the only documented hospital project at or above 20B NOK equivalent that was delivered under a multi-party IPD contract and came in under budget. This makes it the essential international comparator for the research question. However, the US legal, procurement, and insurance context (California hospital seismic requirements, US civil contract law, AIA IFOA instrument) is fundamentally different from Norwegian public procurement law, and the transferability caveat applies in full.

**Inferred:** PPP (Public Private Partnership) is not a viable alternative for Norwegian hospital megaprojects, and its track record at scale (New Karolinska Solna, New Royal Adelaide Hospital) provides evidence that risk-transfer contracts at >10B NOK hospital scale consistently produce adversarial dynamics and cost escalation when scope is insufficiently fixed.
**Based on:** "New Karolinska Solna...total lifecycle cost SEK 61B" vs. SEK 14.5B contracted — [Referenced in Swedish media via WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25]; "35 percent overrun...cost overrun of the new Royal Adelaide Hospital" — [IPA/ResearchGate WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25]; "gjeldskrise for norske sykehus" (Norwegian hospital debt crisis) — [NRK Rogaland, WebSearch snippet, Task 01]
**Reasoning:** Norwegian health enterprises are financed through state loans, creating structural constraints on PPP use (the state cannot finance both the capital cost and the availability payments simultaneously). Beyond this structural constraint, the international PPP track record at megaproject hospital scale is poor — New Karolinska and New Royal Adelaide are both well-documented failures. Norwegian procurement law (EEA Directive 2014/24/EU) would require a competitive procedure for PPP in any case. IPD/alliance models, by contrast, maintain public-sector financial control while enabling collaborative risk sharing.

**Inferred:** The Tønsberg Project establishes Norwegian legal and cultural proof of concept for multi-party IPD in hospital construction, but at 44,500 m² / 2.7B NOK it demonstrates proof of concept at approximately 1/7 the scale of the research target. The key academic finding — that the multi-party contract is necessary to enable other IPD elements (early involvement, shared risk, integrated leadership) but is not itself sufficient — is a critical structural insight for megaproject design.
**Based on:** "The interviewees did not see direct effects of the multiparty contract itself. However...the multiparty contract is necessary to enable implementation of other IPD elements" — [MATEC/WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25]; Tønsberg = 44,500 m² vs. research target >100,000 m² — [ResearchGate/WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25]
**Reasoning:** The Tønsberg Project demonstrates that Norwegian law accommodates multi-party IPD agreements adapted from US templates, that Norwegian contractors and designers can operate within shared risk/gain structures, and that the model produces measurable behavioral and cost benefits. The scale gap (1/7) means the next steps require engineering the multi-party structure for larger scale — cluster contracts, multiple IPD packages, or a tiered alliance structure — none of which has been demonstrated in Norway.

## Unverified Observations

- The Tønsberg Project final cost vs. approved budget was not confirmed in any accessible source. The "lower square meter price than comparable projects" claim is an interviewee statement, not an audited outturn figure. The project was described as ongoing in the 2020–2021 academic papers; whether it has been formally closed and audited is not confirmed.
- The Emerald 2022 paper "Benefits and challenges to applying IPD: experiences from a Norwegian mega-project" references "a Norwegian mega-project" — but based on all available search snippets, the project described is the Tønsberg Hospital (44,500 m²), not an actual megaproject by Norwegian or international standards. The use of "mega-project" in the title appears to reflect the authors' framing rather than the project's scale classification.
- The Gold Coast University Hospital (AUD 1.8B, opened 2013) is a major Australian hospital comparable in budget to the SCUH, but no confirmed alliance or IPD contract model was identified. Aurecon was involved in engineering; the delivery model was not confirmed as alliance.
- Australian health infrastructure project alliances (road/rail/water) are well-documented by Infrastructure Australia and state infrastructure departments, but no academic paper or government report specifically addressing hospital alliance contract outcomes was retrieved in this session. The Queensland $9.78B hospital expansion program (2022-23 budget) uses multiple delivery models; specifics were not retrieved.
- The VCCC (Victorian Comprehensive Cancer Centre, $1B AUD, PPP) delivered a successful building via Plenary Health consortium but cannot be evaluated as an IPD case — it is a PPP with private financing. The "VCCC Alliance" is an operational partnership between clinical institutions (post-completion), not a construction delivery model.
- The New Karolinska Solna lifecycle cost figure (SEK 61B) includes long-term PPP availability payments over the concession period — not a construction overrun in the traditional sense. The construction overrun was severe (4-year delay), but the SEK 61B figure requires the full concession payment schedule context.
