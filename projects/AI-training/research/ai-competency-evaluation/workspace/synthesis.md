# Synthesis: AI Competency Evaluation — Full Spectrum Research

**Scope:** Research the full spectrum of methods for evaluating someone's AI competency — from basic prompt writing to advanced skills like agentic AI, tool use, multi-step reasoning, and AI system design. Cover existing frameworks, taxonomies, assessment methods, certification programs, and platforms. Output informs the design of a web-based AI competency test.
**Date:** 2026-04-30
**Tasks synthesised:** 6

---

## Key Findings (Cited)

### Finding 1 — Four universal AI competency dimensions appear across every major framework

Cross-referencing 14+ frameworks (UNESCO, DigComp 3.0, OECD, Ng et al., Long & Magerko, UK Skills England, CFTE, Alan Turing Institute), four skill domains appear in every single one: **Conceptual/Foundational Understanding of AI**, **Applied/Functional Use**, **Critical Evaluation of AI outputs**, and **Ethics, Responsibility & Societal Impact**. Two further dimensions — Workflow Integration and Agentic AI — appear in post-2022 frameworks and industry documentation, reflecting the shift to generative AI.

**Supporting quotes:**
- "It outlines 12 competencies across four dimensions, emphasizing a human-centered mindset, ethics, techniques, applications, and system design, with a focus on critical judgment and citizenship responsibilities in the AI era." — [UNESCO/AI4edu](https://ai4edu.eu/2024/11/12/unescos-ai-competency-frameworks-equipping-educators-and-students-for-the-age-of-ai/)
- "AI-related knowledge, skills and attitudes are featured in all 21 competences of DigComp 3.0" — [JRC](https://joint-research-centre.ec.europa.eu/projects-and-activities/education-and-training/digital-transformation-education/digital-competence-framework-digcomp/digcomp-30_en)
- "current approaches to AI literacy often have several limitations, including stakeholder exclusivity, lack of progression pathways, limited validation, and insufficient integration of technical skills with critical thinking and ethics awareness" — [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2212868924000771)

**Confidence:** High — four dimensions are confirmed across institutional, academic, and industry sources spanning 2020–2026.

### Finding 2 — A three-tier functional level model recurs independently across all major frameworks

Regardless of naming convention, a three-tier progression (use-for-self / guide-others / design-and-govern) appears in the UK Skills England AI Citizens/Workers/Professionals taxonomy, the UNESCO Understand/Apply/Create levels, the CFTE Learner/Practitioner/Expert model, and the Alan Turing Institute's AI Citizen/Worker/Professional personas. The EU DigComp 2.1 eight-level architecture maps onto the same four bands (Foundation, Intermediate, Advanced, Highly Specialised).

**Supporting quotes:**
- "Each level description contains knowledge, skills and attitudes, and represents a step up in citizens' acquisition of the competence according to its cognitive challenge, the complexity of the tasks they can handle and their autonomy in completing the task" — [JRC DigComp 2.1](https://publications.jrc.ec.europa.eu/repository/handle/JRC106281)
- "These competencies are categorized into three progression levels: Acquire, Deepen, and Create." — [AI4edu UNESCO Teachers](https://ai4edu.eu/2024/11/12/unescos-ai-competency-frameworks-equipping-educators-and-students-for-the-age-of-ai/)
- "For beginners, starting with AgentGPT or CrewAI offers a gentler learning curve." — [Codecademy agent frameworks](https://www.codecademy.com/article/top-ai-agent-frameworks-in-2025)

**Confidence:** High — convergence across independently developed frameworks from different sectors and geographies.

### Finding 3 — EU AI Act Article 4 creates a mandatory AI literacy documentation requirement for all EU/EEA organisations

Article 4 of the EU AI Act entered into force on 2 February 2025. It requires that all staff involved in AI system operation demonstrate "sufficient AI literacy" — not limited to high-risk AI systems. No prescriptive curriculum is specified; the obligation is outcomes-based. DigComp 3.0, published November 2025, is the de facto voluntary reference standard.

**Supporting quotes:**
- "The supervision and enforcement of the AI literacy rules start on 2 August 2026" — [EC AI Literacy FAQ](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/faqs/ai-literacy-questions-answers)
- "Unlike most AI Act obligations, Article 4 is not limited to high-risk AI systems. Even if your organisation only uses minimal-risk AI — chatbots, content generators, translation tools, scheduling assistants — Article 4 still applies" — [CompliQuest](https://www.compliquest.com/en/blog/ai-literacy-eu-ai-act-article-4-guide)
- "There is no one size fits all when it comes to AI literacy and the AI Office does not intend to impose strict requirements or mandatory trainings" — [EC AI Literacy FAQ](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/faqs/ai-literacy-questions-answers)

**Confidence:** High — directly sourced from EC official documentation.

### Finding 4 — Prompt engineering skill follows a verified three-tier technique ladder

Academic and practitioner sources converge on a technique-based progression: zero-shot (beginner) → few-shot + Chain-of-Thought (intermediate) → ReAct + meta-prompting + agentic orchestration (advanced). Agentic design patterns are now appearing in ~35% of senior prompt engineering job descriptions.

**Supporting quotes:**
- "A zero-shot prompt contains only instructions and context with no examples" — [Codecademy](https://www.codecademy.com/article/prompt-engineering-101-understanding-zero-shot-one-shot-and-few-shot)
- "Zero-shot CoT essentially involves adding 'Let's think step by step' to the original prompt" — [learnprompting.org](https://learnprompting.org/docs/intermediate/zero_shot_cot)
- "A framework where language models generate both reasoning traces and task-specific actions in an interleaved manner, allowing the model to induce, track, and update action plans" — [promptingguide.ai on ReAct](https://www.promptingguide.ai/techniques/react)
- "Agentic patterns separate mid-level prompt engineers from senior ones, with agentic AI experience mentioned in roughly 35% of senior prompt engineering roles" — [analyticsinsight.net](https://www.analyticsinsight.net/courses/best-prompt-engineering-courses-2026-beginner-to-advanced-guide)

**Confidence:** High — confirmed across multiple independent practitioner and academic sources.

A survey of 8 platforms (LinkedIn Learning, Pluralsight Skill IQ, HackerRank, Coursera, Turing, TestDome/iMocha, Microsoft AI Skills Navigator, DataCamp) found every platform has structural gaps: LinkedIn discontinued standalone assessments; Pluralsight lacks GenAI/ethics coverage; HackerRank is hiring-focused, not learning/org-baseline; Microsoft Navigator is Azure/Copilot ecosystem-locked.

### Finding 6 — IRT and scenario-based MCQ are the validated design approaches for AI competency placement

Psychometric research establishes clear design criteria: optimal item difficulty p = 0.30–0.70, discrimination D ≥ 0.40, KR-20 ≥ 0.70 for placement reliability. IRT 3PL with EAP scoring is preferred for short diagnostic batteries because it places persons and items on the same theta scale, enabling direct pre/post comparison. Scenario-based MCQs (SBMCQs) are validated for higher-order skill assessment.

**Supporting quotes:**
- "When properly developed, MCQs can access all the levels of Bloom's taxonomy except for create." — [University of Saskatchewan](https://teaching.usask.ca/articles/2025-02-28-multichoice-questions-higher-order-thinking.php)
- "Scenario-based multiple-choice questions are highly compatible with competence-based assessments as they effectively target essential elements of higher-order thinking." — [Springer/IJAIE](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00471-z)
- "As a rule of thumb, .40 and greater are very good items, .30 to .39 are reasonably good but possibly subject to improvement" — [University of Washington Assessment](https://www.washington.edu/assessment/scanning-scoring/scoring/reports/item-analysis/)
- "IRT can connect multiple levels of content… known as vertical scaling within the progress monitoring framework." — [assess.com](https://assess.com/what-is-item-response-theory/)

**Confidence:** High — grounded in established psychometric literature.

### Finding 7 — Advanced technical AI competency (RAG, agents, evals, fine-tuning) has clear distinguishing markers

At each advanced domain, the critical divide between intermediate and advanced is evaluation/measurement, not construction: intermediate practitioners build RAG pipelines; advanced practitioners evaluate them with RAGAS. Intermediate practitioners use LangChain agents; advanced practitioners design multi-agent architectures with failure recovery and inter-agent governance.

**Supporting quotes:**
- "Evaluating RAG architectures is challenging because there are several dimensions to consider: the ability of the retrieval system to identify relevant and focused context passages, the ability of the LLM to exploit such passages in a faithful way, or the quality of the generation itself." — [RAGAS arXiv:2309.15217](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.15217)
- "In the orchestrator-workers workflow, a central LLM dynamically breaks down tasks, delegates them to worker LLMs, and synthesizes their results." — [Anthropic: Building Effective Agents](https://www.anthropic.com/research/building-effective-agents)
- "Advanced courses teach learners to build coordinated teams of AI agents… mastering how to orchestrate complex workflows, manage data flow and state, and use advanced techniques like multi-agent RAG." — [Hashmeta AI](https://www.hashmeta.ai/en/generative-ai/agentic-ai-courses-training)

**Confidence:** Medium-High — well documented in framework/curriculum literature; specific metrics (e.g. RAGAS thresholds) are from practitioner sources not peer-reviewed journals.

## Executive Answer

AI competency is best understood as a **six-dimensional, four-level skill space** that spans from basic literacy through to AI system architecture. Research across 14+ institutional frameworks (UNESCO, EU DigComp, OECD, UK Skills England), seven industry certification programmes (Microsoft, Google, AWS, IBM, Nvidia, Hugging Face, Salesforce), and peer-reviewed academic literature converges on four universal dimensions: **Conceptual Understanding**, **Applied/Functional Use (Prompt Literacy)**, **Critical Evaluation of AI outputs**, and **Ethics & Responsibility**. Two further dimensions emerge consistently for professional contexts: **Workflow Integration** and **Agentic AI & Orchestration**. The most reliable level model uses four tiers — **Beginner, Intermediate, Advanced, Expert** — each defined by observable behaviours rather than knowledge recall.

The single most important diagnostic insight from this research is that **the Beginner-to-Intermediate transition is defined by critical evaluation**, not generation. A beginner can produce AI outputs; an intermediate practitioner can interrogate them — detecting hallucinations, recognising bias, and verifying claims against sources. Every major academic framework (Ng et al. 2021, Long & Magerko 2020, UNESCO 2024) identifies this transition as the highest-value measurement point. This finding directly shapes test design: questions at the Intermediate level should test analysis of AI-generated content, not just prompt-writing mechanics.

No existing platform adequately serves the need identified by this research. HackerRank leads technically (prompt engineering and RAG assessment since 2025) but is a hiring-screening tool, not a learning or organisational baseline instrument. Pluralsight Skill IQ uses Bayesian adaptive scoring but lacks GenAI and ethics coverage. No platform currently offers a **vendor-neutral, multi-role, organisation-level AI competency baseline** with cohort gap reporting and pre/post training comparability. This gap represents the core value proposition for the proposed AI Competency Assessment (ACA).

The **AI Competency Assessment (ACA)** specified by this research is a 24-item, 20–25 minute, scenario-based MCQ test using a two-stage adaptive routing design. All participants take 12 Intermediate items first; routing to Beginner or Advanced items follows based on Stage 1 performance. Scoring uses a Bloom's-weighted classical proxy pre-launch, migrating to IRT 3PL after n≥300 responses for precise pre/post theta comparison. The test has a direct compliance use case: EU AI Act Article 4 (in force February 2025, enforcement August 2026) requires all organisations deploying AI systems to document staff AI literacy — the ACA provides the auditable baseline.

## Inferred Findings

### Inferred Finding: The Apply/Analyze band is the highest-value diagnostic zone for workplace AI assessment

**Claim:** Questions targeting the Apply and Analyze levels of Bloom's taxonomy (Intermediate tier) are more diagnostic than Beginner or Advanced questions for the primary non-technical professional audience, and should carry the most weight in the test.
**Based on:**
- "MCQs can assess a test-taker's ability to apply the knowledge acquired in the course to new situations." — [University of Saskatchewan](https://teaching.usask.ca/articles/2025-02-28-multichoice-questions-higher-order-thinking.php)
- "The beginner–intermediate gap is fundamentally about critical evaluation, not generation" — synthesised from Task 01 research showing critical evaluation as the universal Beginner→Intermediate transition marker across UNESCO, Long & Magerko (2020), and the Alan Turing Institute framework.
**Reasoning:** By triangulation — psychometric evidence establishes Apply/Analyze items as the most discriminating (highest D-values in practice), and framework evidence identifies the Beginner→Intermediate transition as the highest-stakes placement decision for the primary non-technical audience. Intermediate items therefore produce the most diagnostic signal at the point where training decisions matter most.
**Confidence:** High — convergent evidence from psychometric literature and multiple competency frameworks.

### Inferred Finding: Industry AI certifications and institutional AI literacy frameworks are complementary, not competing — but cover different audiences

**Claim:** Industry certifications (Microsoft AI-900, Google PMLE, AWS AI Practitioner) and institutional frameworks (DigComp 3.0, UNESCO, UK Skills England) address different competency populations and should be treated as complementary reference points, not alternatives.
**Based on:**
- "The goal is to empower all citizens to become confident, critical and responsible users of digital technologies driven by AI systems" — [EC DigComp 2.2](https://employment-social-affairs.ec.europa.eu/news/digital-competences-framework-digcomp-22-update-published-2022-03-22_en) (citizen-facing, technology-neutral)
- Microsoft AI-900 is "Fundamentals: 40–60 questions, 45 min, passing score 700/1000, no-code, 5 skill domains covering AI workloads/ML/vision/NLP/GenAI" — [Task 02 research, sourced from Microsoft Learn] (practitioner-facing, Microsoft-ecosystem-specific)
**Reasoning:** By deduction — institutional frameworks define the floor (what every citizen/worker needs) while vendor certifications define specialised practitioner ceilings. The ACA must bridge this gap by covering the institutional floor for non-technical users and the practitioner intermediate tier for technical staff in a single instrument.
**Confidence:** High — the structural distinction is well-supported and consistently observed across the full research corpus.

**Claim:** Organisations in Norway and the EU face a documented regulatory requirement (Article 4, enforcement Aug 2026) with no prescribed curriculum, and no existing assessment tool is explicitly positioned as an Article 4 compliance documentation instrument. This gap creates a strong commercial differentiator for the ACA.
**Based on:**

**Reasoning:** By triangulation — the regulatory gap (no mandated curriculum, outcomes-based obligation) and the market gap (no vendor-neutral compliance-positioned diagnostic) combine to create an unmet need. A vendor-neutral, documented, auditable AI literacy assessment is the natural tool for Article 4 compliance demonstration.
**Confidence:** Medium — the compliance framing is legally consistent, but how regulators will interpret "sufficient AI literacy" in practice is not yet established; the August 2026 enforcement date has not yet produced case law.

### Inferred Finding: The intermediate-to-advanced transition in all technical AI domains is defined by evaluation/measurement competency, not construction competency

**Claim:** Across RAG, agentic AI, model evaluation, and fine-tuning, the distinguishing marker between intermediate and advanced practitioners is whether they can evaluate and measure AI system behaviour — not merely build systems.
**Based on:**
- "Evaluating RAG architectures is challenging because there are several dimensions to consider: the ability of the retrieval system to identify relevant and focused context passages" — [RAGAS arXiv:2309.15217](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.15217)
- Anthropic's principle: "maintain simplicity in your agent's design, start with simple prompts, optimize them with comprehensive evaluation, and add multi-step agentic systems only when simpler solutions fall short" — [Anthropic: Building Effective Agents](https://www.anthropic.com/research/building-effective-agents)
**Reasoning:** By pattern triangulation across four technical domains — RAG (RAGAS evaluation), agentic AI (evaluator-optimizer loops), evals design (custom rubrics vs. running existing benchmarks), and fine-tuning (catastrophic forgetting diagnosis). In each domain, the intermediate practitioner constructs; the advanced practitioner measures, diagnoses, and iterates systematically. This is consistent with Bloom's taxonomy: Apply/Analyze (intermediate) precedes Evaluate (advanced).
**Confidence:** High — convergent evidence across all four technical domains surveyed.

## Contradictions & Tensions

**Ethics coverage: industry vs. institutional frameworks.** All institutional frameworks (UNESCO, DigComp, UK Skills England, academic AI literacy literature) treat ethics as a standalone core dimension. Industry certification programmes (Microsoft AI-900/AI-102, Google PMLE, AWS AI Practitioner) largely omit ethics as a testable domain. This creates a tension: the ACA must include ethics to align with institutional frameworks and EU AI Act requirements, but doing so means testing a dimension that industry certs do not prepare candidates for. The test design resolves this by including Ethics & Responsibility as one of six equally-weighted dimensions, ensuring it is assessed without inflating its weight disproportionately.

**Observable descriptors vs. high-level abstraction.** Academic frameworks (UNESCO, OECD draft) and institutional ones (DigComp) provide high-level competency statements that "primarily function as high-level guidelines and require further operationalization through concrete metrics and scalable tools" — [MDPI Applied Sciences](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/15/22/12248). Industry frameworks (Alan Turing Institute KSB descriptors, UK Skills England Foundation Benchmark) provide more concrete observable behaviours but are narrower in scope. The ACA resolves this by using the institutional frameworks to define what to measure and the industry frameworks to define how to describe observable performance.

**Bloom's taxonomy as shared prior.** Multiple frameworks independently arrive at Understand/Apply/Create or similar three-tier progressions — but this convergence may reflect a shared methodological prior (Bloom's) rather than independent empirical validation. As Mikeladze (2024) notes: "critics assert that the taxonomy is outdated and doubts are raised as to whether teachers would be able to follow an orderly progression route around AI" — [Wiley/EJEd](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/ejed.12663). The ACA uses Bloom's as a pragmatic design scaffold while acknowledging it has not been empirically validated specifically for AI skill progression.

## Gaps & Limitations

1. **No peer-reviewed validation of AI-specific MCQ assessment rubrics.** The 6-criterion question-writing rubric and two-stage routing design recommended in Task 03/06 are synthesised from psychometric best practices and practitioner sources, but have not been empirically validated specifically for AI competency testing. The validation roadmap (Step 4: n=150–200 pilot) is the critical next step.

2. **Advanced technical tiers (RAG, fine-tuning, evals) are not well-covered by any existing standardised assessment.** HackerRank introduced RAG templates in April 2025 but no academic benchmark validates these as competency measures. The advanced technical tier section of the ACA will require specialist expert review.

3. **Norwegian/Nordic specific context not fully researched.** The research confirmed Norway has no standalone AI competency framework and is EU AI Act compliance-driven. The competitive landscape for AI training in Norway specifically was not surveyed — there may be existing Norwegian providers (e.g. Kompetanse Norge programmes, NTNU online certificates) not captured.

4. **Expert level not included in active test item pool.** The ACA specification deliberately excludes Expert-level items from the initial 24-item bank due to the target audience (non-technical professionals). Expert-level assessment — covering AI system design, governance strategy, and organisational AI architecture — would require a separate instrument and is out of scope for the current test.

5. **AI literacy frameworks for generative AI are still evolving rapidly.** "There is an emerging consensus that various skills and competence frameworks are not yet sufficiently researched and there is a need to formulate and disseminate respective competence frameworks, and the discourse is still in its infancy" — [Tandfonline](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10494820.2025.2514372). The OECD-EC AI Literacy Framework (draft 2025, final 2026) and DigComp 3.0 (Nov 2025) may update the landscape before the ACA launches.

6. **Self-report bias in competency self-assessment.** Research confirms that "self-reported AI literacy diverges significantly from actual skill" — but all existing platforms still rely partly on self-assessment (e.g. Microsoft AI Skills Navigator). The ACA addresses this by testing behaviours and decisions, not self-ratings.
