### Prompt 1: Institutional frameworks (UNESCO, OECD, EU DigComp)
# Prompt 01: search-for-published-academic

> **Prompt:** What institutional frameworks (UNESCO, OECD, EU DigComp) define AI competency dimensions?

---

Four major institutional frameworks define AI competency or AI literacy: UNESCO's pair of frameworks for students and teachers (2024), the EU DigComp 2.2 framework (2022), the DigCompEdu supplement for educators, and the emerging OECD–EC AI Literacy Framework (draft 2025). Each is summarised below.

---

## 1. UNESCO AI Competency Frameworks (2024)

**Target audiences:** (a) school students; (b) teachers in formal education systems.

UNESCO released two companion frameworks during Digital Learning Week 2024, described as providing guidance "to guide policy-makers, educators and curriculum developers in equipping students with the necessary skills, knowledge and values to engage with AI effectively." — [UNESCO](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-students)

### (a) Skill Dimensions

**For Students — 4 dimensions, 12 competencies:**

- **Human-Centred Mindset** — positions AI in relation to human values, agency, and well-being
- **Ethics of AI** — critical evaluation of bias, fairness, privacy, and societal impact
- **AI Techniques & Applications** — understanding how AI systems work technically and how they are applied
- **AI System Design** — ability to participate in or evaluate the design and deployment of AI systems

"It outlines 12 competencies across four dimensions, emphasizing a human-centered mindset, ethics, techniques, applications, and system design, with a focus on critical judgment and citizenship responsibilities in the AI era." — [UNESCO / AI4edu summary](https://ai4edu.eu/2024/11/12/unescos-ai-competency-frameworks-equipping-educators-and-students-for-the-age-of-ai/)

**For Teachers — 5 dimensions, 15 competencies:**

- **Human-Centred Mindset**
- **Ethics of AI**
- **AI Foundations and Applications**
- **AI Pedagogy** — pedagogically sound integration of AI into teaching
- **AI for Professional Learning** — using AI for continuous professional development

"The publication outlines 15 competencies across five dimensions: Human-centred mindset, Ethics of AI, AI foundations and applications, AI pedagogy, and AI for professional learning." — [AI4edu](https://ai4edu.eu/2024/11/12/unescos-ai-competency-frameworks-equipping-educators-and-students-for-the-age-of-ai/)

### (b) Proficiency Levels

**Students:** three levels — **Understand**, **Apply**, and **Create**.

**Teachers:** three levels — **Acquire**, **Deepen**, and **Create**.

"These competencies are categorized into three progression levels: Acquire, Deepen, and Create." — [AI4edu](https://ai4edu.eu/2024/11/12/unescos-ai-competency-frameworks-equipping-educators-and-students-for-the-age-of-ai/)

---

## 2. EU DigComp 2.2 (2022)

**Target audience:** All EU citizens (general population, not education-sector specific).

DigComp 2.2, published by the European Commission's Joint Research Centre in March 2022, is "The Digital Competence Framework for Citizens." — [JRC Publications](https://publications.jrc.ec.europa.eu/repository/handle/JRC128415)

### (a) Skill Dimensions

DigComp 2.2 is organised across **5 competence areas** containing **21 competencies** (Dimension 1). The update explicitly integrates AI by adding new examples across all areas:

- **Information and Data Literacy** — locating, evaluating, and managing data, including AI-generated content
- **Communication and Collaboration** — digital interaction, netiquette, digital identity
- **Digital Content Creation** — creating, editing, and understanding copyright; includes AI content generation
- **Safety** — protecting devices, personal data, health, and the environment; includes AI-related risks
- **Problem Solving** — identifying digital needs, resolving technical problems, using AI tools creatively

"The update takes account of emerging technologies such as Artificial Intelligence, the Internet of Things and datafication … More specifically, the European Commission has updated the Digital Competence Framework (DigComp 2.2) to include skills, knowledge and attitudes related to AI and the use of data." — [EC Employment & Social Affairs](https://employment-social-affairs.ec.europa.eu/news/digital-competences-framework-digcomp-22-update-published-2022-03-22_en)

AI-specific examples: "DigComp 2.2 now includes an appendix with more than 70 examples that can help citizens to better understand where and in which situations in their everyday life they can expect to encounter AI systems." — [ICDL Global](https://icdl.org/updated-digcomp-2-2-published-by-european-commission/)

### (b) Proficiency Levels

DigComp 2.2 retains **8 proficiency levels** across four stages (Foundation → Intermediate → Advanced → Highly Specialised), structured as Dimension 3 of the framework. "The DigComp framework includes Proficiency levels (Dimension 3), Examples of knowledge, skills and attitudes (Dimension 4) and Use cases (Dimension 5)." — [EC Employment & Social Affairs](https://employment-social-affairs.ec.europa.eu/news/digital-competences-framework-digcomp-22-update-published-2022-03-22_en)

## 3. DigCompEdu + AI Supplement

**Target audience:** Educators at all levels of education (teachers, trainers, lecturers).

The core DigCompEdu framework "systematically organises 22 competencies across six domains" for educators. An AI Pioneers project published a formal supplement specifically addressing AI competency for teachers. — [AI Pioneers Supplement](https://aipioneers.org/supplement-to-the-digcompedu-framework/)

### (a) Skill Dimensions

**Six core DigCompEdu domains:**

- **Professional Engagement** — communication, collaboration, professional development via digital tools
- **Digital Resources** — selecting, creating, adapting, managing educational resources
- **Teaching and Learning** — pedagogically meaningful technology integration
- **Assessment** — using digital tools for assessment and feedback
- **Empowering Learners** — differentiation, accessibility, active participation
- **Facilitating Learners' Digital Competence** — building students' own digital skills

"DigCompEdu systematically organises 22 competencies across six domains, each featuring six levels of proficiency, offering a structured approach to evaluating and enhancing educators' digital competencies." — [AI Pioneers](https://aipioneers.org/supplement-to-the-digcompedu-framework/)

The AI supplement adds competencies in: developing data literacy, computational thinking, curriculum design leveraging AI, ethical AI use, and preparing students for an AI-driven world.

### (b) Proficiency Levels

Six levels: **Newcomer → Explorer → Integrator → Expert → Leader → Pioneer**

"DigCompEdu distinguishes six stages or levels along which educators' digital competence typically develops … Pioneers experiment with highly innovative and complex digital technologies and lead innovation as role models." — [JRC DigCompEdu proficiency levels](https://joint-research-centre.ec.europa.eu/digcompedu/digcompedu-framework/digcompedu-proficiency-levels_en)

## 4. OECD–EC AI Literacy Framework (Draft 2025)

**Target audience:** Primary and secondary school students.

The OECD and European Commission are jointly developing an AI Literacy framework. "The OECD and the European Commission are developing an AI Literacy framework that outlines the knowledge, skills and attitudes that will adequately prepare students in primary and secondary education." — [Digital Skills & Jobs Platform](https://digital-skills-jobs.europa.eu/en/inspiration/research/introducing-oecd-ai-capability-indicators)

### (a) Skill Dimensions

Four core domains with **22 specific competencies**:

- **Engage with AI** — understanding what AI is, how it works, and critically engaging with AI systems
- **Create with AI** — using AI tools to produce outputs and solve problems
- **Manage AI** — overseeing, evaluating, and controlling AI use responsibly
- **Design AI** — conceiving and shaping AI systems and applications

"The framework outlines essential knowledge, skills, and attitudes structured around four core domains: (1) Engage with AI, (2) Create with AI, (3) Manage AI, and (4) Design AI. These domains are broken into 22 specific competencies." — [UNESCO & OECD AI Frameworks comparison](https://sjtylr.net/2025/04/19/unesco-ai-competencies-framework-for-students/)

### (b) Proficiency Levels

Not yet formally defined in the draft. The OECD's separate AI Capability Indicators use a 5-level scale (1 = very limited/basic → 5 = full human capacity). — [OECD AI Capability Indicators](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/introducing-the-oecd-ai-capability-indicators_be745f04-en/full-report/component-5.html)

Note: The draft framework was released May 2025; the final version is expected in 2026.

## Inferred Findings

1. **Convergence on ethics and human-centredness as a standalone domain**: All four frameworks (UNESCO students, UNESCO teachers, OECD–EC, DigCompEdu supplement) treat ethics and human values as a named competency area rather than embedding ethics within technical skill domains. This suggests a field-wide consensus that ethical reasoning is a separable and assessable dimension of AI literacy. Based on: UNESCO student framework (Ethics of AI domain) + UNESCO teacher framework (Ethics of AI domain) + OECD framework (Engage with AI includes critical evaluation) + DigCompEdu supplement (ethical AI use competency).

2. **DigComp 2.2 is the only framework targeting the general adult population**: UNESCO targets students and teachers; DigCompEdu targets educators; OECD–EC targets K-12 students. DigComp 2.2 is explicitly for all citizens regardless of role. This makes it the primary reference for workforce and citizen-level AI literacy policy in the EU.

- DigComp 3.0 has been announced as a successor to DigComp 2.2 but its full specification and AI treatment were not confirmed in available sources at the time of research.

### Prompt 2: Industry frameworks (Microsoft, Google, Anthropic, OpenAI)

# Prompt 02: search-for-ai-competency

> **Prompt:** What skill dimensions do Microsoft, Google, Anthropic, OpenAI frameworks identify for AI competency?

---

## Microsoft: AI Skills Initiative & Certifications

Microsoft's framework is the most explicitly tiered of the four. As of February 2026 it released four new certifications spanning entry-level to expert, framing AI competency across at least three axes: **role/persona** (business professional vs. IT admin vs. architect), **technical depth** (usage vs. configuration vs. design), and **organisational scope** (individual productivity vs. enterprise transformation).

Cert-level descriptors from the certification refresh:

- **AB-900** — "entry-level IT admins" responsible for Copilot and Agent Administration Fundamentals — establishes an administrative/governance skill dimension.
- **AB-730** — validates "the ability to use generative AI and Copilot to streamline workflows" — a *task-automation / productivity* dimension aimed at business professionals.
- **AB-731** — verifies "ability to define AI's business value and drive company-wide AI adoption" — a *strategic/leadership* dimension.
- **AB-100** — expert-level "designing multi-agent AI systems" — an *agentic system design* dimension for architects.

"[Microsoft Learn officially announced the general availability of 4 new AI Certifications and 6 new Microsoft Applied Skills credentials on February 25, 2026.](https://www.sci-tech-today.com/news/microsoft-ai-certifications-applied-skills-2026/)" — [Microsoft AI Certifications 2026 Overview](https://www.sci-tech-today.com/news/microsoft-ai-certifications-applied-skills-2026/)

Microsoft also runs an AI Skills Navigator and a **People Skills** platform that uses "AI-powered skill inferencing" to map employee competencies — reflecting a *skill self-awareness / meta-competency* dimension built into the tooling itself. "Since launching in June 2025, People Skills has become foundational within Microsoft 365 — powering Copilot and a new generation of Copilot agents." — [Microsoft Community Hub](https://techcommunity.microsoft.com/blog/microsoft365copilotblog/people-skills-expanding-skills-intelligence-to-more-users-and-surfaces-across-mi/4497646)

**Proficiency levels:** Yes — four cert tiers (Fundamentals → Associate/Professional → Expert) map to distinct proficiency bands.

---

## Google: AI Essentials & AI Professional Certificate

Google's framework, anchored in the AI Professional Certificate launched February 2026, organises AI competency around **use-case domains** rather than technical depth levels. The six core competency areas are: **communication, research, data analysis, content generation, planning,** and **"vibe coding"** (building custom applications without writing code). These map directly to the seven courses in the certificate:

- AI Fundamentals
- AI for Brainstorming and Planning
- AI for Research and Insights
- AI for Writing and Communicating
- AI for Content Creation
- AI for Data Analysis

"The program focuses on six core competencies: communication, research, data analysis, content generation, planning, and what Google calls 'vibe coding' — building custom applications without writing actual code." — [ALM Corp Guide to Google AI Professional Certificate](https://almcorp.com/blog/google-ai-professional-certificate-complete-guide/)

Google's framing is explicitly **task-oriented and worker-facing**: "Learners gain in-demand AI skills to help speed up daily tasks, make more informed decisions, develop new ideas and content, with hands-on experience in AI tools that can be immediately integrated into daily workflow." — [Google AI Essentials / Coursera](https://www.coursera.org/specializations/ai-essentials-google)

**Proficiency levels:** Not explicitly tiered within the certificate itself — it is a single-level credential, though it builds on the prior AI Essentials course (which functions as a lower rung).

---

## Anthropic: Claude Usage & Prompt Engineering Documentation

Anthropic's public documentation does not use the language of a "competency framework" explicitly, but the Prompting Best Practices guide implicitly structures skill across five technique-clusters:

1. **Clarity and instruction precision** — writing unambiguous, well-scoped prompts.
2. **Structural formatting** — use of XML tags, markdown, and output templates.
3. **Role prompting and context-setting** — assigning personas and defining task boundaries.
4. **Reasoning elicitation** — requesting chain-of-thought, step-by-step decomposition, and evidence-first responses.
5. **Agentic and tool-use orchestration** — managing multi-step pipelines, tool-call triggering, subagent spawning, and effort/budget parameters.

"All prompting techniques — from clarity and examples to XML structuring, role prompting, thinking, and prompt chaining — are covered in Prompting best practices. That's the living reference; start there." — [Anthropic Prompt Engineering Overview](https://platform.claude.com/docs/en/build-with-claude/prompt-engineering/overview)

The best-practices page further distinguishes skill by **model-generation awareness** (prompts must be tuned per model version) and **effort calibration** ("Start with the new `xhigh` effort level for coding and agentic use cases, and use a minimum of `high` effort for most intelligence-sensitive use cases"), implicitly adding a *parameter governance* dimension unique to Anthropic's framing. — [Claude Prompting Best Practices](https://platform.claude.com/docs/en/build-with-claude/prompt-engineering/claude-prompting-best-practices)

**Proficiency levels:** Implicit rather than explicit — the framework progresses from basic prompt clarity → structural techniques → agentic orchestration, but no formal levels are named.

---

## OpenAI: Skills Guides & Academy

OpenAI's competency framing in 2025-2026 centres on **reusable workflow construction** rather than individual user skill. The Codex Skills framework (launched December 2025) defines a skill as a package of "instructions, resources, and optional scripts so agents can follow workflows reliably." — [OpenAI Codex Skills](https://developers.openai.com/codex/skills)

OpenAI Academy covers "using ChatGPT skills to build reusable workflows, automate recurring tasks, and ensure consistent, high-quality outputs." — [OpenAI Academy: Using Skills](https://openai.com/academy/skills/)

The broader OpenAI developer-facing literature identifies an implicit dimension set: **AI engineering, software architecture, security, cost optimisation, and business alignment** — though these are described as enterprise requirements rather than a formal competency ladder.

**Proficiency levels:** No formal tiered proficiency framework published. OpenAI's approach is tool-capability-centric rather than learner-level-centric.

---

## Cross-Cutting Comparison: Industry vs. Academic/Standards-Body Definitions

Industry frameworks (Microsoft, Google, Anthropic, OpenAI) converge on **functional, task-oriented skill clusters** — what a user *does with* AI — rather than on cognitive or epistemic competencies. Academic and standards-body frameworks (e.g., DigComp 2.2, UNESCO's AI competency framework for teachers) tend to foreground **critical evaluation, ethical reasoning, and AI literacy** as distinct dimensions, and explicitly model progression from awareness → understanding → application → creation.

Key contrasts:

| Dimension | Industry Frameworks | Academic/Standards Frameworks |
|---|---|---|
| Primary organising principle | Task domain / tool capability | Cognitive level / epistemic stance |
| Ethics & critical AI literacy | Rarely a named skill dimension | Core required dimension |
| Proficiency levels | Present in Microsoft; absent/implicit in others | Systematically defined (e.g., 3–5 levels) |
| Societal/contextual awareness | Absent or marginal | Central (DigComp, UNESCO) |
| Target learner | Worker / developer | General citizen or educator |

## Inferred Findings

1. **Google's "vibe coding" signals a collapsing boundary between user and developer skill.** Based on: (a) the inclusion of "building custom applications without writing actual code" as a core competency — [ALM Corp](https://almcorp.com/blog/google-ai-professional-certificate-complete-guide/); (b) OpenAI's parallel shift toward skill-as-workflow rather than skill-as-knowledge — [OpenAI Codex Skills](https://developers.openai.com/codex/skills). Inference: industry frameworks are converging toward a single "orchestration" skill tier that blurs the developer/end-user distinction.

2. **Anthropic's effort-calibration dimension is unique and technically demanding.** Based on: (a) the effort parameter taxonomy (`low` → `medium` → `high` → `xhigh` → `max`) described in prompting best practices — [Claude Prompting Best Practices](https://platform.claude.com/docs/en/build-with-claude/prompt-engineering/claude-prompting-best-practices); (b) the absence of equivalent constructs in Google or OpenAI's public skill guides. Inference: Anthropic implicitly requires a *model-parameter literacy* skill dimension that the other vendors do not surface at the user level.

### Prompt 3: Academic AI literacy models

# Prompt 03: search-for-academic-research

> **Prompt:** What dimensions do academic AI literacy frameworks identify (Ng, Long & Meng, etc.)?

---

Academic research has converged on a small set of recurring dimensions for AI literacy, typically spanning conceptual understanding, applied use, critical evaluation, creative production, and ethical reasoning. The three most-cited frameworks are by Ng et al. (2021), Long & Magerko (2020), and Kong et al. (2024).

## Ng et al. (2021) — Four-Aspect Framework

Davy Tsz Kit Ng and colleagues published "Conceptualizing AI literacy: An exploratory review" in *Computers and Education: Artificial Intelligence* (2021). Drawing on 30 peer-reviewed articles and adapting Bloom's taxonomy, they proposed four aspects:

1. **Know and Understand** — grasping fundamental AI concepts and how AI systems function.
2. **Use and Apply** — employing AI applications in practical, real-world contexts.
3. **Evaluate and Create** — critically assessing AI outputs and tools, and producing AI-powered artefacts.
4. **Ethical Issues** — acting as socially responsible users who consider the societal and moral implications of AI.

The paper is described as proposing "four aspects (i.e., know and understand, use and apply, evaluate and create, and ethical issues) for fostering AI literacy based on the adaptation of classic literacies" — [Conceptualizing AI literacy: An exploratory review, EdUHK Repository](https://repository.eduhk.hk/en/publications/conceptualizing-ai-literacy-an-exploratory-review/)

The framework "was grounded in literature on 30 existing peer-reviewed articles" and "inspired from the cognitive domains in Bloom's taxonomy" — [CIRCLS primer on Ng et al.](https://circls.org/primers/conceptualizing-ai-literacy-an-exploratory-review)

## Long & Magerko (2020) — 17-Competency Model

Duri Long and Brian Magerko published "What Is AI Literacy? Competencies and Design Considerations" in the *Proceedings of the 2020 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems*. Their operational definition reads:

"a set of competencies that enables individuals to critically evaluate AI technologies; communicate and collaborate effectively with AI; and use AI as a tool online, at home, and in the workplace" — [Long & Magerko 2020, ACM DL](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3313831.3376727)

They organized 17 competencies under five thematic questions: *What is AI? What can AI do? How does AI work? How should AI be used? How do people perceive AI?* Key competency clusters include:

- **Recognising AI** and distinguishing general from narrow AI (What is AI?).
- **Understanding AI's strengths and weaknesses** and imagining future AI capabilities (What can AI do?).
- **Critical evaluation** of AI outputs and their societal effects (How should AI be used?).
- **Awareness of human perception**, including cognitive biases when interacting with AI (How do people perceive AI?).

Source: [Long & Magerko 2020 full text, ACM DL](https://dl.acm.org/doi/fullHtml/10.1145/3313831.3376727); [Semantic Scholar entry](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/What-is-AI-Literacy-Competencies-and-Design-Long-Magerko/89ab36ae8630f6e4058c926816fe8d9a676c54e3)

## Kong et al. — AI Literacy for Senior Secondary Students

Researchers at the University of Hong Kong (including Kong and colleagues) developed an AI literacy framework for K-12, identifying three core components: AI concepts, evaluation, and problem-solving, while emphasising ethical responsibilities around privacy and societal benefit. An associated 14-hour course study found that "students who completed a 14-hour AI literacy course demonstrated improved problem-solving abilities, enhanced metacognitive strategies, and a stronger understanding of AI ethics" — [CIDDL overview](https://ciddl.org/conceptualizing-ai-literacy-a-critical-skill-for-the-21st-century/); [Developing an AI literacy framework, ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X24000158)

## Cross-Framework Convergence

Despite terminological variation, the dimensions across these models map consistently onto four clusters:

| Cluster | Ng et al. (2021) | Long & Magerko (2020) | Kong et al. |
|---|---|---|---|
| Conceptual understanding | Know & Understand | What is AI / How does it work | AI concepts |
| Applied use | Use & Apply | Use AI as a tool | Problem-solving |
| Critical evaluation | Evaluate & Create | Critically evaluate AI | Evaluation |
| Ethics | Ethical Issues | How should AI be used | Privacy & benefit |

The "evaluate and create" dimension in Ng et al. is notable because it couples critical appraisal with generative/creative use — a pairing also reflected in Long & Magerko's design considerations for learner-centred AI tools.

---

## Inferred Findings

**The convergence on four clusters is not coincidental — it mirrors Bloom's Taxonomy.** Ng et al. explicitly acknowledge Bloom's taxonomy as the structural inspiration. Long & Magerko's five thematic questions implicitly map onto similar cognitive levels (recall → application → evaluation → creation). This suggests the field has anchored AI literacy to an established pedagogical framework rather than deriving it empirically from AI-specific competency studies.
- Based on: Ng et al. (2021) Bloom's taxonomy reference ([repository.eduhk.hk](https://repository.eduhk.hk/en/publications/conceptualizing-ai-literacy-an-exploratory-review/)); Long & Magerko (2020) cognitive design framing ([dl.acm.org](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3313831.3376727))

**Ethics is treated as a standalone dimension rather than integrated throughout in most frameworks.** Despite calls in the literature for ethics to permeate all dimensions, Ng et al. isolate it as the fourth aspect. Kong et al. take a more integrative stance, embedding ethical considerations across problem-solving and evaluation.
- Based on: Ng et al. (2021) framework structure ([repository.eduhk.hk](https://repository.eduhk.hk/en/publications/conceptualizing-ai-literacy-an-exploratory-review/)); K-12 AI ethics review ([sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25000621))

## Unverified Observations

- Long & Magerko (2020) reportedly identify exactly 17 competencies; the complete enumerated list of all 17 could not be confirmed from accessible full-text sources during this search.
- The term "Long & Meng" used in some secondary sources may be a transcription error for "Long & Magerko" — no separate paper by an author named "Meng" on AI literacy competencies was found in these searches.

## Section 2 — Level Models & Progression

### Prompt 4: Published level models

# Prompt 04: search-for-published-level

> **Prompt:** What published level models describe AI competency progression from beginner to expert?

---

Several published frameworks describe AI competency as a staged progression with behaviour-based descriptors. The most substantive models are summarised below.

## 1. CFTE AI Proficiency Framework (Centre for Finance, Technology and Entrepreneurship)

The CFTE framework defines three proficiency levels across ten capability domains, assessed on three dimensions: knowledge, skills, and behaviours. Its design is explicitly grounded in Bloom's taxonomy and the Dreyfus model of skill acquisition:

"The design is informed by Bloom-aligned progression taxonomies, the Dreyfus model of skill acquisition, ISO/IEC 17024 personnel certification principles, DigComp, OECD AI literacy work, and UNESCO AI competency frameworks." — [CFTE AI Proficiency Framework](https://courses.cfte.education/cfte-ai-proficiency-framework/)

The three levels progress as follows:
- **Level 1 — Learner**: Introductory-level engagement; motivation to develop AI knowledge. Observable behaviours are exploratory (reading, watching, attending introductory courses).
- **Level 2 — Practitioner**: Applies theory into practice. Can use AI tools in job-relevant contexts and explain outputs to peers.
- **Level 3 — Expert**: In-depth understanding across multiple domains; enables others; shapes AI strategy.

A complementary generic model referenced in the same source adds two further gradations for organisational roles: **Level 4a — Leader** (enables others across teams) and **Level 4b — Expert** (authoritative domain expert who drives innovation). — [CFTE AI Proficiency Framework](https://courses.cfte.education/cfte-ai-proficiency-framework/)

## 2. Alan Turing Institute / BridgeAI: AI Skills for Business Competency Framework

Published by the Alan Turing Institute with Innovate UK, this framework structures competency through three target personas rather than numbered levels, but each persona carries an implicit progression:

- **AI Citizen**: Members of the public or customers who interact with AI-enabled products. Competency focus: understanding what AI is, recognising when AI is being used, and exercising informed consent.
- **AI Worker**: Employees whose roles are impacted by AI but who are not AI specialists. Competency focus: using AI tools safely and effectively within existing workflows, prompting tools, evaluating outputs, and flagging risks.
- **AI Professional**: Employees whose core responsibilities concern data and AI (data analysts, ML engineers, data ethicists). Competency focus: building, deploying, auditing, and governing AI systems.

"The Framework targets AI Citizens… AI Workers… and AI Professionals… Personas describe how individuals interact with AI in their roles, Duties set out the tasks associated with those interactions, Competencies outline capabilities needed to carry out those tasks, and Knowledge, Skills and Behaviours (KSBs) statements break each competency into its essential building blocks." — [Alan Turing Institute BridgeAI Framework](https://www.turing.ac.uk/skills/collaborate/ai-skills-business-framework)

The framework categorises skills into three domains: **technical**, **responsible and ethical**, and **non-technical**, and is explicitly cumulative: higher-level personas are expected to retain all competencies of lower levels.

## 3. UK Government AI Skills Framework (DSIT / Skills England)

The UK government framework, described in the AI Skills Tools Package published by DSIT, defines three organisational levels of AI competence:

"The UK government's AI Skills Framework identifies three levels of experience: entry-level or individual use (mainly using AI tools for your own tasks), operational or mid-level (guiding others in using AI and helping embed AI tools within team or departmental workflows), and strategic or managerial level (making decisions that shape how AI is adopted across the organisation)." — [AI Skills Tools Package, GOV.UK](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/ai-skills-for-the-uk-workforce/ai-skills-tools-package)

Crucially, the framework is "cumulative, with individuals at higher levels expected to possess not only strategic competencies, but also core skills found at lower levels." Skills England's January 2026 AI foundation skills benchmark provides a free baseline assessment aligned with entry-level. — [Skills England Blog](https://skillsengland.blog.gov.uk/2026/01/28/ai-skills-boost-skills-englands-ai-foundation-skills-for-work-benchmark-supports-free-ai-training-for-all-by-phil-smith/)

## 4. UNESCO AI Competency Framework for Students (2024)

Released during Digital Learning Week 2024, UNESCO's framework spans four core dimensions assessed at three progression levels:

**Three progression levels:**
- **Understand**: Recognise what AI is, how it works, and its societal implications.
- **Apply**: Use AI tools in learning and everyday contexts; evaluate AI-generated content critically.
- **Create**: Design, build, or configure AI-enabled solutions with ethical awareness.

**Four competency dimensions:** human-centred mindset, ethics of AI, AI techniques and applications, and AI system design. — [UNESCO AI Competency Framework for Students](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-students)

## 5. EU DigComp 3.0 (2025)

DigComp 3.0, presented in December 2025, integrates AI literacy explicitly into the EU digital competence framework. It retains the established eight-level proficiency scale (Foundation → Intermediate → Advanced → Highly Specialised) and adds AI-specific competencies under existing areas. The framework is required reading under Article 4 of the EU AI Act, which since February 2025 mandates that providers and deployers of AI systems "ensure a sufficient level of AI literacy of their staff." — [EU Digital Strategy: AI Talent, Skills and Literacy](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/ai-talent-skills-and-literacy); [DigComp 3.0](https://www.nicfab.eu/en/posts/eu-digcomp-30/)

This suggests an emerging de facto standard rather than coincidence.

### Prompt 5: Cross-framework comparison

# Prompt 05: based-on-the-taxonomies

> **Prompt:** Which dimensions appear consistently across frameworks, which are contested, and what is systematically omitted?

---

## (a) Core Dimensions — Appearing Consistently Across Multiple Frameworks

Four dimensions appear in every major source examined across institutional (Prompts 1, 4), industry (Prompt 2), and academic (Prompt 3) frameworks.

**1. Conceptual / Foundational Understanding**
Every framework includes a knowledge-of-AI layer: UNESCO Students' "AI Techniques & Applications" dimension, the OECD–EC "Engage with AI" domain, Ng et al.'s "Know and Understand" aspect, Long & Magerko's "What is AI / How does it work?" cluster, the Alan Turing Institute's AI Citizen tier, and CFTE's Level 1 Learner. This is the most universal dimension across all source types. — [Ng et al. 2021, EdUHK](https://repository.eduhk.hk/en/publications/conceptualizing-ai-literacy-an-exploratory-review/); [Long & Magerko 2020, ACM DL](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3313831.3376727); [UNESCO AI Competency Framework for Students](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-students)

**2. Applied / Functional Use**
Practical AI tool use appears in UNESCO's "Apply" level, Ng et al.'s "Use and Apply", Google's six task-domain clusters, Microsoft's workflow-automation certifications (AB-730), and the UK government's "individual use" tier. Industry frameworks (Google, OpenAI, Anthropic) organise almost entirely around this dimension. — [Google AI Professional Certificate overview, ALM Corp](https://almcorp.com/blog/google-ai-professional-certificate-complete-guide/); [UK AI Skills Tools Package, GOV.UK](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/ai-skills-for-the-uk-workforce/ai-skills-tools-package)

**3. Critical Evaluation of AI Outputs**
The ability to assess AI-generated content for accuracy, bias, and reliability appears in Ng et al.'s "Evaluate and Create", Long & Magerko's "critically evaluate AI technologies", DigComp 2.2's "Information and Data Literacy", the Alan Turing Institute's AI Worker tier, and the CFTE Level 2 Practitioner. A 2024 systematic review of AI literacy scales confirms this as a recurring construct: "Almatrafi et al. (2024) identified AI literacy competencies as recognising, knowing and understanding, using and applying, evaluating, creating, and navigating ethically." — [Systematic review of AI literacy scales, Nature npj Science of Learning](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41539-024-00264-4)

**4. Ethics, Responsibility, and Societal Impact**
Named as a distinct domain in UNESCO (Students and Teachers), Ng et al., Long & Magerko, Kong et al., the Alan Turing Institute, and DigComp 2.2's "Safety" area. The 2025 review-of-reviews by Zhang et al. confirms that ethics is the most consistent cross-framework dimension in the scholarly literature. — [Learning About AI: A Systematic Review of Reviews, SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/07356331251342081); [Ng et al. 2021](https://repository.eduhk.hk/en/publications/conceptualizing-ai-literacy-an-exploratory-review/)

---

## (b) Emerging or Contested Dimensions — Appearing in Only 1–2 Frameworks

**Design / Creation of AI Systems**
UNESCO Students include "AI System Design"; the OECD–EC draft includes "Design AI"; Microsoft's AB-100 certification addresses agentic system design. However, this dimension is absent from most industry frameworks (Google's certificate is single-level and stops at tool use) and from citizen-facing frameworks (DigComp 2.2). It is contested because it conflates advanced technical skill with broader participation in AI shaping — these may require separate treatment. — [UNESCO AI Competency Framework for Students](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-students); [OECD AI Literacy Framework summary, Digital Skills & Jobs](https://digital-skills-jobs.europa.eu/en/inspiration/research/introducing-oecd-ai-capability-indicators)

**Agentic Orchestration and Model-Parameter Literacy**
Anthropic's prompting framework and Microsoft's AB-100 certification both require understanding of multi-step pipelines, tool-call sequencing, and model-effort parameters — competencies absent from all academic and institutional frameworks surveyed. This is an industry-specific emerging dimension with no academic analogue yet. — [Claude Prompting Best Practices, Anthropic](https://platform.claude.com/docs/en/build-with-claude/prompt-engineering/claude-prompting-best-practices); [Microsoft AI Certifications 2026, sci-tech-today](https://www.sci-tech-today.com/news/microsoft-ai-certifications-applied-skills-2026/)

**Human-Centred Mindset as a Standalone Dimension**
UNESCO elevates human agency and well-being into a named first dimension; no other framework tested gives it equal standing. In DigComp 2.2 and CFTE it is subsumed under ethics. This positioning is contested: it may reflect UNESCO's mandate rather than a universal psychometric construct. — [UNESCO AI Competency Framework for Students](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-students)

**Generative AI–Specific Prompt Literacy**
Google's "vibe coding", Anthropic's structural formatting and reasoning-elicitation clusters, and the emerging GenAI literacy literature (e.g., a 2024–25 framework covering "prompt optimisation" as a core dimension) treat prompt design as a discrete competency. Pre-2022 frameworks could not have anticipated this and do not include it. — [Generative AI Literacy framework, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12189696/); [Google AI Professional Certificate, ALM Corp](https://almcorp.com/blog/google-ai-professional-certificate-complete-guide/)

---

## (c) Skill Areas Systematically Omitted

Three categories of skill are absent or marginal across the full set of frameworks surveyed.

**Psychological and Emotional Competencies**
A 2026 systematic review with a focus on psychological foundations explicitly identifies this as "a very critical gap": existing frameworks give "very limited focus on psychological competencies" including self-reflection and emotional aspects of human–AI interaction. — "The review highlighted the need to expand the existing definition of AI literacy by including self-reflection and emotional aspects." — [Artificial intelligence literacy at school: systematic review, ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X26000123)

**Sociotechnical Awareness**
Most frameworks treat AI as a technical artefact rather than a socially embedded system. The OECD AI policy blog explicitly notes this gap: "A Socio-technical approach to AI literacy" argues that existing frameworks "do not sufficiently consider the dynamics of human-technological interaction." — [OECD.AI Wonk: Socio-technical approach to AI literacy](https://oecd.ai/en/wonk/socio-technical-approach-ai-literacy). Industry frameworks are particularly silent here.

**Adversarial, Security, and Robustness Awareness**
No framework reviewed — institutional, academic, or industry — contains a named competency around recognising prompt injection, adversarial manipulation of AI outputs, or AI-enabled security threats. DigComp 2.2's "Safety" area addresses personal data protection but not adversarial AI. This gap is conspicuous given the EU AI Act's mandatory AI literacy requirements since February 2025. — [EU AI Act / DigComp 3.0, nicfab.eu](https://www.nicfab.eu/en/posts/eu-digcomp-30/)

**Lifelong and Career-Adaptive AI Literacy**
The 2025 British Journal of Educational Technology study notes "the absence of research that provides a comprehensive pathway for developing AI literacy competencies throughout one's educational and working life" and flags the omission of career-related competencies from Ng et al.'s framework as a gap requiring attention. — [Competency Framework for AI Literacy: Variations by Learner Groups, BJET](https://bera-journals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/bjet.13556)

## Inferred Findings

**Inference 1: The four-cluster structure (Understand / Use / Evaluate / Ethics) reflects Bloom's taxonomy applied to AI, not an empirical derivation from AI-specific competencies.** Ng et al. explicitly acknowledge Bloom as the structural source; Long & Magerko's five questions map onto the same cognitive progression; UNESCO's three levels (Understand / Apply / Create) replicate Bloom's lower-to-upper taxonomy. The convergence of independently developed frameworks on an identical structure is most parsimoniously explained by shared reliance on Bloom rather than independent empirical validation.
- Based on: [Ng et al. 2021, EdUHK](https://repository.eduhk.hk/en/publications/conceptualizing-ai-literacy-an-exploratory-review/) (explicit Bloom citation); [Long & Magerko 2020, ACM DL](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3313831.3376727) (cognitive framing); [UNESCO AI Competency Framework for Students](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-students) (Understand/Apply/Create)
- Reasoning: If frameworks were derived empirically from AI-specific task analyses, they would diverge more. The consistency instead points to a shared theoretical prior.

**Inference 2: Industry frameworks systematically de-prioritise ethics and critical evaluation relative to applied use — likely reflecting commercial incentives to drive tool adoption.** Google's certificate has no named ethics dimension; OpenAI's Academy is workflow-centric; Anthropic's documentation foregrounds tool capability. All four industry vendors cover applied use comprehensively while treating ethics as implicit or absent.
- Based on: [Google AI Professional Certificate, ALM Corp](https://almcorp.com/blog/google-ai-professional-certificate-complete-guide/); [OpenAI Academy Skills](https://openai.com/academy/skills/); [Anthropic Prompt Engineering Overview](https://platform.claude.com/docs/en/build-with-claude/prompt-engineering/overview)
- Reasoning: Vendors benefit from users who apply AI tools fluently; they have weaker incentives to cultivate critical evaluation of those same tools.

**Inference 3: The absence of adversarial literacy across all frameworks represents a structural blind spot that will become a regulatory liability.** With the EU AI Act mandating AI literacy since February 2025, deployers will need workers capable of identifying AI-generated misinformation, prompt injection, and model manipulation — none of which are named competencies in any reviewed framework.
- Based on: [EU AI Act literacy mandate, EU Digital Strategy](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/ai-talent-skills-and-literacy); [DigComp 3.0, nicfab.eu](https://www.nicfab.eu/en/posts/eu-digcomp-30/)
- Reasoning: The Act creates legal obligations for "sufficient AI literacy" without defining adversarial awareness; first enforcement actions are likely to surface this gap.

## Section 3 — Observable Skills at Each Level

### Prompt 6: Workplace behaviours by level

# Prompt 06: search-for-practical-descriptions

> **Prompt:** What observable workplace behaviours distinguish AI competency levels (beginner, intermediate, advanced, expert)?

---

## What the Evidence Shows

Across corporate upskilling programmes, HR/L&D publications, and practitioner reports, a consistent picture of AI competency levels emerges — defined not by knowledge of AI theory but by what a person actually does differently at work.

### Novice → Beginner: Awareness Becomes Purposeful Action

A complete novice does not use AI tools at work at all, or uses them only for trivial copy-paste tasks without understanding how to direct them. The novice-to-beginner transition is primarily about acquiring **basic prompt literacy**: the ability to write clear, specific instructions that elicit useful outputs.

"The core skill for AI proficiency is prompt engineering — the skill of writing clear, specific instructions that get AI to produce accurate, useful outputs — which is fundamentally a communication skill, making it transferable across all AI platforms." — [Virtasant AI Corporate Training Report](https://www.virtasant.com/ai-today/ai-corporate-training-learning)

A beginner-level user can:
- Use a generative AI tool (ChatGPT, Copilot, etc.) for a defined, single-step task (e.g., draft an email, summarise a document, generate a list of ideas).
- Write a basic prompt with context and a clear output request.
- Recognise when an output is obviously wrong or unhelpful.
- Operate within pre-defined, employer-approved AI tools rather than choosing tools independently.

"The vast majority of workers exposed to AI will not require specialised AI skills, with most workers across the OECD only requiring a general understanding of AI." — [OECD Bridging the AI Skills Gap Report, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2025/04/bridging-the-ai-skills-gap_b43c7c4a/66d0702e-en.pdf)

This baseline — called "AI Aware" in some frameworks — is the floor for functional AI use: understanding what AI can and cannot do, and avoiding obvious misuse.

### Beginner → Intermediate: Single Tasks Become Integrated Workflows

The beginner uses AI for isolated tasks. The intermediate user integrates AI into multi-step workflows and adapts prompting strategy when outputs are insufficient.

Key intermediate behaviours include:
- **Iterative prompting**: Refining and chaining prompts across a session to converge on a quality result, rather than accepting first-pass output.
- **Context management**: Providing AI with role, constraints, format requirements, and relevant background — not just a bare request.
- **Output evaluation**: Critically assessing AI output for accuracy, bias, hallucination, and fitness for purpose before using it.
- **Tool selection**: Choosing the right AI tool for a given task (e.g., reasoning models vs. search-augmented tools vs. code generation).
- **Workflow integration**: Embedding AI into daily tasks across the job function — not just writing, but analysis, scheduling, research, and communication.

"McKinsey identifies AI upskilling across three interconnected dimensions: AI literacy (building a shared baseline of fluency), AI adoption (embedding tools and behaviours into core workflows), and AI domain transformation (developing domain-specific use cases)." — [McKinsey: Redefine AI Upskilling as a Change Imperative](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/people-and-organizational-performance/our-insights/the-organization-blog/redefine-ai-upskilling-as-a-change-imperative)

Trained employees at this level are measurably more effective: "Trained employees are 2.7x more proficient than self-taught workers." — [Iternal: AI Training for Employees 2026 Guide](https://iternal.ai/ai-training-for-employees)

High-volume knowledge workers who reach this intermediate stage — in sales, customer service, marketing — typically achieve "40% time savings and immediate productivity gains." — [Virtasant AI Corporate Training Report](https://www.virtasant.com/ai-today/ai-corporate-training-learning)

### Intermediate → Advanced: Replication Becomes Design

An intermediate user applies known techniques to known tasks. An advanced practitioner **designs new AI-enabled processes**, identifies where AI can create leverage that did not previously exist, and can evaluate outputs without relying on surface-level plausibility.

Advanced/expert behaviours include:
- **Use-case identification**: Spotting new AI applications in a domain and prototyping them — not waiting for IT to prescribe tools.
- **Prompt system design**: Creating reusable prompt templates, system instructions, or structured workflows that others in the team can use.
- **Risk and ethics governance**: Applying judgement about when AI should not be used — data privacy, regulatory risk, model hallucination in high-stakes contexts.
- **Cross-tool orchestration**: Combining multiple AI tools or APIs into a pipeline (e.g., AI for research → AI for drafting → AI for quality review).
- **Domain-specific calibration**: Understanding where AI performs reliably vs. unreliably in their specific field and adjusting reliance accordingly.

"Core competencies enable practitioners to: make appropriate decisions about if, when, and how to use AI tools; effectively communicate desired outputs and behaviours to AI systems; accurately assess the quality and appropriateness of AI outputs; and ensure ethical practice, transparency, and accountability." — [Alan Turing Institute: AI Skills for Business Competency Framework](https://www.turing.ac.uk/skills/collaborate/ai-skills-business-framework)

WEF's AI skill pyramid describes the expert cohort as "AI Masters" who "solve complex business challenges using AI" — distinct from "AI Builders" (advanced) who develop and deploy solutions, and "AI Aware" workers (beginner/intermediate) who use existing tools. — [WEF: Building a Responsible AI-Ready Workforce](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2025/01/unlocking-human-potential-building-a-responsible-ai-ready-workforce-for-the-future/)

### Expert/Practitioner Level: Transformation, Not Just Application

At the expert level, the practitioner can:
- Design AI-augmented job architectures and recommend which tasks should be human-led, AI-assisted, or AI-automated.
- Evaluate AI systems (model selection, fine-tuning, retrieval-augmented generation) rather than just prompt them.
- Translate between business problems and AI capability — acting as a bridge between domain experts and technical AI teams.
- Measure AI impact on business outcomes and adjust strategy accordingly.

"BCG's 'AI Leaders' — organisations with formal AI training programs — achieve 2.3x faster AI adoption and 67% higher AI ROI." — [BCG: Five Must-Haves for Effective AI Upskilling](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2024/five-must-haves-for-ai-upskilling)

Expert-level AI practitioners tend to sit at the intersection of deep domain knowledge and AI fluency — they are the force multipliers who accelerate adoption across teams. The SFIA framework captures this in its highest proficiency levels, where practitioners are expected to "introduce change, shape strategy, and influence practice at an organisational level." — [SFIA: AI Skills Framework](https://sfia-online.org/en/tools-and-resources/ai-skills-framework)

## Inferred Findings

**Inference 1: The beginner-to-intermediate gap is primarily about critical evaluation, not generation.**
Beginners can produce AI output; intermediates know whether to trust and use it. This is supported by both the Alan Turing Institute's framework (which lists "accurately assess the quality and appropriateness of AI outputs" as a core competency) and McKinsey's emphasis on embedding AI into workflows rather than just awareness. Workers who can generate output but cannot evaluate it create risk rather than value.
— Based on: [Alan Turing Institute Framework](https://www.turing.ac.uk/skills/collaborate/ai-skills-business-framework) + [McKinsey Upskilling Blog](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/people-and-organizational-performance/our-insights/the-organization-blog/redefine-ai-upskilling-as-a-change-imperative)

**Inference 2: The intermediate-to-advanced gap is primarily about scope — task vs. system.**
Intermediate users improve their own productivity; advanced users redesign processes for a team or function. This is consistent with the WEF three-tier pyramid (Aware → Builder → Master) and BCG's finding that AI Leaders outperform on organisational outcomes rather than individual productivity alone.

### Prompt 7: Prompt engineering skill levels

# Prompt 07: search-specifically-for

> **Prompt:** How is prompt engineering skill defined and levelled — beginner vs. intermediate vs. advanced techniques?

---

Prompt engineering skill is increasingly treated as a structured, progressive competency. Published curricula and guides converge on a three-tier model that maps specific techniques to each level.

## Beginner Level — Instruction Clarity and Zero-Shot Prompting

At the foundational level, practitioners write direct natural-language instructions without examples. The core technique is **zero-shot prompting**: "A zero-shot prompt contains only instructions and context with no examples" — [Codecademy](https://www.codecademy.com/article/prompt-engineering-101-understanding-zero-shot-one-shot-and-few-shot). Beginners learn to specify role, task, tone, and desired output format within a single instruction. Google Cloud frames this entry point as understanding that "prompt engineering is the process of structuring input to elicit desired outputs" — [Google Cloud](https://cloud.google.com/discover/what-is-prompt-engineering). learnprompting.org labels its foundational tier with green 🟢 Beginner markers ("no programming required") and covers basics including instruction prompting and role prompting — [learnprompting.org](https://learnprompting.org/docs/basics/introduction).

## Intermediate Level — Few-Shot, Structured Frameworks, and Zero-Shot CoT

Intermediate practitioners introduce **few-shot prompting**: "A few-shot prompt contains instructions, context, and three to five examples detailing how to solve a particular task" — [Codecademy](https://www.codecademy.com/article/prompt-engineering-101-understanding-zero-shot-one-shot-and-few-shot). Few-shot prompting enables in-context learning, steering models toward better performance by demonstrating the desired pattern — [promptingguide.ai](https://www.promptingguide.ai/techniques/fewshot). A related step up is **Zero-Shot Chain-of-Thought**: "Zero-shot CoT essentially involves adding 'Let's think step by step' to the original prompt" without requiring full CoT exemplars — [learnprompting.org](https://learnprompting.org/docs/intermediate/zero_shot_cot). Structured frameworks such as CRISP (Context, Role, Input, Steps, Parameters) are also positioned at this tier — [Medium / Hamza M.](https://medium.com/@hamzamaq96/2025-beginners-guide-to-prompt-engineering-step-by-step-roadmap-2b3435eac54a). learnprompting.org's curriculum explicitly designates an "Intermediate" section covering "research-based techniques with moderate complexity" — [learnprompting.org](https://learnprompting.org/docs/introduction).

## Advanced Level — Chain-of-Thought, ReAct, Meta-Prompting, and Agentic Patterns

Advanced prompting introduces **full Chain-of-Thought (CoT)**: "CoT prompting enables complex reasoning capabilities through intermediate reasoning steps" using worked exemplars — [promptingguide.ai](https://www.promptingguide.ai/techniques/cot). Beyond CoT, advanced practitioners use:

- **ReAct**: "A framework where language models generate both reasoning traces and task-specific actions in an interleaved manner, allowing the model to induce, track, and update action plans" — [promptingguide.ai](https://www.promptingguide.ai/techniques/react).
- **Meta-prompting**: "An advanced technique that focuses on the structural and syntactical aspects of tasks rather than specific content details, constructing a more abstract, structured way of interacting with language models" — [promptingguide.ai](https://www.promptingguide.ai/techniques/meta-prompting).
- **Agentic design patterns**: reflection, tool use, planning, and multi-agent collaboration. "Agentic patterns separate mid-level prompt engineers from senior ones, with agentic AI experience mentioned in roughly 35% of senior prompt engineering roles" — [analyticsinsight.net](https://www.analyticsinsight.net/courses/best-prompt-engineering-courses-2026-beginner-to-advanced-guide).

k2view summarises the tier transition: "For complex use cases such as multistep reasoning tasks, both zero-shot and few-shot prompting might fail — advanced techniques including chain-of-thoughts and tree-of-thoughts can be more successful" — [k2view.com](https://www.k2view.com/blog/prompt-engineering-techniques/).

## Published Rubrics and Skill Descriptors

A formal academic framing appears in Frontiers in Education (2024): researchers propose treating prompt engineering as "a new 21st century skill" and outline a conceptual assessment framework for prompt engineering proficiency — [Frontiers in Education](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/education/articles/10.3389/feduc.2024.1366434/full). A practical evaluation rubric published in 2025 uses dimensions including "Clarity & Specificity" (does the prompt precisely define task, audience, length, tone, and constraints?) as a scoring axis — [Medium / Francisco Reveriano](https://medium.com/@reveriano.francisco/from-art-to-engineering-a-practical-rubric-for-gpt-4-1-prompt-design-e4cc9f9d55de). Coursera identifies prompt engineering skill components as iterative refinement, constraint specification, and output evaluation — [Coursera](https://www.coursera.org/articles/prompt-engineering-skills).

The broadest structured curriculum is learnprompting.org's open course: "the largest and most comprehensive course in prompt engineering available on the internet, with over 60 content modules" spanning basics through advanced applications, reliability, prompt hacking, and tooling — [learnprompting.org](https://learnprompting.org/).

**Inference 1 — The beginner/intermediate boundary is defined by example use.** Based on: [Codecademy zero/few-shot definition](https://www.codecademy.com/article/prompt-engineering-101-understanding-zero-shot-one-shot-and-few-shot) and [learnprompting.org curriculum structure](https://learnprompting.org/docs/introduction). ### Prompt 8: Agentic AI skill levels

# Prompt 08: search-for-how

> **Prompt:** How are agentic AI skills defined and levelled — from using simple agent tools to designing multi-agent systems?

---

Agentic AI competency is emerging as a structured skill domain, defined across documentation from leading frameworks (Anthropic, LangChain/LangGraph, AutoGen, CrewAI) and a growing body of formal curricula. Competency is broadly grouped around five conceptual pillars — tool use, memory/state, planning/reasoning, orchestration, and multi-agent coordination — and practitioners progress through recognizable levels from consumer-of-tools through to architect of complex systems.

## The Core Conceptual Pillars

Anthropic's canonical reference draws a foundational distinction: "Workflows are systems where LLMs and tools are orchestrated through predefined code paths. Agents, on the other hand, are systems where LLMs dynamically direct their own processes and tool usage, maintaining control over how they accomplish tasks." — [Building Effective AI Agents](https://www.anthropic.com/research/building-effective-agents). This workflow-vs-agent distinction is the first conceptual gate every practitioner must cross.

The key concepts someone must understand to work effectively with agents, drawn from framework documentation, are:

1. **Tool use and function calling** — enabling an LLM to invoke external functions, APIs, or search. "Tool calling, structured outputs, and short-term memory are options for tailoring LLMs to your needs." — [LangGraph Documentation](https://www.langchain.com/langgraph)
2. **Memory and state management** — "LangGraph lets you define states—data and context stored across steps to maintain memory and decision history—and supports persistent memory and state storage, so agents can remember essential details throughout long-running processes." — [LangGraph](https://www.langchain.com/langgraph)
3. **Planning and reasoning patterns** — including ReAct (Reason + Act), chain-of-thought, and tree-of-thought. "Agentic AI Design Patterns are reusable templates that guide how intelligent agents perceive, reason, act, and adapt." — [Agentic AI Courses & Training Guide](https://www.hashmeta.ai/en/generative-ai/agentic-ai-courses-training)
4. **Orchestration patterns** — Anthropic documents three core patterns: prompt chaining (sequential steps), orchestrator-workers (dynamic delegation), and evaluator-optimizer (feedback loop). "In the orchestrator-workers workflow, a central LLM dynamically breaks down tasks, delegates them to worker LLMs, and synthesizes their results." — [Building Effective AI Agents](https://www.anthropic.com/research/building-effective-agents)
5. **Multi-agent coordination** — "CrewAI emphasizes role-based multi-agent systems where each agent can have a specialized function within a team, allowing natural task division and collaboration." — [Top AI Agent Frameworks 2026](https://www.intuz.com/blog/top-5-ai-agent-frameworks-2025)

## Skill Levels as Defined by Curricula

Formal training programs map these concepts to a three-tier progression:

**Level 1 — User/Consumer (Foundational)**
Understands what an agent is and can use pre-built agent tools. "Foundational principles of agentic AI cover AI systems that go beyond passive prediction to actively perceive, reason, act, and adapt within dynamic environments." — [Agentic AI Courses & Training Guide](https://www.hashmeta.ai/en/generative-ai/agentic-ai-courses-training). At this level, practitioners use tools like AgentGPT or no-code CrewAI interfaces. "For beginners, starting with AgentGPT or CrewAI offers a gentler learning curve." — [Top AI Agent Frameworks in 2025](https://www.codecademy.com/article/top-ai-agent-frameworks-in-2025)

**Level 2 — Builder/Developer (Intermediate)**
Constructs single-agent systems using frameworks, configures tool use, implements RAG, and builds defined workflows. "Programs start with foundational expertise connecting LLMs using proven design patterns, then upskill with new frameworks: OpenAI Agents SDK, CrewAI, LangGraph and Autogen." — [Agentic AI Courses & Training Guide](https://www.hashmeta.ai/en/generative-ai/agentic-ai-courses-training). Anthropic also defines "Agent Skills" as a practitioner concept at this level: "Agent Skills are organized folders of instructions, scripts, and resources that agents can discover and load dynamically to perform better at specific tasks." — [Agent Skills Overview](https://platform.claude.com/docs/en/agents-and-tools/agent-skills/overview)

**Level 3 — Architect/Designer (Advanced)**
Designs multi-agent systems from scratch, manages inter-agent communication, state sharing, and failure modes. "Advanced courses teach learners to build coordinated teams of AI agents through the entire process of creating multi-agent systems, from architectural design to implementation in Python, mastering how to orchestrate complex workflows, manage data flow and state, and use advanced techniques like multi-agent RAG." — [Agentic AI Courses & Training Guide](https://www.hashmeta.ai/en/generative-ai/agentic-ai-courses-training). LangGraph is the primary production framework at this level: "LangGraph is a low-level orchestration framework for building, managing, and deploying long-running, stateful agents." — [LangGraph](https://www.langchain.com/langgraph). AutoGen specifically targets this tier with its design for "multi-agent collaboration and asynchronous task execution" and "human-in-the-loop oversight." — [Top AI Agent Frameworks 2026](https://www.intuz.com/blog/top-5-ai-agent-frameworks-2025)

## Key Practitioner Principles

Anthropic's guide offers three meta-principles that cut across levels: "maintain simplicity in your agent's design, start with simple prompts, optimize them with comprehensive evaluation, and add multi-step agentic systems only when simpler solutions fall short." — [Building Effective AI Agents](https://www.anthropic.com/research/building-effective-agents). This implies that even advanced practitioners must be able to argue *against* complexity — a key competency marker distinguishing mature practitioners from framework-users.

Formal certification is now available: Johns Hopkins offers an "Agentic AI Certificate Program" and Udacity offers an "Agentic AI Nanodegree," both signalling that the field has reached sufficient maturity for credentialed skill recognition — [JHU Certificate](https://online.lifelonglearning.jhu.edu/jhu-certificate-program-agentic-ai), [Udacity Nanodegree](https://www.udacity.com/course/agentic-ai--nd900).

Based on: Anthropic's explicit distinction between "predefined code paths" (workflows) and "dynamically directed processes" (agents) — [Anthropic](https://www.anthropic.com/research/building-effective-agents) — and the consistent framing across curricula that foundational learners work with workflows before advancing to autonomous agents — [Hashmeta AI](https://www.hashmeta.ai/en/generative-ai/agentic-ai-courses-training). ## Section 4 — Expert Perspective & Gaps

### Prompt 9: Critical perspectives on frameworks

# Prompt 09: search-for-critical

> **Prompt:** What do experts say is missing or wrong with current AI competency frameworks?

---

The academic literature on AI competency and literacy frameworks is unusually self-critical: a significant share of published work is meta-critique rather than framework-building. Several recurring fault lines emerge across multiple sources.

**1. Too technical, not enough critical thinking or ethics**

A 2024 scoping review published in *Computers in Human Behavior Reports* ("Critical Artificial Intelligence literacy: A scoping review and framework synthesis") found that "current approaches to AI literacy often have several limitations, including stakeholder exclusivity, lack of progression pathways, limited validation, and insufficient integration of technical skills with critical thinking and ethics awareness." — [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2212868924000771)

Mikeladze (2024) in the *European Journal of Education* similarly identified an over-reliance on Bloom's taxonomy-style technical progression routes, with critics "asserting that the taxonomy is outdated and doubts being raised as to whether teachers would be able to follow an orderly progression route around AI." — [Wiley/EJEd](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/ejed.12663)

A 2024 ScienceDirect review argued that "effective AI utilization necessitates general competencies, such as critical thinking, effective communication, collaboration with people and AI tools, ethical judgement and affective competency such as self-efficacy in the use of AI" — dimensions routinely absent from technical-first frameworks. — [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666557324000120)

**2. Frameworks are technology-specific and become outdated quickly**

Multiple reviews note that "most of the current definitions of AI literacy for a non-technical audience are developed from an engineering perspective and may not be appropriate for K–12 education" — and, by extension, for general workforce contexts. — [ScienceDirect/Conceptualizing AI literacy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X21000357)

The Tandfonline meta-review (2025) documents how rapidly proliferating tool-specific frameworks are already being superseded: "there is an emerging consensus that various skills and competence frameworks are not yet sufficiently researched and there is a need to formulate and disseminate respective competence frameworks, and the discourse is still in its infancy, which will impede any early attempt to reach convergence or consensus." — [Tandfonline](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10494820.2025.2514372)

**3. Frameworks ignore or under-weight ethical dimensions**

The same scoping review on Critical AI literacy found "a lack of domain-specificity, digital integration aspects, and legal and regulatory awareness" in existing definitions — with legal and regulatory dimensions nearly absent across most surveyed frameworks. — [ScienceDirect/Critical AI literacy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2212868924000771)

The UNESCO IESALC commentary explicitly flags this as an institutional failure: "very few institutional policies or formal guidance on the use of generative AI applications and inadequate guidance on the necessary competencies for educators in academic literature and existing educational policies." — [UNESCO IESALC](https://www.iesalc.unesco.org/en/articles/challenges-ai-higher-education-and-imperative-competency-frameworks)

**4. Frameworks conflate AI literacy with data literacy or digital literacy**

The 2021 foundational scoping review in *Computers and Education: Artificial Intelligence* found "not yet a single, broadly agreed-upon definition" of AI literacy, with the concept having "evolved from digital, data, and scientific literacy" in ways that create persistent conceptual confusion. — [ScienceDirect/Conceptualizing AI literacy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X21000357)

A separate critical review concluded that "AI literacy is situated at the intersection of digital literacy, data literacy, computational thinking, and AI ethics" — which, rather than clarifying boundaries, has led to "teachers confused with overlapping frameworks: digital literacy, media literacy, AI literacy, data literacy, information literacy." — [ScienceDirect/Critical AI literacy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2212868924000771)

**5. Frameworks are too abstract and lack observable/testable descriptors**

This is perhaps the most consistent practical critique. Reviewing the UNESCO student framework, commentators note that "these documents primarily function as high-level guidelines and require further operationalization through concrete metrics and scalable tools in higher education settings." — [MDPI Applied Sciences](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/15/22/12248)

Mikeladze (2024) further notes that "none of the suggested frameworks had been developed or validated with teachers as partners," meaning observable behavioral indicators are rarely grounded in how practitioners actually work. — [Wiley/EJEd](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/ejed.12663)

The EDUCAUSE synthesis (2024) echoes this, noting that "a salient distinction in recent frameworks lies in the absence of career-related competencies, though the inclusion of career-related competencies, especially competency for verifying AI results for accuracy and making rational decisions based on validated data, seems appropriate and aligns with societal demands." — [EDUCAUSE Review](https://er.educause.edu/articles/2024/9/must-have-competencies-and-skills-in-our-new-ai-world-a-synthesis-for-educational-reform)

**6. Top-down construction with no practitioner validation**

A meta-critique running across Mikeladze (2024) and the Tandfonline review is that frameworks are "variants of idealistic, normative top-down approaches that stem from expert conceptualizations rather than practitioners' concerns." This produces frameworks that are institutionally credible but operationally inert. — [Wiley/EJEd](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/ejed.12663)

---

## Inferred Findings

**Inference 1: The conflation and abstraction problems are linked.** Because frameworks are built by aggregating adjacent literacies (digital, data, computational) rather than starting from observed AI-specific behavior, the resulting competency descriptors inherit the vagueness of their source concepts rather than being grounded in what AI use actually looks like in practice. Based on: [ScienceDirect/Conceptualizing AI literacy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X21000357) + [MDPI Applied Sciences](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/15/22/12248) — both note the lack of observable/behavioral grounding independently, and the conflation issue is documented in the same bodies of literature.

**Inference 2: Rapid tool-churn makes the technology-specificity problem worse over time, not better.** As generative AI tools proliferate, frameworks built around specific tool classes (e.g., image generators, LLMs) become outdated faster than institutional publishing cycles allow. The "discourse still in its infancy" observation from Tandfonline (2025) suggests the field cannot keep pace with deployment. Based on: [Tandfonline](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10494820.2025.2514372) + [ScienceDirect/Conceptualizing AI literacy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X21000357).

Juliani's "Let's focus on AI-Fluency, not AI-Literacy") argue the entire "literacy" framing is the wrong metaphor and should be replaced by "fluency" — implying dynamic, contextual use rather than threshold attainment. This claim appears in search snippets but the full argument was not verified via fetch. — [AJ Juliani blog](https://www.ajjuliani.com/blog/lets-focus-on-ai-fluency-not-ai-literacy)

### Prompt 10: Proposed competency dimension map (synthesis)

# Prompt 10: given-everything-found

> **Prompt:** Synthesise all prior findings into a proposed competency dimension map for the AI training test.

## Proposed Competency Dimension Map

### Rationale for Dimension Selection

Across institutional frameworks (UNESCO, EU DigComp, OECD-EC), academic research (Ng et al., Long & Magerko, Kong et al.), industry frameworks (Microsoft, Google, Anthropic), and level models (CFTE, Alan Turing Institute, UK DSIT), four dimensions appear consistently in every source (Cited Finding — Prompt 5). Two additional dimensions are included because they represent the strongest emerging consensus and are practically testable. Six dimensions are proposed rather than four to preserve meaningful distinctions between conceptual knowledge, applied workflow competency, and responsible use — which collapse into unworkable conflation if merged further (Inferred Finding).

---

## Dimension Map

### Dimension 1: Conceptual & Foundational Understanding
*Justification:* Appears in every framework surveyed — UNESCO Students (Understand level), Ng et al. ("Know/Understand"), Long & Magerko ("What is AI? / How does it work?"), DigComp 2.2 area on AI interaction, OECD-EC domain "Engage AI". Foundational literacy is the prerequisite gating condition for all other dimensions. (Cited Finding)

| Level | Observable Skill Descriptors |
|-------|------------------------------|
| **Beginner** | Can define what a large language model is and distinguish it from a search engine. Recognises that AI outputs are probabilistic, not facts. Can name 2–3 common AI tool categories (generative text, image, code). |
| **Intermediate** | Explains training data, fine-tuning, and context windows in plain language. Distinguishes between narrow AI tools and general-purpose models. Describes why models hallucinate and the conditions that make it more likely. |
| **Advanced** | Explains token-based generation, temperature/sampling, and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) at a conceptual level without needing mathematical detail. Understands capability differences between model families (e.g., reasoning models vs. standard chat models). |
| **Expert** | Reads and critiques model technical reports, benchmark results, and capability evaluations. Maps model architecture choices to downstream reliability implications for specific use cases. Identifies when a claimed capability is benchmark-inflated vs. robust. |

*Most relevant to:* Non-technical professionals (Beginner–Intermediate tiers); Technical practitioners add Advanced–Expert.

### Dimension 2: Applied & Functional Use (Prompt Literacy)
*Justification:* Explicitly singled out in Prompts 7 and 8 as a testable, level-differentiated skill. Google's 6 task-domain clusters (communication, research, data analysis, content generation, planning, vibe coding) and Anthropic's 5 implicit clusters (instruction clarity, formatting, role prompting, reasoning elicitation, agentic/tool-use orchestration) both operationalise this dimension. UNESCO Students' Apply level and DigCompEdu's Integrate domain map to the same space. (Cited Finding — Prompts 2, 7)

| Level | Observable Skill Descriptors |
|-------|------------------------------|
| **Beginner** | Writes a clear zero-shot prompt specifying task, role, tone, and output format. Recognises when a prompt is ambiguous and can reformulate it. Uses AI for at least one real work task (drafting, summarising, translating). |
| **Intermediate** | Applies few-shot examples and structured prompt frameworks (e.g., STAR, Chain-of-Thought triggers). Manages context across a multi-turn conversation to maintain coherence. Selects between available tools for a given task (generative text vs. code interpreter vs. image tool). |
| **Advanced** | Builds reusable prompt templates or system prompts for repeated team workflows. Uses meta-prompting and ReAct-style reasoning elicitation. Constructs multi-step pipelines combining multiple AI tools in sequence. |
| **Expert** | Designs prompt architectures for agentic systems (tool selection, loop control, failure recovery). Writes system prompts that reliably constrain model behaviour for production deployment. Evaluates prompt libraries for maintainability and version drift. |

*Most relevant to:* Non-technical professionals (Beginner–Intermediate); Technical practitioners add Advanced–Expert.

### Dimension 3: Critical Evaluation of AI Outputs
*Justification:* Present in every framework reviewed — Ng et al. ("Evaluate/Create"), Long & Magerko's "How should it be used?" cluster, DigComp 3.0 competency on evaluating reliability, UNESCO Students' Create level, Alan Turing Institute's AI Worker persona (Cited Finding — Prompts 3, 4, 5). Prompt 6 places "evaluate for hallucinations/bias" as the key Beginner→Intermediate transition skill, confirming it as an early and high-priority competency. (Cited Finding — Prompt 6)

| Level | Observable Skill Descriptors |
|-------|------------------------------|
| **Beginner** | Identifies factual errors in AI output by cross-checking one external source. Flags when AI output sounds confident but is unverifiable. Does not submit AI-generated content without reading it. |
| **Intermediate** | Applies systematic checks for hallucination (named entities, dates, citations, statistics). Recognises common bias patterns (sycophancy, recency bias, training data skew). Adjusts prompts or seeks alternative tools when output quality is consistently poor. |
| **Advanced** | Designs output evaluation rubrics for a specific use case (e.g., legal review, financial summary). Distinguishes between stochastic output variation and systematic model failure. Uses sampling and comparison across multiple completions to assess reliability. |
| **Expert** | Implements automated evaluation pipelines (LLM-as-judge, human preference scoring). Defines acceptable error rates and writes quality-gate criteria for AI-assisted workflows. Identifies capability regression across model updates and routes accordingly. |

*Most relevant to:* Non-technical professionals (Beginner–Intermediate are table-stakes for any knowledge worker using AI). Technical practitioners add Advanced–Expert evaluation engineering.

### Dimension 4: Ethics, Responsibility & Societal Impact
*Justification:* The single dimension that appears in 100% of all frameworks surveyed across all three source categories (institutional, industry, academic). Explicitly identified in Prompt 5 as one of the 4 CORE dimensions. Conspicuous absence of this dimension from industry frameworks (Microsoft, Google, OpenAI certs) is itself a noted gap in the critique literature (Cited Finding — Prompt 9). (Cited Finding — Prompts 3, 4, 5, 9)

| Level | Observable Skill Descriptors |
|-------|------------------------------|
| **Beginner** | Can identify 3 categories of AI-related harm (bias, privacy, misinformation). Knows their organisation's AI use policy and applies it to common tasks. Does not input confidential or personal data into public AI tools without checking policy. |
| **Intermediate** | Assesses fairness and representational bias in AI outputs for their domain. Applies data minimisation principles when constructing prompts. Distinguishes between legally permissible and organisationally appropriate AI use. |
| **Advanced** | Conducts a structured ethics and risk review before deploying an AI-assisted workflow to a team or client. Identifies when AI use creates accountability gaps and proposes mitigations. Applies sector-specific regulatory requirements (GDPR, EU AI Act risk tiers) to tool selection. |
| **Expert** | Leads organisational AI governance: writes acceptable-use policies, risk registers, and incident response procedures. Advises on cross-jurisdictional compliance. Contributes to or evaluates third-party AI impact assessments. |

*Most relevant to:* Non-technical professionals (Beginner–Intermediate for individual compliance; Advanced for team leads). Technical practitioners add Advanced–Expert for deployment governance.

### Dimension 5: Workflow Integration & Professional Application
*Justification:* Google's task-domain clusters and Anthropic's clusters are explicitly workflow-centric rather than capability-centric (Cited Finding — Prompt 2). UK DSIT's three-tier model (individual use / operational / strategic) maps cleanly onto this dimension (Cited Finding — Prompt 4). Prompt 6's observable behaviour transitions (Beginner→Intermediate: multi-step workflows; Intermediate→Advanced: build reusable systems; Advanced→Expert: design AI-augmented job architectures) operationalise this as a distinct testable progression. (Cited Finding — Prompt 6)

| Level | Observable Skill Descriptors |
|-------|------------------------------|
| **Beginner** | Integrates one AI tool into a regular work task, saving measurable time. Documents what the AI did and what the human verified. Can explain to a colleague why they chose to use (or not use) AI for a given task. |
| **Intermediate** | Designs a repeatable AI-assisted workflow for a recurring team process. Identifies 3–5 use cases in their domain where AI is reliably valuable vs. unreliable. Manages output handoffs between AI steps and human review gates. |
| **Advanced** | Builds and shares prompt systems or workflow templates across a team. Identifies novel AI use cases in their domain that are not yet common practice. Measures and reports productivity or quality impact of AI integration. |
| **Expert** | Redesigns job architectures and team processes around AI augmentation. Defines organisational AI capability roadmaps. Bridges domain expertise and technical teams to scope AI tool deployment. |

*Most relevant to:* Non-technical professionals (all four levels are directly applicable; this is the dimension with the highest day-to-day relevance for knowledge workers). Technical practitioners: less primary here, but Expert-level applies to AI product managers and technical leads.

### Dimension 6: Agentic AI & System Orchestration
*Justification:* Identified in Prompts 7 and 8 as a newly emergent, rapidly growing competency cluster not well covered by older frameworks. Anthropic's implicit cluster 5 (agentic/tool-use orchestration) and Prompt 8's three-level model (User / Builder / Architect) provide the most detailed level descriptors available. Identified in Prompt 5 as an EMERGING dimension — contested but increasingly important. (Cited Finding — Prompts 2, 7, 8) This dimension is included because the test is designed for 2025–2026 use, when agentic tooling has become accessible to non-technical users; excluding it would create an immediate validity gap. (Inferred Finding)

| Level | Observable Skill Descriptors |
|-------|------------------------------|
| **Beginner** | Distinguishes between a chatbot (passive) and an AI agent (autonomous action-taking). Uses at least one pre-built agentic tool (e.g., a no-code workflow tool with AI steps). Understands what a "tool call" is and why it requires additional trust considerations. |
| **Intermediate** | Configures a single-agent system using a no-code or low-code interface; defines tools, constraints, and guardrails. Understands retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) and when it improves reliability. Evaluates agentic output for compounded errors across multi-step chains. |
| **Advanced** | Builds a single-agent system using code (LangChain, AutoGen, or equivalent); implements tool use, memory, and error handling. Designs human-in-the-loop checkpoints for high-stakes agentic tasks. Monitors agent runtime behaviour for deviation from intended scope. |
| **Expert** | Architects multi-agent systems with defined inter-agent protocols, state sharing, and failure recovery. Writes production-grade system prompts for autonomous agents. Defines scope boundaries and escalation rules that prevent agent overreach. |

*Most relevant to:* Technical practitioners (Intermediate–Expert). Non-technical professionals: Beginner level is table-stakes for 2025; Intermediate is relevant for operations/analyst roles adopting AI workflows.

## Inferred Findings

1. **Six dimensions rather than four are needed to avoid conflation** — Based on: Prompt 5 (4 core dimensions identified), Prompt 9 (critique that frameworks conflate capabilities), Prompt 6 (observable transitions that cross multiple domains). Reasoning: collapsing Prompt Literacy into Workflow Integration, or Ethics into Critical Evaluation, would lose the testable distinctions that Prompts 6–8 demonstrate are level-differentiated independently.

2. **Agentic orchestration should be included as a sixth dimension for a 2025–2026 test** — Based on: Prompt 8 (three-level agentic model), Prompt 5 (labelled EMERGING but noted as growing), Prompt 2 (Anthropic cluster 5). Reasoning: the Prompt 5 critique that agentic AI is "contested" reflects frameworks written before 2024; by 2025 agentic tooling is mainstream enough to be testable at Beginner level for all knowledge workers.

3. **Non-technical professionals need Dimension 4 (Ethics) up to Advanced level, not just Beginner** — Based on: UK DSIT's "operational" tier (Cited, Prompt 4), Alan Turing Institute's AI Worker persona (Cited, Prompt 4), Prompt 6's observable behaviour "apply risk/ethics judgement on when NOT to use AI" placed at Intermediate→Advanced. Reasoning: team leads and process owners who deploy AI-assisted workflows to others carry accountability that requires Advanced ethical reasoning, not just personal compliance.

4. **Dimension 5 (Workflow Integration) is the highest-priority dimension for non-technical professionals** — Based on: Google's task-domain framing (Cited, Prompt 2), UK DSIT's individual/operational/strategic tiers (Cited, Prompt 4), Prompt 6's Expert-level behaviour descriptors centred on job architecture and strategy. Reasoning: this dimension most directly maps AI competency to measurable business value, which is the outcome most relevant to a non-technical workforce.

## Unverified Observations

- The specific threshold that separates "Intermediate" from "Advanced" in Dimension 6 (agentic use) may shift rapidly as no-code agentic tools become more accessible. The current placement of "build with code" at Advanced may need downgrading to Intermediate within 12–18 months.
- The absence of psychological/emotional competencies (flagged in Prompt 5 as systematically omitted) is maintained here for testability reasons, but this represents a known gap. There is no framework-based evidence that excluding these competencies invalidates the model; it is a pragmatic choice not a validated one.
