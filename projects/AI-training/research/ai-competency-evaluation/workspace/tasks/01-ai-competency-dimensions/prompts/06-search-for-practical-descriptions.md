# Prompt 06: search-for-practical-descriptions

> **Prompt:** What observable workplace behaviours distinguish AI competency levels (beginner, intermediate, advanced, expert)?

---

## What the Evidence Shows

Across corporate upskilling programmes, HR/L&D publications, and practitioner reports, a consistent picture of AI competency levels emerges — defined not by knowledge of AI theory but by what a person actually does differently at work.

### Novice → Beginner: Awareness Becomes Purposeful Action

A complete novice does not use AI tools at work at all, or uses them only for trivial copy-paste tasks without understanding how to direct them. The novice-to-beginner transition is primarily about acquiring **basic prompt literacy**: the ability to write clear, specific instructions that elicit useful outputs.

"The core skill for AI proficiency is prompt engineering — the skill of writing clear, specific instructions that get AI to produce accurate, useful outputs — which is fundamentally a communication skill, making it transferable across all AI platforms." — [Virtasant AI Corporate Training Report](https://www.virtasant.com/ai-today/ai-corporate-training-learning)

A beginner-level user can:
- Use a generative AI tool (ChatGPT, Copilot, etc.) for a defined, single-step task (e.g., draft an email, summarise a document, generate a list of ideas).
- Write a basic prompt with context and a clear output request.
- Recognise when an output is obviously wrong or unhelpful.
- Operate within pre-defined, employer-approved AI tools rather than choosing tools independently.

"The vast majority of workers exposed to AI will not require specialised AI skills, with most workers across the OECD only requiring a general understanding of AI." — [OECD Bridging the AI Skills Gap Report, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2025/04/bridging-the-ai-skills-gap_b43c7c4a/66d0702e-en.pdf)

This baseline — called "AI Aware" in some frameworks — is the floor for functional AI use: understanding what AI can and cannot do, and avoiding obvious misuse.

### Beginner → Intermediate: Single Tasks Become Integrated Workflows

The beginner uses AI for isolated tasks. The intermediate user integrates AI into multi-step workflows and adapts prompting strategy when outputs are insufficient.

Key intermediate behaviours include:
- **Iterative prompting**: Refining and chaining prompts across a session to converge on a quality result, rather than accepting first-pass output.
- **Context management**: Providing AI with role, constraints, format requirements, and relevant background — not just a bare request.
- **Output evaluation**: Critically assessing AI output for accuracy, bias, hallucination, and fitness for purpose before using it.
- **Tool selection**: Choosing the right AI tool for a given task (e.g., reasoning models vs. search-augmented tools vs. code generation).
- **Workflow integration**: Embedding AI into daily tasks across the job function — not just writing, but analysis, scheduling, research, and communication.

"McKinsey identifies AI upskilling across three interconnected dimensions: AI literacy (building a shared baseline of fluency), AI adoption (embedding tools and behaviours into core workflows), and AI domain transformation (developing domain-specific use cases)." — [McKinsey: Redefine AI Upskilling as a Change Imperative](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/people-and-organizational-performance/our-insights/the-organization-blog/redefine-ai-upskilling-as-a-change-imperative)

Trained employees at this level are measurably more effective: "Trained employees are 2.7x more proficient than self-taught workers." — [Iternal: AI Training for Employees 2026 Guide](https://iternal.ai/ai-training-for-employees)

High-volume knowledge workers who reach this intermediate stage — in sales, customer service, marketing — typically achieve "40% time savings and immediate productivity gains." — [Virtasant AI Corporate Training Report](https://www.virtasant.com/ai-today/ai-corporate-training-learning)

### Intermediate → Advanced: Replication Becomes Design

An intermediate user applies known techniques to known tasks. An advanced practitioner **designs new AI-enabled processes**, identifies where AI can create leverage that did not previously exist, and can evaluate outputs without relying on surface-level plausibility.

Advanced/expert behaviours include:
- **Use-case identification**: Spotting new AI applications in a domain and prototyping them — not waiting for IT to prescribe tools.
- **Prompt system design**: Creating reusable prompt templates, system instructions, or structured workflows that others in the team can use.
- **Risk and ethics governance**: Applying judgement about when AI should not be used — data privacy, regulatory risk, model hallucination in high-stakes contexts.
- **Cross-tool orchestration**: Combining multiple AI tools or APIs into a pipeline (e.g., AI for research → AI for drafting → AI for quality review).
- **Domain-specific calibration**: Understanding where AI performs reliably vs. unreliably in their specific field and adjusting reliance accordingly.

"Core competencies enable practitioners to: make appropriate decisions about if, when, and how to use AI tools; effectively communicate desired outputs and behaviours to AI systems; accurately assess the quality and appropriateness of AI outputs; and ensure ethical practice, transparency, and accountability." — [Alan Turing Institute: AI Skills for Business Competency Framework](https://www.turing.ac.uk/skills/collaborate/ai-skills-business-framework)

WEF's AI skill pyramid describes the expert cohort as "AI Masters" who "solve complex business challenges using AI" — distinct from "AI Builders" (advanced) who develop and deploy solutions, and "AI Aware" workers (beginner/intermediate) who use existing tools. — [WEF: Building a Responsible AI-Ready Workforce](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2025/01/unlocking-human-potential-building-a-responsible-ai-ready-workforce-for-the-future/)

### Expert/Practitioner Level: Transformation, Not Just Application

At the expert level, the practitioner can:
- Design AI-augmented job architectures and recommend which tasks should be human-led, AI-assisted, or AI-automated.
- Evaluate AI systems (model selection, fine-tuning, retrieval-augmented generation) rather than just prompt them.
- Translate between business problems and AI capability — acting as a bridge between domain experts and technical AI teams.
- Measure AI impact on business outcomes and adjust strategy accordingly.

"BCG's 'AI Leaders' — organisations with formal AI training programs — achieve 2.3x faster AI adoption and 67% higher AI ROI." — [BCG: Five Must-Haves for Effective AI Upskilling](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2024/five-must-haves-for-ai-upskilling)

Expert-level AI practitioners tend to sit at the intersection of deep domain knowledge and AI fluency — they are the force multipliers who accelerate adoption across teams. The SFIA framework captures this in its highest proficiency levels, where practitioners are expected to "introduce change, shape strategy, and influence practice at an organisational level." — [SFIA: AI Skills Framework](https://sfia-online.org/en/tools-and-resources/ai-skills-framework)

## Inferred Findings

**Inference 1: The beginner-to-intermediate gap is primarily about critical evaluation, not generation.**
Beginners can produce AI output; intermediates know whether to trust and use it. This is supported by both the Alan Turing Institute's framework (which lists "accurately assess the quality and appropriateness of AI outputs" as a core competency) and McKinsey's emphasis on embedding AI into workflows rather than just awareness. Workers who can generate output but cannot evaluate it create risk rather than value.
— Based on: [Alan Turing Institute Framework](https://www.turing.ac.uk/skills/collaborate/ai-skills-business-framework) + [McKinsey Upskilling Blog](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/people-and-organizational-performance/our-insights/the-organization-blog/redefine-ai-upskilling-as-a-change-imperative)

**Inference 2: The intermediate-to-advanced gap is primarily about scope — task vs. system.**
Intermediate users improve their own productivity; advanced users redesign processes for a team or function. This is consistent with the WEF three-tier pyramid (Aware → Builder → Master) and BCG's finding that AI Leaders outperform on organisational outcomes rather than individual productivity alone.
— Based on: [WEF AI Workforce Story](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2025/01/unlocking-human-potential-building-a-responsible-ai-ready-workforce-for-the-future/) + [BCG Five Must-Haves](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2024/five-must-haves-for-ai-upskilling)

## Unverified Observations

- Some practitioners describe a "plateau effect" where intermediate users stop improving because they receive positive feedback on AI-generated output without realising higher-quality output is achievable with better prompting. This is widely cited informally in practitioner communities but is not yet backed by a published study found in this search.
- The distinction between "advanced" and "expert" is not consistently defined across frameworks — several treat them as a single tier, while others (notably SFIA and WEF) define them separately based on whether the practitioner works on individual use-cases (advanced) vs. shapes organisational AI strategy (expert).
