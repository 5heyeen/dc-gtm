# Prompt 03: search-for-academic-research

> **Prompt:** What dimensions do academic AI literacy frameworks identify (Ng, Long & Meng, etc.)?

---

Academic research has converged on a small set of recurring dimensions for AI literacy, typically spanning conceptual understanding, applied use, critical evaluation, creative production, and ethical reasoning. The three most-cited frameworks are by Ng et al. (2021), Long & Magerko (2020), and Kong et al. (2024).

## Ng et al. (2021) — Four-Aspect Framework

Davy Tsz Kit Ng and colleagues published "Conceptualizing AI literacy: An exploratory review" in *Computers and Education: Artificial Intelligence* (2021). Drawing on 30 peer-reviewed articles and adapting Bloom's taxonomy, they proposed four aspects:

1. **Know and Understand** — grasping fundamental AI concepts and how AI systems function.
2. **Use and Apply** — employing AI applications in practical, real-world contexts.
3. **Evaluate and Create** — critically assessing AI outputs and tools, and producing AI-powered artefacts.
4. **Ethical Issues** — acting as socially responsible users who consider the societal and moral implications of AI.

The paper is described as proposing "four aspects (i.e., know and understand, use and apply, evaluate and create, and ethical issues) for fostering AI literacy based on the adaptation of classic literacies" — [Conceptualizing AI literacy: An exploratory review, EdUHK Repository](https://repository.eduhk.hk/en/publications/conceptualizing-ai-literacy-an-exploratory-review/)

The framework "was grounded in literature on 30 existing peer-reviewed articles" and "inspired from the cognitive domains in Bloom's taxonomy" — [CIRCLS primer on Ng et al.](https://circls.org/primers/conceptualizing-ai-literacy-an-exploratory-review)

## Long & Magerko (2020) — 17-Competency Model

Duri Long and Brian Magerko published "What Is AI Literacy? Competencies and Design Considerations" in the *Proceedings of the 2020 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems*. Their operational definition reads:

"a set of competencies that enables individuals to critically evaluate AI technologies; communicate and collaborate effectively with AI; and use AI as a tool online, at home, and in the workplace" — [Long & Magerko 2020, ACM DL](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3313831.3376727)

They organized 17 competencies under five thematic questions: *What is AI? What can AI do? How does AI work? How should AI be used? How do people perceive AI?* Key competency clusters include:

- **Recognising AI** and distinguishing general from narrow AI (What is AI?).
- **Understanding AI's strengths and weaknesses** and imagining future AI capabilities (What can AI do?).
- **Critical evaluation** of AI outputs and their societal effects (How should AI be used?).
- **Awareness of human perception**, including cognitive biases when interacting with AI (How do people perceive AI?).

Source: [Long & Magerko 2020 full text, ACM DL](https://dl.acm.org/doi/fullHtml/10.1145/3313831.3376727); [Semantic Scholar entry](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/What-is-AI-Literacy-Competencies-and-Design-Long-Magerko/89ab36ae8630f6e4058c926816fe8d9a676c54e3)

## Kong et al. — AI Literacy for Senior Secondary Students

Researchers at the University of Hong Kong (including Kong and colleagues) developed an AI literacy framework for K-12, identifying three core components: AI concepts, evaluation, and problem-solving, while emphasising ethical responsibilities around privacy and societal benefit. An associated 14-hour course study found that "students who completed a 14-hour AI literacy course demonstrated improved problem-solving abilities, enhanced metacognitive strategies, and a stronger understanding of AI ethics" — [CIDDL overview](https://ciddl.org/conceptualizing-ai-literacy-a-critical-skill-for-the-21st-century/); [Developing an AI literacy framework, ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X24000158)

## Cross-Framework Convergence

Despite terminological variation, the dimensions across these models map consistently onto four clusters:

| Cluster | Ng et al. (2021) | Long & Magerko (2020) | Kong et al. |
|---|---|---|---|
| Conceptual understanding | Know & Understand | What is AI / How does it work | AI concepts |
| Applied use | Use & Apply | Use AI as a tool | Problem-solving |
| Critical evaluation | Evaluate & Create | Critically evaluate AI | Evaluation |
| Ethics | Ethical Issues | How should AI be used | Privacy & benefit |

The "evaluate and create" dimension in Ng et al. is notable because it couples critical appraisal with generative/creative use — a pairing also reflected in Long & Magerko's design considerations for learner-centred AI tools.

---

## Inferred Findings

**The convergence on four clusters is not coincidental — it mirrors Bloom's Taxonomy.** Ng et al. explicitly acknowledge Bloom's taxonomy as the structural inspiration. Long & Magerko's five thematic questions implicitly map onto similar cognitive levels (recall → application → evaluation → creation). This suggests the field has anchored AI literacy to an established pedagogical framework rather than deriving it empirically from AI-specific competency studies.
- Based on: Ng et al. (2021) Bloom's taxonomy reference ([repository.eduhk.hk](https://repository.eduhk.hk/en/publications/conceptualizing-ai-literacy-an-exploratory-review/)); Long & Magerko (2020) cognitive design framing ([dl.acm.org](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3313831.3376727))

**Ethics is treated as a standalone dimension rather than integrated throughout in most frameworks.** Despite calls in the literature for ethics to permeate all dimensions, Ng et al. isolate it as the fourth aspect. Kong et al. take a more integrative stance, embedding ethical considerations across problem-solving and evaluation.
- Based on: Ng et al. (2021) framework structure ([repository.eduhk.hk](https://repository.eduhk.hk/en/publications/conceptualizing-ai-literacy-an-exploratory-review/)); K-12 AI ethics review ([sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X25000621))

## Unverified Observations

- Long & Magerko (2020) reportedly identify exactly 17 competencies; the complete enumerated list of all 17 could not be confirmed from accessible full-text sources during this search.
- The term "Long & Meng" used in some secondary sources may be a transcription error for "Long & Magerko" — no separate paper by an author named "Meng" on AI literacy competencies was found in these searches.
