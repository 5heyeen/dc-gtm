# Prompt 05: based-on-the-taxonomies

> **Prompt:** Which dimensions appear consistently across frameworks, which are contested, and what is systematically omitted?

---

## (a) Core Dimensions — Appearing Consistently Across Multiple Frameworks

Four dimensions appear in every major source examined across institutional (Prompts 1, 4), industry (Prompt 2), and academic (Prompt 3) frameworks.

**1. Conceptual / Foundational Understanding**
Every framework includes a knowledge-of-AI layer: UNESCO Students' "AI Techniques & Applications" dimension, the OECD–EC "Engage with AI" domain, Ng et al.'s "Know and Understand" aspect, Long & Magerko's "What is AI / How does it work?" cluster, the Alan Turing Institute's AI Citizen tier, and CFTE's Level 1 Learner. This is the most universal dimension across all source types. — [Ng et al. 2021, EdUHK](https://repository.eduhk.hk/en/publications/conceptualizing-ai-literacy-an-exploratory-review/); [Long & Magerko 2020, ACM DL](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3313831.3376727); [UNESCO AI Competency Framework for Students](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-students)

**2. Applied / Functional Use**
Practical AI tool use appears in UNESCO's "Apply" level, Ng et al.'s "Use and Apply", Google's six task-domain clusters, Microsoft's workflow-automation certifications (AB-730), and the UK government's "individual use" tier. Industry frameworks (Google, OpenAI, Anthropic) organise almost entirely around this dimension. — [Google AI Professional Certificate overview, ALM Corp](https://almcorp.com/blog/google-ai-professional-certificate-complete-guide/); [UK AI Skills Tools Package, GOV.UK](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/ai-skills-for-the-uk-workforce/ai-skills-tools-package)

**3. Critical Evaluation of AI Outputs**
The ability to assess AI-generated content for accuracy, bias, and reliability appears in Ng et al.'s "Evaluate and Create", Long & Magerko's "critically evaluate AI technologies", DigComp 2.2's "Information and Data Literacy", the Alan Turing Institute's AI Worker tier, and the CFTE Level 2 Practitioner. A 2024 systematic review of AI literacy scales confirms this as a recurring construct: "Almatrafi et al. (2024) identified AI literacy competencies as recognising, knowing and understanding, using and applying, evaluating, creating, and navigating ethically." — [Systematic review of AI literacy scales, Nature npj Science of Learning](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41539-024-00264-4)

**4. Ethics, Responsibility, and Societal Impact**
Named as a distinct domain in UNESCO (Students and Teachers), Ng et al., Long & Magerko, Kong et al., the Alan Turing Institute, and DigComp 2.2's "Safety" area. The 2025 review-of-reviews by Zhang et al. confirms that ethics is the most consistent cross-framework dimension in the scholarly literature. — [Learning About AI: A Systematic Review of Reviews, SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/07356331251342081); [Ng et al. 2021](https://repository.eduhk.hk/en/publications/conceptualizing-ai-literacy-an-exploratory-review/)

## (b) Emerging or Contested Dimensions — Appearing in Only 1–2 Frameworks

**Design / Creation of AI Systems**
UNESCO Students include "AI System Design"; the OECD–EC draft includes "Design AI"; Microsoft's AB-100 certification addresses agentic system design. However, this dimension is absent from most industry frameworks (Google's certificate is single-level and stops at tool use) and from citizen-facing frameworks (DigComp 2.2). It is contested because it conflates advanced technical skill with broader participation in AI shaping — these may require separate treatment. — [UNESCO AI Competency Framework for Students](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-students); [OECD AI Literacy Framework summary, Digital Skills & Jobs](https://digital-skills-jobs.europa.eu/en/inspiration/research/introducing-oecd-ai-capability-indicators)

**Agentic Orchestration and Model-Parameter Literacy**
Anthropic's prompting framework and Microsoft's AB-100 certification both require understanding of multi-step pipelines, tool-call sequencing, and model-effort parameters — competencies absent from all academic and institutional frameworks surveyed. This is an industry-specific emerging dimension with no academic analogue yet. — [Claude Prompting Best Practices, Anthropic](https://platform.claude.com/docs/en/build-with-claude/prompt-engineering/claude-prompting-best-practices); [Microsoft AI Certifications 2026, sci-tech-today](https://www.sci-tech-today.com/news/microsoft-ai-certifications-applied-skills-2026/)

**Human-Centred Mindset as a Standalone Dimension**
UNESCO elevates human agency and well-being into a named first dimension; no other framework tested gives it equal standing. In DigComp 2.2 and CFTE it is subsumed under ethics. This positioning is contested: it may reflect UNESCO's mandate rather than a universal psychometric construct. — [UNESCO AI Competency Framework for Students](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-students)

**Generative AI–Specific Prompt Literacy**
Google's "vibe coding", Anthropic's structural formatting and reasoning-elicitation clusters, and the emerging GenAI literacy literature (e.g., a 2024–25 framework covering "prompt optimisation" as a core dimension) treat prompt design as a discrete competency. Pre-2022 frameworks could not have anticipated this and do not include it. — [Generative AI Literacy framework, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12189696/); [Google AI Professional Certificate, ALM Corp](https://almcorp.com/blog/google-ai-professional-certificate-complete-guide/)

## (c) Skill Areas Systematically Omitted

Three categories of skill are absent or marginal across the full set of frameworks surveyed.

**Psychological and Emotional Competencies**
A 2026 systematic review with a focus on psychological foundations explicitly identifies this as "a very critical gap": existing frameworks give "very limited focus on psychological competencies" including self-reflection and emotional aspects of human–AI interaction. — "The review highlighted the need to expand the existing definition of AI literacy by including self-reflection and emotional aspects." — [Artificial intelligence literacy at school: systematic review, ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X26000123)

**Sociotechnical Awareness**
Most frameworks treat AI as a technical artefact rather than a socially embedded system. The OECD AI policy blog explicitly notes this gap: "A Socio-technical approach to AI literacy" argues that existing frameworks "do not sufficiently consider the dynamics of human-technological interaction." — [OECD.AI Wonk: Socio-technical approach to AI literacy](https://oecd.ai/en/wonk/socio-technical-approach-ai-literacy). Industry frameworks are particularly silent here.

**Adversarial, Security, and Robustness Awareness**
No framework reviewed — institutional, academic, or industry — contains a named competency around recognising prompt injection, adversarial manipulation of AI outputs, or AI-enabled security threats. DigComp 2.2's "Safety" area addresses personal data protection but not adversarial AI. This gap is conspicuous given the EU AI Act's mandatory AI literacy requirements since February 2025. — [EU AI Act / DigComp 3.0, nicfab.eu](https://www.nicfab.eu/en/posts/eu-digcomp-30/)

**Lifelong and Career-Adaptive AI Literacy**
The 2025 British Journal of Educational Technology study notes "the absence of research that provides a comprehensive pathway for developing AI literacy competencies throughout one's educational and working life" and flags the omission of career-related competencies from Ng et al.'s framework as a gap requiring attention. — [Competency Framework for AI Literacy: Variations by Learner Groups, BJET](https://bera-journals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/bjet.13556)

## Inferred Findings

**Inference 1: The four-cluster structure (Understand / Use / Evaluate / Ethics) reflects Bloom's taxonomy applied to AI, not an empirical derivation from AI-specific competencies.** Ng et al. explicitly acknowledge Bloom as the structural source; Long & Magerko's five questions map onto the same cognitive progression; UNESCO's three levels (Understand / Apply / Create) replicate Bloom's lower-to-upper taxonomy. The convergence of independently developed frameworks on an identical structure is most parsimoniously explained by shared reliance on Bloom rather than independent empirical validation.
- Based on: [Ng et al. 2021, EdUHK](https://repository.eduhk.hk/en/publications/conceptualizing-ai-literacy-an-exploratory-review/) (explicit Bloom citation); [Long & Magerko 2020, ACM DL](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3313831.3376727) (cognitive framing); [UNESCO AI Competency Framework for Students](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-students) (Understand/Apply/Create)
- Reasoning: If frameworks were derived empirically from AI-specific task analyses, they would diverge more. The consistency instead points to a shared theoretical prior.

**Inference 2: Industry frameworks systematically de-prioritise ethics and critical evaluation relative to applied use — likely reflecting commercial incentives to drive tool adoption.** Google's certificate has no named ethics dimension; OpenAI's Academy is workflow-centric; Anthropic's documentation foregrounds tool capability. All four industry vendors cover applied use comprehensively while treating ethics as implicit or absent.
- Based on: [Google AI Professional Certificate, ALM Corp](https://almcorp.com/blog/google-ai-professional-certificate-complete-guide/); [OpenAI Academy Skills](https://openai.com/academy/skills/); [Anthropic Prompt Engineering Overview](https://platform.claude.com/docs/en/build-with-claude/prompt-engineering/overview)
- Reasoning: Vendors benefit from users who apply AI tools fluently; they have weaker incentives to cultivate critical evaluation of those same tools.

**Inference 3: The absence of adversarial literacy across all frameworks represents a structural blind spot that will become a regulatory liability.** With the EU AI Act mandating AI literacy since February 2025, deployers will need workers capable of identifying AI-generated misinformation, prompt injection, and model manipulation — none of which are named competencies in any reviewed framework.
- Based on: [EU AI Act literacy mandate, EU Digital Strategy](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/ai-talent-skills-and-literacy); [DigComp 3.0, nicfab.eu](https://www.nicfab.eu/en/posts/eu-digcomp-30/)
- Reasoning: The Act creates legal obligations for "sufficient AI literacy" without defining adversarial awareness; first enforcement actions are likely to surface this gap.

## Unverified Observations

- The "AI Literacy Heptagon" framework (arxiv.org, 2025) claims to address higher education specifically and may include dimensions not covered by the frameworks in this study; the full dimension list was not retrieved.
- SFIA 9's AI skills map reportedly uses a seven-level responsibility scale that would provide the most granular adversarial and security dimension of any framework, but full descriptors were not publicly accessible.
- The final OECD–EC AI Literacy Framework (expected 2026) may address the sociotechnical and psychological gaps identified here; the draft version was not fully retrievable.
