# Prompt 04: search-for-published-level

> **Prompt:** What published level models describe AI competency progression from beginner to expert?

---

Several published frameworks describe AI competency as a staged progression with behaviour-based descriptors. The most substantive models are summarised below.

## 1. CFTE AI Proficiency Framework (Centre for Finance, Technology and Entrepreneurship)

The CFTE framework defines three proficiency levels across ten capability domains, assessed on three dimensions: knowledge, skills, and behaviours. Its design is explicitly grounded in Bloom's taxonomy and the Dreyfus model of skill acquisition:

"The design is informed by Bloom-aligned progression taxonomies, the Dreyfus model of skill acquisition, ISO/IEC 17024 personnel certification principles, DigComp, OECD AI literacy work, and UNESCO AI competency frameworks." — [CFTE AI Proficiency Framework](https://courses.cfte.education/cfte-ai-proficiency-framework/)

The three levels progress as follows:
- **Level 1 — Learner**: Introductory-level engagement; motivation to develop AI knowledge. Observable behaviours are exploratory (reading, watching, attending introductory courses).
- **Level 2 — Practitioner**: Applies theory into practice. Can use AI tools in job-relevant contexts and explain outputs to peers.
- **Level 3 — Expert**: In-depth understanding across multiple domains; enables others; shapes AI strategy.

A complementary generic model referenced in the same source adds two further gradations for organisational roles: **Level 4a — Leader** (enables others across teams) and **Level 4b — Expert** (authoritative domain expert who drives innovation). — [CFTE AI Proficiency Framework](https://courses.cfte.education/cfte-ai-proficiency-framework/)

## 2. Alan Turing Institute / BridgeAI: AI Skills for Business Competency Framework

Published by the Alan Turing Institute with Innovate UK, this framework structures competency through three target personas rather than numbered levels, but each persona carries an implicit progression:

- **AI Citizen**: Members of the public or customers who interact with AI-enabled products. Competency focus: understanding what AI is, recognising when AI is being used, and exercising informed consent.
- **AI Worker**: Employees whose roles are impacted by AI but who are not AI specialists. Competency focus: using AI tools safely and effectively within existing workflows, prompting tools, evaluating outputs, and flagging risks.
- **AI Professional**: Employees whose core responsibilities concern data and AI (data analysts, ML engineers, data ethicists). Competency focus: building, deploying, auditing, and governing AI systems.

"The Framework targets AI Citizens… AI Workers… and AI Professionals… Personas describe how individuals interact with AI in their roles, Duties set out the tasks associated with those interactions, Competencies outline capabilities needed to carry out those tasks, and Knowledge, Skills and Behaviours (KSBs) statements break each competency into its essential building blocks." — [Alan Turing Institute BridgeAI Framework](https://www.turing.ac.uk/skills/collaborate/ai-skills-business-framework)

The framework categorises skills into three domains: **technical**, **responsible and ethical**, and **non-technical**, and is explicitly cumulative: higher-level personas are expected to retain all competencies of lower levels.

## 3. UK Government AI Skills Framework (DSIT / Skills England)

The UK government framework, described in the AI Skills Tools Package published by DSIT, defines three organisational levels of AI competence:

"The UK government's AI Skills Framework identifies three levels of experience: entry-level or individual use (mainly using AI tools for your own tasks), operational or mid-level (guiding others in using AI and helping embed AI tools within team or departmental workflows), and strategic or managerial level (making decisions that shape how AI is adopted across the organisation)." — [AI Skills Tools Package, GOV.UK](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/ai-skills-for-the-uk-workforce/ai-skills-tools-package)

Crucially, the framework is "cumulative, with individuals at higher levels expected to possess not only strategic competencies, but also core skills found at lower levels." Skills England's January 2026 AI foundation skills benchmark provides a free baseline assessment aligned with entry-level. — [Skills England Blog](https://skillsengland.blog.gov.uk/2026/01/28/ai-skills-boost-skills-englands-ai-foundation-skills-for-work-benchmark-supports-free-ai-training-for-all-by-phil-smith/)

## 4. UNESCO AI Competency Framework for Students (2024)

Released during Digital Learning Week 2024, UNESCO's framework spans four core dimensions assessed at three progression levels:

**Three progression levels:**
- **Understand**: Recognise what AI is, how it works, and its societal implications.
- **Apply**: Use AI tools in learning and everyday contexts; evaluate AI-generated content critically.
- **Create**: Design, build, or configure AI-enabled solutions with ethical awareness.

**Four competency dimensions:** human-centred mindset, ethics of AI, AI techniques and applications, and AI system design. — [UNESCO AI Competency Framework for Students](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-students)

## 5. EU DigComp 3.0 (2025)

DigComp 3.0, presented in December 2025, integrates AI literacy explicitly into the EU digital competence framework. It retains the established eight-level proficiency scale (Foundation → Intermediate → Advanced → Highly Specialised) and adds AI-specific competencies under existing areas. The framework is required reading under Article 4 of the EU AI Act, which since February 2025 mandates that providers and deployers of AI systems "ensure a sufficient level of AI literacy of their staff." — [EU Digital Strategy: AI Talent, Skills and Literacy](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/ai-talent-skills-and-literacy); [DigComp 3.0](https://www.nicfab.eu/en/posts/eu-digcomp-30/)

## Inferred Findings

**Convergence on a three-tier functional model:** Across the CFTE framework, the UK government framework, the Alan Turing Institute's persona model, and UNESCO's Understand–Apply–Create scale, a three-tier progression recurs: (1) awareness/use for self, (2) guided application and peer support, (3) design/strategy/governance. This suggests an emerging de facto standard rather than coincidence.
- Based on: [CFTE AI Proficiency Framework](https://courses.cfte.education/cfte-ai-proficiency-framework/); [AI Skills Tools Package, GOV.UK](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/ai-skills-for-the-uk-workforce/ai-skills-tools-package); [UNESCO AI Competency Framework for Students](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-students)
- Reasoning: All three independently arrive at a usage/application/creation or individual/operational/strategic triad, suggesting this maps naturally onto Bloom's taxonomy applied to tool use.

**Behaviour-based descriptors are the differentiating feature of stronger frameworks:** Frameworks that reach down to observable KSBs (Alan Turing Institute) or behaviour-anchored descriptors (CFTE) are more actionable for assessment than knowledge-only taxonomies. This aligns with ISO/IEC 17024 certification principles cited by CFTE.
- Based on: [CFTE AI Proficiency Framework](https://courses.cfte.education/cfte-ai-proficiency-framework/); [Alan Turing Institute BridgeAI Framework](https://www.turing.ac.uk/skills/collaborate/ai-skills-business-framework)

## Unverified Observations

- The SFIA AI Skills Framework (sfia-online.org) maps AI/ML skills across its standard seven-level responsibility scale (from Level 1 entry-level to Level 7 C-suite strategy), which would provide the most granular progression model of any framework found, but the full level descriptors for AI-specific skills could not be retrieved due to access restrictions. Source homepage only: [SFIA AI Skills Framework](https://sfia-online.org/en/tools-and-resources/ai-skills-framework)
- The AI Literacy Framework developed jointly by the European Commission and OECD (draft released May 2025, final version expected 2026) may provide a more detailed beginner-to-expert scale than DigComp 3.0 alone, but the draft content was not publicly retrievable at time of research. — [EU Education Area Event](https://education.ec.europa.eu/event/empowering-learners-for-the-age-of-ai-launch-of-the-draft-ai-literacy-framework-and-stakeholder-consultations)
