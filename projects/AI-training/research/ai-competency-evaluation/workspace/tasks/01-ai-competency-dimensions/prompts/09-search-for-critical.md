# Prompt 09: search-for-critical

> **Prompt:** What do experts say is missing or wrong with current AI competency frameworks?

---

The academic literature on AI competency and literacy frameworks is unusually self-critical: a significant share of published work is meta-critique rather than framework-building. Several recurring fault lines emerge across multiple sources.

**1. Too technical, not enough critical thinking or ethics**

A 2024 scoping review published in *Computers in Human Behavior Reports* ("Critical Artificial Intelligence literacy: A scoping review and framework synthesis") found that "current approaches to AI literacy often have several limitations, including stakeholder exclusivity, lack of progression pathways, limited validation, and insufficient integration of technical skills with critical thinking and ethics awareness." — [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2212868924000771)

Mikeladze (2024) in the *European Journal of Education* similarly identified an over-reliance on Bloom's taxonomy-style technical progression routes, with critics "asserting that the taxonomy is outdated and doubts being raised as to whether teachers would be able to follow an orderly progression route around AI." — [Wiley/EJEd](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/ejed.12663)

A 2024 ScienceDirect review argued that "effective AI utilization necessitates general competencies, such as critical thinking, effective communication, collaboration with people and AI tools, ethical judgement and affective competency such as self-efficacy in the use of AI" — dimensions routinely absent from technical-first frameworks. — [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666557324000120)

**2. Frameworks are technology-specific and become outdated quickly**

Multiple reviews note that "most of the current definitions of AI literacy for a non-technical audience are developed from an engineering perspective and may not be appropriate for K–12 education" — and, by extension, for general workforce contexts. — [ScienceDirect/Conceptualizing AI literacy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X21000357)

The Tandfonline meta-review (2025) documents how rapidly proliferating tool-specific frameworks are already being superseded: "there is an emerging consensus that various skills and competence frameworks are not yet sufficiently researched and there is a need to formulate and disseminate respective competence frameworks, and the discourse is still in its infancy, which will impede any early attempt to reach convergence or consensus." — [Tandfonline](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10494820.2025.2514372)

**3. Frameworks ignore or under-weight ethical dimensions**

The same scoping review on Critical AI literacy found "a lack of domain-specificity, digital integration aspects, and legal and regulatory awareness" in existing definitions — with legal and regulatory dimensions nearly absent across most surveyed frameworks. — [ScienceDirect/Critical AI literacy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2212868924000771)

The UNESCO IESALC commentary explicitly flags this as an institutional failure: "very few institutional policies or formal guidance on the use of generative AI applications and inadequate guidance on the necessary competencies for educators in academic literature and existing educational policies." — [UNESCO IESALC](https://www.iesalc.unesco.org/en/articles/challenges-ai-higher-education-and-imperative-competency-frameworks)

**4. Frameworks conflate AI literacy with data literacy or digital literacy**

The 2021 foundational scoping review in *Computers and Education: Artificial Intelligence* found "not yet a single, broadly agreed-upon definition" of AI literacy, with the concept having "evolved from digital, data, and scientific literacy" in ways that create persistent conceptual confusion. — [ScienceDirect/Conceptualizing AI literacy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X21000357)

A separate critical review concluded that "AI literacy is situated at the intersection of digital literacy, data literacy, computational thinking, and AI ethics" — which, rather than clarifying boundaries, has led to "teachers confused with overlapping frameworks: digital literacy, media literacy, AI literacy, data literacy, information literacy." — [ScienceDirect/Critical AI literacy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2212868924000771)

**5. Frameworks are too abstract and lack observable/testable descriptors**

This is perhaps the most consistent practical critique. Reviewing the UNESCO student framework, commentators note that "these documents primarily function as high-level guidelines and require further operationalization through concrete metrics and scalable tools in higher education settings." — [MDPI Applied Sciences](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/15/22/12248)

Mikeladze (2024) further notes that "none of the suggested frameworks had been developed or validated with teachers as partners," meaning observable behavioral indicators are rarely grounded in how practitioners actually work. — [Wiley/EJEd](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/ejed.12663)

The EDUCAUSE synthesis (2024) echoes this, noting that "a salient distinction in recent frameworks lies in the absence of career-related competencies, though the inclusion of career-related competencies, especially competency for verifying AI results for accuracy and making rational decisions based on validated data, seems appropriate and aligns with societal demands." — [EDUCAUSE Review](https://er.educause.edu/articles/2024/9/must-have-competencies-and-skills-in-our-new-ai-world-a-synthesis-for-educational-reform)

**6. Top-down construction with no practitioner validation**

A meta-critique running across Mikeladze (2024) and the Tandfonline review is that frameworks are "variants of idealistic, normative top-down approaches that stem from expert conceptualizations rather than practitioners' concerns." This produces frameworks that are institutionally credible but operationally inert. — [Wiley/EJEd](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/ejed.12663)

---

## Inferred Findings

**Inference 1: The conflation and abstraction problems are linked.** Because frameworks are built by aggregating adjacent literacies (digital, data, computational) rather than starting from observed AI-specific behavior, the resulting competency descriptors inherit the vagueness of their source concepts rather than being grounded in what AI use actually looks like in practice. Based on: [ScienceDirect/Conceptualizing AI literacy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X21000357) + [MDPI Applied Sciences](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/15/22/12248) — both note the lack of observable/behavioral grounding independently, and the conflation issue is documented in the same bodies of literature.

**Inference 2: Rapid tool-churn makes the technology-specificity problem worse over time, not better.** As generative AI tools proliferate, frameworks built around specific tool classes (e.g., image generators, LLMs) become outdated faster than institutional publishing cycles allow. The "discourse still in its infancy" observation from Tandfonline (2025) suggests the field cannot keep pace with deployment. Based on: [Tandfonline](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10494820.2025.2514372) + [ScienceDirect/Conceptualizing AI literacy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X21000357).
