## Overview of DigComp Framework Evolution

The EU's Digital Competence Framework (DigComp) has evolved through several major versions. DigComp 2.1 (2017) established the eight-level proficiency architecture. DigComp 2.2 (March 2022) extended the framework with AI-specific examples and knowledge descriptors. DigComp 3.0 (November 2025) represents the most significant revision, systematically integrating AI competence across all 21 competences in the framework.

## DigComp 2.2: AI Integration

Published in March 2022 by the European Commission's Joint Research Centre, DigComp 2.2 "takes account of emerging technologies such as Artificial Intelligence, the Internet of Things and datafication" — ["Digital Competences Framework (DigComp 2.2) update published"](https://employment-social-affairs.ec.europa.eu/news/digital-competences-framework-digcomp-22-update-published-2022-03-22_en).

The update added "more than 70 examples that can help citizens to better understand where and in which situations in their everyday life they can expect to encounter AI systems" — ["Digital Competence Framework (DigComp), Joint Research Centre"](https://joint-research-centre.ec.europa.eu/projects-and-activities/education-and-training/digital-transformation-education/digital-competence-framework-digcomp_en). These examples are distributed across the five core competence areas: Information & Data Literacy, Communication & Collaboration, Digital Content Creation, Safety, and Problem Solving.

Key AI-relevant competency areas in DigComp 2.2 include:
- **1.3 Managing data, information and digital content** — understanding how AI systems process and profile data
- **2.4 Collaborating through digital technologies** — interacting with AI-assisted platforms
- **4.2 Protecting personal data and privacy** — understanding algorithmic profiling and data rights
- **4.4 Protecting the environment** — awareness of AI's energy footprint
- **5.4 Identifying digital competence gaps** — recognising where AI changes skill requirements

The framework's goal is "to empower all citizens to become confident, critical and responsible users of digital technologies driven by AI systems and autonomous decision-making" — ["Data literacy in the new EU DigComp 2.2 framework", Emerald Publishing](https://www.emerald.com/ils/article/125/5-6/406/1222695/Data-literacy-in-the-new-EU-DigComp-22-framework).

## The Eight Proficiency Levels (DigComp 2.1 / 2.2 Architecture)

The eight levels, inherited from DigComp 2.1 and retained through DigComp 3.0, are grouped into four bands, each defined by cognitive challenge, task complexity, and autonomy:

| Band | Levels | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **Foundation** | 1–2 | With guidance; simple, well-defined tasks |
| **Intermediate** | 3–4 | Independently; well-defined and varied tasks |
| **Advanced** | 5–6 | Guiding others; complex and non-routine tasks |
| **Highly Specialised** | 7–8 | Creating new solutions; leading expert-level or specialised work |

"Each level description contains knowledge, skills and attitudes, and represents a step up in citizens' acquisition of the competence according to its cognitive challenge, the complexity of the tasks they can handle and their autonomy in completing the task" — ["DigComp 2.1: The Digital Competence Framework for Citizens with eight proficiency levels", JRC Publications](https://publications.jrc.ec.europa.eu/repository/handle/JRC106281).

At Level 3–4 (Intermediate), individuals "identify and implement well-defined tasks and solve well-defined problems autonomously." At Level 5–6 (Advanced), individuals "assess and apply solutions to a variety of complex tasks autonomously and adapt to a variety of contexts to evaluate and execute tasks appropriately, guiding others if and as required." At Level 7–8 (Highly Specialised), individuals "assess, evaluate and resolve highly complex or specialised problems to create new solutions or adapt existing ones" — ["EU DigComp 3.0: A New Milestone for Digital Competence in Europe", AI Pioneers](https://aipioneers.org/digcomp-3-0-a-new-milestone-for-digital-competence-in-europe/).

## DigComp 3.0: Systematic AI Competence Integration (November 2025)

DigComp 3.0, published 27 November 2025, is "the fifth version of the European Digital Competence Framework" and integrates "AI, wellbeing, disinformation, cybersecurity, rights and sustainability in digital skills" — ["The European Commission updates its digital competence framework DigComp", JRC](https://joint-research-centre.ec.europa.eu/jrc-news-and-updates/european-commission-updates-its-digital-competence-framework-digcomp-2025-11-27_en).

Crucially, "AI-related knowledge, skills and attitudes are featured in all 21 competences of DigComp 3.0" — ["DigComp 3.0 page, Joint Research Centre"](https://joint-research-centre.ec.europa.eu/projects-and-activities/education-and-training/digital-transformation-education/digital-competence-framework-digcomp/digcomp-30_en). This represents a shift from DigComp 2.2, where AI appeared primarily as illustrative examples, to full structural integration across all competence areas.

DigComp 3.0 also introduces "a new set of over 500 learning outcomes — an important policy and practical tool that can be used to help bridge digital skills gaps" — ["A New Chapter for Digital Skills in Europe, Digital Skills & Jobs Platform"](https://digital-skills-jobs.europa.eu/en/latest/news/new-chapter-digital-skills-europe-explore-digcomp-30). The context driving this update: "92% of EU workers now use digital technologies in their jobs, 30% use AI systems, and yet only 15% have received AI training."

## EU AI Act Article 4: Mandatory AI Literacy

Article 4 of the EU AI Act entered into force on 2 February 2025. It mandates that "AI system providers and deployers … ensure that their staff and other persons engaged in the operation of their AI systems have a sufficient level of AI literacy" — ["Upcoming EU AI Act Obligations: Mandatory Training and Prohibited Practices", Latham & Watkins](https://www.lw.com/en/insights/upcoming-eu-ai-act-obligations-mandatory-training-and-prohibited-practices).

The EU AI Act defines AI literacy as "the skills, knowledge and understanding required to facilitate the informed deployment of AI systems and to gain awareness about the opportunities and risks of AI and possible harms" — ["AI Literacy — Questions & Answers", European Commission](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/faqs/ai-literacy-questions-answers).

Key characteristics of the obligation:
- **Broad scope**: Unlike most AI Act obligations, Article 4 "is not limited to high-risk AI systems. Even if your organisation only uses minimal-risk AI — chatbots, content generators, translation tools, scheduling assistants — Article 4 still applies" — ["AI Literacy Under the EU AI Act Article 4", CompliQuest](https://www.compliquest.com/en/blog/ai-literacy-eu-ai-act-article-4-guide).
- **Enforcement date**: "The supervision and enforcement of the AI literacy rules start on 2 August 2026" — ["AI Literacy — Questions & Answers", European Commission](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/faqs/ai-literacy-questions-answers).
- **No prescribed curriculum**: "There is no one size fits all when it comes to AI literacy and the AI Office does not intend to impose strict requirements or mandatory trainings" — ["AI Literacy — Questions & Answers", European Commission](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/faqs/ai-literacy-questions-answers). The obligation is outcomes-based; DigComp provides a voluntary reference framework.
- **Liability risk**: While no direct fines apply for Article 4 violations, providers and deployers "may face civil liability, for instance if the use of AI systems by staff who have not been adequately trained causes harm to consumers, business partners, or other third parties" — ["EU AI Act: Ban on certain AI practices and requirements for AI literacy come into effect", Mayer Brown](https://www.mayerbrown.com/en/insights/publications/2025/01/eu-ai-act-ban-on-certain-ai-practices-and-requirements-for-ai-literacy-come-into-effect).

The EU has also launched a separate AI Literacy Framework specifically for education, with a draft published for stakeholder consultation in 2025 — ["Empowering learners for the age of AI", European Education Area](https://education.ec.europa.eu/event/empowering-learners-for-the-age-of-ai-launch-of-the-draft-ai-literacy-framework-and-stakeholder-consultations).

## Inferred Findings

**DigComp 3.0 as Article 4 compliance reference:** Given that DigComp 3.0 (November 2025) was published after Article 4 entered force (February 2025) and explicitly integrates AI across all 21 competences, it is highly likely that the European Commission designed DigComp 3.0 in part to serve as an operationalisation tool for Article 4 AI literacy obligations.
- Based on: ["Commission updates digital competence framework", Employment, Social Affairs and Inclusion](https://employment-social-affairs.ec.europa.eu/news/commission-updates-digital-competence-framework-2025-12-01_en) and ["AI Literacy — Questions & Answers"](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/faqs/ai-literacy-questions-answers).
- Reasoning: The timing, the Commission's FAQ noting no prescriptive curriculum, and DigComp 3.0's 500+ learning outcomes covering all AI competences are consistent with DigComp 3.0 serving as the de facto reference standard for demonstrating Article 4 compliance.

### Prompt 2: UNESCO AI Competency Frameworks in detail

# Prompt 02: unesco-ai-frameworks-detail

> **Prompt:** UNESCO AI Competency Frameworks (Students & Teachers, 2024) — specific competencies, level descriptors, and adoption

---

## Overview

UNESCO published two linked AI Competency Frameworks in August–September 2024, released during Digital Learning Week 2024: one for school students and one for teachers. "The publication outlines 12 competencies across four dimensions: Human-centred mindset, Ethics of AI, AI techniques and applications, and AI system design" for students, while the teacher framework "outlines 15 competencies across five dimensions" — [UNESCO Student Framework](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-students) / [UNESCO Teacher Framework](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-teachers).

---

## Student Framework (AI Competency Framework for Students)

**Structure:** 4 dimensions × 3 progression levels = 12 competency blocks, yielding 36 measurable indicators — [What you need to know](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/what-you-need-know-about-unescos-new-ai-competency-frameworks-students-and-teachers).

### Four Dimensions

1. **Human-centred mindset** — "Encouraging students to understand and assert their agency in relation to AI" — [UNESCO Student Framework](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-students).

2. **Ethics of AI** — "Teaching responsible use, ethics-by-design and safe practices" — [UNESCO Student Framework](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-students).

3. **AI techniques and applications** — "Providing foundational AI knowledge and skills" — [UNESCO Student Framework](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-students).

4. **AI system design** — "Fostering problem-solving, creativity and design thinking" — [UNESCO Student Framework](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-students).

### Three Progression Levels (Students)

The student framework uses the progression sequence **Understand → Apply → Create**:

- **Understand** — Students gain foundational knowledge about what AI is, how it works, and its societal implications. Focus is on conceptual literacy and critical awareness.
- **Apply** — Students use AI tools appropriately in educational and real-world contexts, exercising informed judgment about when and how to deploy AI.
- **Create** — Students design, build, or innovate using AI systems, integrating technical and ethical thinking to generate novel solutions.

(Level descriptor wording synthesized from the draft framework — [UNESCO Draft Frameworks PDF](https://www.unesco.org/sites/default/files/medias/fichiers/2024/04/UNESCO-Draft-AI-competency-frameworks-for-teachers-and-school-students.pdf).)

The framework "encourages integrating AI-related topics into core subjects across the curriculum, emphasizing interdisciplinary learning in both STEM and the social studies" — [UNESCO Student page](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-students). It is available in Arabic, English, French, Portuguese, Spanish, and Vietnamese — [unesdoc.unesco.org](https://unesdoc.unesco.org/ark:/48223/pf0000391105).

---

## Teacher Framework (AI Competency Framework for Teachers)

**Structure:** 5 dimensions × 3 progression levels = 15 competency blocks — [UNESCO Teacher Framework](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-teachers).

### Five Dimensions

1. **Human-centred mindset** — "Defines the critical values and attitudes necessary for interactions between humans and AI-based systems that teachers should promote" — [CIDDL Summary](https://ciddl.org/summary-of-unesco-ai-competency-framework-for-teachers/).

2. **AI Ethics** — "Establishes essential ethical principles, along with institutional laws, regulations and rules that teachers need to understand, apply and adapt" — [CIDDL Summary](https://ciddl.org/summary-of-unesco-ai-competency-framework-for-teachers/).

3. **AI Foundations and Applications** — "Specifies the transferable knowledge and skills teachers need to select, apply, and personalise AI tools for teaching and learning environments, fostering creativity" — [CIDDL Summary](https://ciddl.org/summary-of-unesco-ai-competency-framework-for-teachers/).

4. **AI Pedagogy** — Defined as "a set of competencies required for purposeful and effective AI–pedagogy integration. This covers the ability to validate and select proper AI tools and to integrate them into pedagogical strategies to support course preparation, teaching, learning, socialization, social caring, and learning assessment" — [AI Competency Frameworks search results](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-teachers).

5. **AI for Professional Development** — "Details the competencies teachers must develop to use AI in their ongoing professional learning" — [CIDDL Summary](https://ciddl.org/summary-of-unesco-ai-competency-framework-for-teachers/).

### Three Progression Levels (Teachers)

The teacher framework uses the sequence **Acquire → Deepen → Create**:

- **Acquire** — "The overall curricular goal in the 'Acquire' level is to support all teachers to reach a basic level of AI competency or literacy required by the teaching profession across varied contexts. Establishes the essential set of AI competencies all teachers need to evaluate, select, and appropriately use AI tools" — [UNESCO Teacher Framework search results](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-teachers).

- **Deepen** — "Defines intermediate competencies required to design pedagogical strategies and teaching materials that integrate AI in the classroom. At the Deepen level, educators should be able to integrate AI into their pedagogical practices and critically assess its impact on student learning" — [UNESCO Teacher Framework search results](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-teachers).

- **Create** — "Establishes advanced competencies for creatively configuring AI systems and innovatively using them in education. At the Create level, teachers should use AI to innovate their teaching, designing new approaches to learning that fully leverage AI's capabilities" — [UNESCO Teacher Framework search results](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-teachers).

---

## Adoption and Implementation

UNESCO describes the frameworks as "the first globally standardized instruments for defining and measuring AI competencies in education," targeting all 193+ UN member states and potentially impacting approximately 1.5 billion students and 75 million educators — [IAU-HESD](https://www.iau-hesd.net/action/unesco-ai-competency-frameworks-students-and-teachers).

The stated rollout plan follows three phases: Phase 1 (2024–2025): global dissemination and initial policy adoption; Phase 2 (2025–2027): integration into national curricula and teacher training rollouts; Phase 3 (2027–2030): full-scale implementation and monitoring through formal assessment mechanisms — [AI4edu](https://ai4edu.eu/2024/11/12/unescos-ai-competency-frameworks-equipping-educators-and-students-for-the-age-of-ai/).

The UNESCO project "will guide efforts of Member States in the formulation of national frameworks and institutional programmes on digital and AI competencies" through a series of international seminars — [UNESCO G77 competencies page](https://www.unesco.org/en/digital-education/g77-competencies). Cedefop (EU VET agency) has already republished the teacher framework as a reference resource — [Cedefop](https://www.cedefop.europa.eu/en/tools/vet-toolkit-tackling-early-leaving/resources/unesco-ai-competency-framework-teachers), signalling uptake in European vocational education contexts. Prior to the frameworks' launch, "only seven countries had developed AI frameworks or programmes for teachers" — [What you need to know](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/what-you-need-know-about-unescos-new-ai-competency-frameworks-students-and-teachers).

UNESCO-ICHEI (the International Centre for Higher Education Innovation) engaged with both frameworks as part of its 2024 milestone activities — [ICHEI 2024 Milestones](https://en.ichei.org/en/news/information/897.html). No specific national government assessments or certification systems built directly on these frameworks were confirmed in sources retrieved (as of April 2026).

## Inferred Findings

**The student and teacher frameworks are intentionally mirrored but not identical in their level language.** The student framework uses "Understand / Apply / Create" — terminology aligned with Bloom's Taxonomy — while the teacher framework uses "Acquire / Deepen / Create," reflecting a professional development logic (competency acquisition, pedagogical integration, innovation). This suggests UNESCO deliberately aligned the student framework with curriculum design conventions and the teacher framework with CPD/professional learning traditions.

- Based on: [UNESCO Student Framework](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-students) (Understand/Apply/Create confirmed); [UNESCO Teacher Framework](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-teachers) (Acquire/Deepen/Create confirmed); [CIDDL Summary](https://ciddl.org/summary-of-unesco-ai-competency-framework-for-teachers/).
- Reasoning: The parallel structure (same 3-level design, same top-level "Create" outcome, different intermediate terminology) is purposeful rather than coincidental — matching each framework to the audience's domain conventions.

**Formal country-level adoption is likely still in early stages.** The lack of confirmed national assessment implementations (as of searches in April 2026) despite a Phase 1 (2024–2025) rollout plan suggests that most countries are still in the adaptation and translation phase rather than formal assessment deployment.

- Based on: [AI4edu rollout plan](https://ai4edu.eu/2024/11/12/unescos-ai-competency-frameworks-equipping-educators-and-students-for-the-age-of-ai/); [UNESCO G77 competencies page](https://www.unesco.org/en/digital-education/g77-competencies).
- Reasoning: UNESCO frameworks typically function as reference tools adopted voluntarily and adapted nationally; the three-phase timeline itself implies that formal assessment (Phase 3) was not expected before 2027.

# Prompt 03: national-ai-skills-frameworks

## UK: Skills England / DSIT AI Skills Framework

The UK has produced one of the most structured national AI workforce frameworks to date, published through the Department for Science, Innovation and Technology (DSIT) and operationalised via Skills England.

The **AI Skills for Business Competency Framework** (currently at Version 2, with Version 3 in consultation as of January 2026) divides competencies across three tiers of worker:

- **AI Citizens** — members of the public or customers of organisations that use AI
- **AI Workers** — employees not primarily in data/AI roles but whose work is impacted by AI tools
- **AI Professionals** — employees whose core responsibilities centre on data and AI

"The framework categorises AI skills into: technical; responsible and ethical; and non-technical domains, aligned with three job levels." — [Skills England / GOV.UK](https://skillsengland.blog.gov.uk/2026/01/28/ai-skills-boost-skills-englands-ai-foundation-skills-for-work-benchmark-supports-free-ai-training-for-all-by-phil-smith/)

The framework is explicitly cumulative: "individuals at higher levels expected to possess strategic competencies and core skills found at lower levels." — [Skills England Blog](https://skillsengland.blog.gov.uk/2026/01/28/ai-skills-boost-skills-englands-ai-foundation-skills-for-work-benchmark-supports-free-ai-training-for-all-by-phil-smith/)

The **AI Foundation Skills for Work Benchmark** (published January 2026) establishes the baseline competency for all workers and includes: "understanding the risks and consequences of using AI, creating prompts and adapting settings for generative AI tools, and using simple AI dashboards to interpret data trends." — [Skills England Blog](https://skillsengland.blog.gov.uk/2026/01/28/ai-skills-boost-skills-englands-ai-foundation-skills-for-work-benchmark-supports-free-ai-training-for-all-by-phil-smith/)

On assessments and reach: "Free AI training for all, as government and industry programme expands to provide 10 million workers with key AI skills by 2030" — [GOV.UK press release](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/free-ai-training-for-all-as-government-and-industry-programme-expands-to-provide-10-million-workers-with-key-ai-skills-by-2030). The AI Skills Boost delivers free, industry-developed short courses online — [GOV.UK AI Skills Tools Package](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/ai-skills-for-the-uk-workforce/ai-skills-tools-package). Formal certification is not mandated at the foundational level; the benchmark functions as a self-assessment and course-completion benchmark rather than a proctored credential.

## Norway: KI-Norge (Digdir) and the AI Competency Landscape

Norway does not yet have a single national AI skills/competency framework equivalent to the UK model, but several interlocking initiatives define the emerging structure.

The Norwegian government is establishing **KI-Norge (AI Norway)** inside Digdir: "a national arena for innovative and responsible artificial intelligence... a driving force and advisory service, as well as serving as a link and partner between key AI players in the public sector, trade and industry, the research sector and academia." — [Regjeringen.no](https://www.regjeringen.no/en/whats-new/gjor-norge-klar-for-trygg-og-innovativ-ki-bruk/id3093081/)

On workforce competency, Digdir's existing digital transformation competency model identifies "fagkunnskap og ferdigheter" (subject knowledge and skills) as its third-tier competency layer, covering what organisations collectively need to succeed with digital transformation — [Digdir.no](https://www.digdir.no/digitalisering-og-samordning/fagkunnskap-og-ferdigheter-digital-transformasjon/3296). However, this model has not yet been extended into a standalone published AI competency framework with defined skill levels and assessments.

The EU AI Act is driving Norway toward mandatory AI literacy requirements: "Fra februar 2025 stilles krav til kompetanse (AI literacy)" — [Deloitte NO regulatory update](https://www.deloitte.com/no/no/services/legal/perspectives/ki-regulatorisk-oppdatering-for-norge-oktober-2025.html) — meaning from February 2025, AI literacy requirements under the EU AI Act apply in Norway via the EEA agreement. A Norwegian implementing law was put to consultation on 30 June 2025, targeting entry into force in 2026. "Den største utfordringen for å ta i bruk KI som nevnes var mangel på kompetanse" (the biggest challenge cited for adopting AI was lack of competency) — [NTNU/NIKT study 2025](https://www.ntnu.no/ojs/index.php/nikt/article/download/6548/5834/25565).

No national Norwegian AI certification programme has been publicly announced as of April 2026.

## US: NIST AI RMF, Executive Orders, and Workforce Initiatives

The US approach is multi-strand rather than a single published skills framework.

**NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0)** is the foundational reference document: "intended for voluntary use and to improve the ability to incorporate trustworthiness considerations into the design, development, use, and evaluation of AI products, services, and systems." — [NIST AI RMF](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework). It defines core AI governance functions (Map, Measure, Manage, Govern) and associated competency expectations, but does not prescribe a workforce skills taxonomy directly.

**NIST AI 600-1** (generative AI profile, 2024) extends the RMF to generative AI risks and identifies 12 generative AI risk areas, each implying specific practitioner competencies — [NIST AI Congressional Mandates](https://www.nist.gov/artificial-intelligence/ai-congressional-mandates-executive-orders-and-actions).

The Trump administration's **Executive Order on AI in Education** (April 23, 2025) initiated the White House Task Force on Artificial Intelligence in Education. A proposed AI Education Framework would "establish a standardized approach to AI education and certification programs, guide curriculum development... assist federal workforce hiring to set requirements for AI-related roles and train staff, and set terminology around specialty areas, work roles, knowledge, skills, abilities, and tasks." — [NICCS/CISA](https://niccs.cisa.gov/training/catalog/cis/certified-nist-ai-risk-management-framework-10-architect-nist-ai-rmf-10-architect)

The **America's AI Action Plan** (July 2025) names NIST in multiple policy recommendations — [White House AI Action Plan PDF](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Americas-AI-Action-Plan.pdf).

**Certifications** exist but are industry-led, not government-mandated: Certified NIST AI RMF 1.0 Architect from Certified Information Security is listed on CISA's NICCS catalogue: "validates competence and understanding for developing and managing AI risk-management based upon the NIST Artificial Intelligence Risk Management Framework 1.0." — [NICCS](https://niccs.cisa.gov/training/catalog/cis/certified-nist-ai-risk-management-framework-10-architect-nist-ai-rmf-10-architect)

## (b) Top 3 Frameworks for Non-Technical Professional Audience

**Cited Finding** (Prompts 1, 2, 3):

1. **DigComp 3.0 (European Commission)** — The most comprehensive reference standard for general workforce AI literacy. Its 21 competences and 500+ learning outcomes are technology-neutral and cover everyday AI use, critical evaluation, privacy, and safety. It is directly relevant to EU AI Act Art. 4 compliance. It requires no prior technical background and applies to all workers, not just tech roles.

2. **UK Skills England AI Framework** — The most operationally mature national framework for professional audiences. Its three-tier structure (Citizens / Workers / Professionals) maps cleanly to organisational role hierarchies. The Foundation Skills Benchmark (live Jan 2026) provides an immediately usable assessment tool. The KSB descriptor format is familiar to HR and L&D professionals.

3. **UNESCO Teacher/Student AI Framework (Teacher track)** — Best fit for organisations in education or public sector, or any programme designing AI literacy curricula. Its three-level progression (Acquire / Deepen / Create) is intuitive for programme designers and maps to Bloom's Taxonomy logic. Useful as a curriculum design scaffold even outside formal education.

## (c) Top 3 Certifications for Technical Practitioner Audience

**Cited Finding** (Prompts 4, 5, 6, 7):

1. **Microsoft AI-102 (Azure AI Engineer Associate)** — The broadest technically rigorous vendor certification, covering six substantive domains including NLP, computer vision, generative AI, and responsible AI. Its 100-minute scenario-based format tests applied judgment, not just recall. Wide enterprise recognition and Azure market share make it the de facto benchmark for cloud AI engineers.

2. **Google PMLE (Professional ML Engineer)** — The most technically demanding of the cloud vendor exams, requiring 3+ years of GCP experience. Its 2-hour proctored format and depth on MLOps, model evaluation, and deployment pipelines make it a credible signal of practitioner-level competence. Relevant for organisations on the Google Cloud stack.

3. **edX MicroMasters (MIT / Columbia)** — The most academically credentialed pathway for practitioners seeking depth. Transferable to full master's degrees and grounded in peer-reviewed curricula. Best suited for practitioners who need theoretical grounding (e.g., AI architects, research engineers) rather than platform-specific skills.

**Honourable mention:** NVIDIA Professional exams are technically deep and growing in relevance as LLM inference infrastructure becomes a practitioner concern, but ecosystem breadth is currently narrower.

## (d) Top 3 Gaps in the Current Landscape

**Inferred Finding** (Prompts 1–7, synthesised):

### Gap 1: Cross-Vendor, Platform-Neutral Technical Certification
All rigorous technical certifications are vendor-locked (Azure, GCP, AWS, NVIDIA, watsonx). There is no vendor-neutral, proctored, industry-recognised certification for AI engineering competence analogous to CompTIA Security+ in cybersecurity. DeepLearning.AI and DataCamp partially fill this space but lack formal accreditation and employer mandates. *Reasoning: Every major tech cert reviewed is tied to a proprietary cloud or platform stack; no neutral body (ISACA, CompTIA, IEEE) yet offers a widely adopted AI practitioner credential.*

### Gap 2: Agentic AI and Human-AI Teaming Competencies
No existing framework — regulatory, national, or vendor — comprehensively addresses competencies for working alongside autonomous AI agents: task delegation, oversight calibration, output verification, and recovery from agent failure. NVIDIA's Agentic AI exams are nascent. UNESCO and DigComp predate widespread agentic deployment. AWS GenAI Professional is in beta. *Reasoning: Agentic AI systems are now in production at scale (as of 2025–26), yet no framework reviewed contains structured descriptors for the human oversight role in agentic workflows.*

### Gap 3: AI Governance and Ethics for Middle Management / Operational Roles
Regulatory frameworks (EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF) address organisational governance but do not translate into personal competency descriptors for the managers, product owners, and operational leads who make day-to-day decisions about AI deployment. DigComp 3.0 covers citizen-level safety and ethics; NIST RMF addresses enterprise governance. The practitioner-level middle ground — what does a procurement manager or team lead need to know to govern AI responsibly? — is not covered by any certification or framework reviewed. *Reasoning: Both technical certs (vendor-specific, too deep) and regulatory frameworks (organisational, not personal) leave this role tier unserved; UK Skills England's "AI Workers" tier hints at it but KSB descriptors are not yet assessment-backed.*

## Inferred Findings

**IF-1: DigComp 3.0 is the effective de facto standard for EU AI Act Art. 4 compliance programmes.**
Based on: Prompt 1 (EU AI Act Art. 4 mandates AI literacy; no prescriptive curriculum specified) + Prompt 1 (DigComp 3.0 is voluntary reference standard from same publisher, European Commission).
Reasoning: With no mandated curriculum, compliance programmes will default to the most authoritative available reference from the same regulatory body. DigComp 3.0's explicit AI embedding across all competences makes it the natural mapping tool.

**IF-2: The 2026 Microsoft cert refresh wave will temporarily reduce market clarity for hiring managers using cert requirements.**
Based on: Prompt 4 (2026 refresh wave replacing all core AI certs) + Prompt 4 (AI-102 and DP-100 are current benchmarks for technical hiring).
Reasoning: When widely-used certs are retired or restructured, job descriptions and HR benchmarks lag. Organisations using cert requirements as hiring filters will face a 12–18 month period of ambiguity, increasing the value of framework-based (DigComp, UK Skills England) competency definitions as stable alternatives.

**IF-3: No existing framework is ready for immediate use as an organisational AI literacy assessment standard without customisation.**
Based on: Prompt 1 (DigComp voluntary, no assessment tool), Prompt 2 (UNESCO no national implementations confirmed), Prompt 3 (UK Foundation Benchmark live but national scope only), Prompts 4–7 (all vendor certs platform-specific or not framework-mapped).
Reasoning: Each framework has either an assessment mechanism without portability, or broad applicability without an assessment mechanism. Organisations designing AI literacy programmes must currently combine elements from multiple sources.
