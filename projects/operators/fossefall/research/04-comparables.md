# Comparable Operators: Fossefall
## Date: 2026-03-31

### Why These Comparables

Fossefall is a finance-first AI-HPC data center startup with ~NOK 60M seed capital, 6-7 employees, a 500 MW target by 2030, and an SPV project-finance structure. The comparables below were selected to illuminate the specific risks and patterns relevant to Fossefall's profile:

1. **Kolos** — Cautionary tale: a Norwegian DC startup with mega-capacity ambitions, finance-first team, and catastrophic execution failure.
2. **Nscale / Aker** — The dominant Norwegian AI-DC player; directly relevant as a competitor, talent competitor, and execution benchmark (Fossefall's VP Construction was poached from Nscale).
3. **Bulk Infrastructure** — A Norwegian DC developer that successfully grew from a one-man startup to a PE-backed platform; illustrates what "good" looks like over a 15-year trajectory.
4. **Green Mountain** — A Norwegian DC startup that grew from a family-office-backed small team to an $850M exit; demonstrates the patient-capital path.
5. **Verne (formerly Verne Global)** — Nordic AI/HPC-focused DC operator with multiple ownership transitions; shows how PE ownership shapes execution and growth.

---

### Comparable Operators

#### 1. Kolos (Norway) — The Cautionary Tale

- **Profile:** Norwegian-American startup launched in 2017 with plans to build the "world's largest data center" — a 1,000 MW, 600,000 sqm colocation facility in Ballangen, Northern Norway. Finance-first team with grand vision but no operational track record.

- **Similarities to Fossefall:**
  - Ambitious MW targets vastly exceeding team capacity and available capital
  - Finance/marketing-first leadership with limited DC construction experience
  - Norwegian location pitch built on cheap renewable power and cool climate
  - Heavy reliance on fundraising that had not yet materialized
  - Small team with no visible PM, procurement, or construction management capability

- **Challenges faced:**
  - **Massive fundraising gap:** Pledged to raise $250M but raised only $2.5M in 2017 — a 99% shortfall
  - **No execution capability:** Project details were "light" and it was "never clear just how real the proposal was" ([DCD](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/hive-blockchain-sells-kolos-subsidiary-to-local-norwegian-municipality-at-a-substantial-loss/))
  - **Internal disputes:** Company directors fell out over the development direction ([Nordic Labour Journal](http://www.nordiclabourjournal.org/i-fokus/in-focus-2023/theme-nordic-infrastructure/article.2023-04-25.6991080087))
  - **Sold at fire-sale prices:** Sold to Hive Blockchain for $9.9M in 2018. Hive subsequently wrote down the asset to $0 and paid the municipality of Narvik $200K to take the shares in 2021
  - **Nothing was ever built** on the 64-hectare site

- **Lessons for Fossefall:**
  - Grand MW targets without proportional capital and team are a red flag to sophisticated investors and customers
  - Finance-first teams that cannot demonstrate construction execution credibility will struggle to close funding rounds
  - Internal alignment on strategy is critical — founder disputes killed Kolos before market conditions did
  - **Key difference:** Fossefall has a stronger start than Kolos: real seed capital (~NOK 60M vs. Kolos's $2.5M), an anchor customer (Seekr), an acquired operational site (Fyresdal), and a VP Construction hired from Nscale. But the gap between ambition (500 MW) and resources (6-7 people, ~NOK 60M) echoes the Kolos pattern

#### 2. Nscale / Aker (Norway) — The Dominant Competitor

- **Profile:** Spun out of crypto-mining firm Arkon Energy in 2024, Nscale has become Europe's largest AI infrastructure company. Backed by Aker ASA, with partnerships with OpenAI and Microsoft. Raised $1.1B Series B (September 2025), followed by a $2B Series C, reaching a $14.6B valuation. "Stargate Norway" in Narvik targets 230 MW expandable to 520 MW. ~354 employees.

- **Similarities to Fossefall:**
  - AI-HPC focused data center developer in Norway
  - Targets massive MW capacity (1.3 GW pipeline)
  - Originally pivoted from a different business (crypto → AI)
  - Relies on renewable Norwegian hydropower as competitive advantage

- **Challenges faced:**
  - **Zoning and power grid delays:** The Korgen facility is stalled waiting for Statnett power allocation due to delays in zoning plans
  - **Execution pressure:** "Any delay or cost overrun on Stargate Norway would directly challenge the financial model underpinning its $14.6 billion valuation"
  - **GPU supply bottlenecks:** NVIDIA allocation constraints can add 12-24 months to timelines
  - **Construction timeline risk:** Must deliver 100,000 GPUs operational by end of 2026 — extremely aggressive
  - **Talent competition:** Rapid scaling from ~120 to 354 employees, with Fossefall already poaching from their ranks

- **Lessons for Fossefall:**
  - Even with $4.68B in total funding and Aker's industrial backing, Nscale faces grid connection and zoning delays — Fossefall will face the same
  - The Norwegian power grid queue is massive: 96 data centers waiting, requesting over 7,000 MW total
  - Having an experienced DC construction leader is table stakes — Fossefall has one VP Construction
  - Nscale's partnership model (50/50 JV with Aker, off-take from OpenAI) de-risks capital; Fossefall's SPV model with angel investors is far more fragile
  - **Competitive threat:** Nscale competes directly for the same power allocations, sites, talent, and customers in Norway

#### 3. Bulk Infrastructure (Norway) — The Successful Trajectory

- **Profile:** Founded in 2006 by Peder Nærbø (financial advisor background) as a logistics real estate company. Pivoted to data centers in 2011. Now operates the N01 campus in Kristiansand (up to 2 GW potential), Oslo campus, and DK01 in Denmark. ~90 employees. PE-backed by BentallGreenOak (>€640M total equity invested). Recent CoreWeave partnership for one of Europe's largest NVIDIA AI deployments.

- **Similarities to Fossefall:**
  - Founded by someone from finance/investment background
  - Started small and grew into DC sector
  - Norwegian company leveraging renewable energy advantage
  - PE/institutional capital model

- **Challenges faced:**
  - **Slow initial ramp:** Construction at N01 began in 2014, but operational data centers were only established circa 2020 — a 6-year development period before revenue
  - **Capital intensity:** Required two major funding rounds from BGO (€140M in 2020, €350M in 2024) plus existing shareholders — over €1B in total capex planned by 2026
  - **Power grid expansion:** Working with regional and national grid companies to add 300 MW of additional capacity — a multi-year process
  - **Team scaling:** Only ~30 construction FTEs per year at N01, plus 15 operations staff

- **Lessons for Fossefall:**
  - The Bulk trajectory shows it takes 5-10 years to go from startup to meaningful MW operational — Fossefall's 2030 target for 500 MW requires executing faster than Bulk did with far more resources
  - A finance-background founder can succeed in DC, but Nærbø spent 5 years in logistics real estate (construction-adjacent) before entering DC
  - PE capital comes in stages tied to milestones — Fossefall's NOK 60M seed must fund enough progress to unlock the next round
  - Bulk has 90 employees to manage its portfolio — Fossefall has 6-7 for comparable ambitions
  - **The BGO relationship took 14 years to develop**

#### 4. Green Mountain (Norway) — The Patient-Capital Success

- **Profile:** Founded in 2009 by the Smedvig family (Norwegian oil/shipping dynasty). Built DC1 in a former NATO ammunition store inside a mountain near Stavanger. Grew to three sites totaling ~24 MW operational. Acquired by Israeli real estate firm Azrieli Group for $850M in 2021. Now expanding aggressively under Azrieli (€1B+ new investment, TikTok as major customer).

- **Similarities to Fossefall:**
  - Norwegian DC startup that started with family/private capital
  - Small initial team
  - Leveraged Norway's renewable energy and unique physical assets
  - Eventually attracted large institutional capital

- **Challenges faced:**
  - **Slow organic growth:** It took 12 years (2009-2021) to reach ~24 MW operational before the Azrieli acquisition
  - **Limited MW scale pre-acquisition**
  - **Remote location challenges**
  - **Market education:** Had to spend years convincing international customers that Norway was a viable DC location

- **Lessons for Fossefall:**
  - Patient capital from a family office allowed Green Mountain to build slowly and get it right — Fossefall's angel/HNW investors may not have the same patience
  - Green Mountain had operational excellence (Tier III certification) as its calling card — Fossefall has no operational track record yet
  - The $850M exit validates the Norwegian DC thesis, but it took 12 years
  - Green Mountain had ~77 employees for ~24 MW operational; Fossefall has 6-7 targeting 500 MW

#### 5. Verne (Iceland/Finland/UK) — The PE Ownership Journey

- **Profile:** Founded 2007-2012, originally as Verne Global in Iceland on a former NATO airbase. AI/HPC-focused from early on. Acquired by Digital 9 Infrastructure in 2021 for £231M, then sold to PE firm Ardian in 2023 for up to $575M. Ardian committed up to $1.2B for expansion. Now operates 70 MW live capacity with 350 MW in development. ~108 employees.

- **Similarities to Fossefall:**
  - AI/HPC-focused from early days
  - Nordic location leveraging renewable energy and cool climate
  - Multiple ownership transitions reflecting capital needs

- **Challenges faced:**
  - **Multiple ownership changes:** Each transition brought strategy shifts and management disruption
  - **Power competition:** Competing with synthetic fuel, hydrogen production for Iceland's renewable power
  - **Scale limitations:** After 15+ years, still at 70 MW operational

- **Lessons for Fossefall:**
  - Even purpose-built AI/HPC operators took 15+ years to reach 70 MW operational
  - Verne has 108 employees for 70 MW live + 350 MW development — team-to-MW ratio indicates Fossefall is at least 10x understaffed
  - **Ardian's $1.2B commitment** came only after Verne had 15+ years of operational track record

---

### Common Patterns

1. **The timeline gap is real:** Successful operators took 5-15 years to reach meaningful operational MW. Kolos (the failure) promised massive scale immediately.

2. **Power grid access is the binding constraint in Norway:** 96 data centers queued with Statnett for over 7,000 MW total. Even Nscale with Aker backing faces grid delays. New regulations (ekomloven, datasenterforskriften from January 2025) add process requirements.

3. **Finance-first founders can succeed — but only with deep execution teams:** Bulk's Nærbø succeeded by building construction capability first. Green Mountain's Smedvig family hired experienced operators. Kolos failed precisely because they never built execution capability.

4. **Team size scales with MW — there are no shortcuts:** Green Mountain: ~77 employees for ~24 MW. Bulk: ~90 employees. Verne: ~108 for 70 MW. Nscale: ~354 for 60 MW + pipeline. Fossefall: 6-7 for 500 MW target.

5. **Institutional capital requires operational proof points:** BGO invested in Bulk after 14 years. Azrieli bought Green Mountain after 12 years. Ardian bought Verne after 15+ years.

6. **The SPV/project-finance model adds complexity:** Each site financed separately means each site needs its own due diligence, lender relationships, and project management.

7. **Construction expertise is non-negotiable:** Nscale hired Alex Sharp (54 data centers built). Bulk has 30+ construction FTEs annually.

---

### Implications for Fossefall

1. **Kolos risk is the existential risk:** Fossefall's profile rhymes uncomfortably with Kolos. Critical differentiators: Fossefall has acquired a site, has an anchor customer, and hired VP Construction from Nscale. But the gap between 500 MW ambition and 6-7 person team is the single biggest risk factor. **Metier opportunity: Help Fossefall avoid the Kolos pattern by providing the execution framework they cannot build in-house fast enough.**

2. **Power grid queue is the strategic bottleneck:** With 96 data centers waiting for Statnett allocations totaling 7,000+ MW, Fossefall's 11+ site pipeline will face multi-year grid connection timelines. **Metier opportunity: Power infrastructure planning and grid connection strategy across the SPV portfolio.**

3. **Team scaling is urgent and competitive:** Every comparable demonstrates that meaningful DC development requires 50-100+ employees. Fossefall is competing with Nscale, Bulk, Green Mountain, and others for the same small pool of Norwegian DC construction talent. **Metier opportunity: Provide interim PM, construction management, and procurement capacity while Fossefall hires.**

4. **SPV structure multiplies execution complexity:** No comparable operator uses an SPV-per-site model at this scale with this small a team. Each SPV needs its own project controls, cost management, contractor management, and investor reporting. **Metier opportunity: Standardized project execution framework across SPVs, including reporting templates for investors.**

5. **The proof-of-concept site matters enormously:** Fyresdal (5.5 MW expandable to 18 MW) is Fossefall's opportunity to demonstrate execution credibility — analogous to Green Mountain's DC1 or Bulk's first N01 building. Success here unlocks institutional capital. **Metier opportunity: Concentrate execution support on Fyresdal to create the track record that unlocks subsequent funding rounds.**
