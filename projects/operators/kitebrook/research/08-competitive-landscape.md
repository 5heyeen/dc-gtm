# Competitive Landscape: Kitebrook
## Date: 2026-04-08

### Why These Comparables
Selected based on: (1) geographic overlap with Kitebrook in Vestland/western Norway, (2) similar scale (50-200 MW greenfield), (3) similar stage (development/pre-construction), and (4) similar ownership pattern (privately held developers).

### Comparable Operators

#### 1. ASP Data Center (Direct Geographic Competitor)
- **Profile:** Stavanger-based, established 2022 by ASP Eiendom AS (property enterprise operating since 2008). CEO: Ole Fredrik Bergseth. Privately-owned Norwegian company developing sustainable DCs in southwestern Norway.
- **Sites:** Dale Fabrikker (Vaksdal, Vestland — 48,500 m², up to 90 MW), Suldal (25 MW, acquired Jan 2026). Multiple facilities across SW Norway.
- **Similarities to Kitebrook:** Property developer entering DC market; greenfield/brownfield sites in Vestland county; hydropower-focused; privately held.
- **Differences:** More aggressive acquisition pace (2 sites in 6 months). Brownfield conversion model (existing buildings) vs Kitebrook's greenfield approach. Appears more active in execution.
- **Key lesson:** ASP is moving faster despite similar scale — Kitebrook's prolonged development timeline (Bordalen zoned since 2019, still no construction) risks losing competitive position in the Bergen area.
- **Source:** [DCD](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/norways-asp-data-center-acquires-former-factory-site-outside-bergen/), [BeBeez](https://bebeez.eu/2025/09/22/norways-asp-data-center-acquires-former-factory-site-outside-bergen/)

#### 2. Lefdal Mine Datacenter (Vestland — Operational Competitor)
- **Profile:** Large-scale DC in a former mine in Måløy, Vestland county. Operational since ~2017. Scaling from 10 MW to 200 MW in phases. Partners include ABB, IBM, Rittal.
- **Similarities:** Vestland location, hydropower, large-scale ambitions (200 MW).
- **Differences:** Already operational. Underground/mine conversion vs greenfield. Has established technology partnerships (ABB, Rittal). Located further north (Sogn og Fjordane).
- **Challenges faced:** Remote location (hour flight from Oslo + ferry), continuous construction while operational, power supply scaling as main constraint.
- **Key lesson:** Even with an operational facility and established partners, scaling to 200 MW is a multi-year challenge driven primarily by power supply. Kitebrook should expect grid connection to be the primary bottleneck.
- **Source:** [DCD](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/analysis/data-mining-lefdal-mine-datacenter/), [Business Norway](https://businessnorway.com/solutions/leftdal-mine-datacenter-large-scale-data-centre-in-a-deep-norwegian-mine)

#### 3. Bulk Infrastructure (National Competitor — Established Operator)
- **Profile:** Norway's largest DC developer. Multiple sites in Norway and Denmark. Established operator scaling up significantly. Well-funded with institutional backing.
- **Similarities:** Norwegian DC developer with large-scale ambitions.
- **Differences:** Far larger organization, established operational history, institutional funding, multiple operational sites. Not a direct peer comparison — Bulk is where Kitebrook might aspire to be in 10+ years.
- **Key lesson:** Bulk's success was built on a large internal team with PM capability, procurement frameworks, and operational processes. Kitebrook's lean model is the opposite approach.
- **Source:** [Bulk Infrastructure](https://bulkinfrastructure.com/)

#### 4. Google (Hyperscale — Context)
- **Profile:** EUR 600M hyperscale DC in Norway, operational from 2026. One of the largest in the world.
- **Relevance:** Not a competitor but context — hyperscale demand in Norway validates Kitebrook's thesis. Google's entry signals market maturity.
- **Key lesson:** Google's investment in Norway proves the market for large-scale, hydro-powered DC capacity. Kitebrook is positioning to serve similar demand from hyperscalers who don't want to build their own facilities.
- **Source:** [DLA Piper](https://www.dlapiper.com/en-us/insights/publications/2024/05/construction-of-googles-eur600-million-hyperscale-data-centre-in-norway)

### Western Norway DC Competition

The Bergen/Vestland area has **3 main development areas** being marketed by Greenbyte/Invest in Bergen:
1. **Børdalen, Samnanger** — Kitebrook's site (100-200 MW)
2. **Matre, Masfjorden** — Kitebrook's second site (30 MW)
3. **Dale Fabrikker, Vaksdal** — Now acquired by ASP Data Center (up to 90 MW)

All 1,200 MW of grid capacity in the Bergen area transmission grid is currently reserved. Competition for grid capacity is intense.

### Common Patterns & Challenges for Norwegian DC Developers

| Challenge | Details | Relevance to Kitebrook |
|-----------|---------|----------------------|
| **Grid connection queues** | 8,500 MW reserved nationally. Queues add 12-18 months to project schedules. | HIGH — Kitebrook has 130 MW queued but Statnett infrastructure upgrades are prerequisites. |
| **Construction cost escalation** | Norwegian construction wages rose 5.7% in 2024, outpacing CPI. | HIGH — Every year of delay increases costs. Bordalen in planning since 2019. |
| **Skilled labor shortage** | Nordic DC construction sector faces shortage of specialized workers. | HIGH — Kitebrook's remote western Norway locations compound this. |
| **Harsh winter construction** | Temperatures to -30°C, humidity challenges, seasonal construction constraints. | MEDIUM — Vestland is milder than northern Norway, but still a factor. |
| **Regulatory uncertainty** | Proposed DC concession system (March 2026 parliament debate). | HIGH — Could add permitting complexity for all developers including Kitebrook. |
| **Remote site logistics** | Rural/fjord locations create construction logistics challenges. | MEDIUM — Both Bordalen and Matre are in relatively remote locations. |

### Implications for Kitebrook

1. **ASP Data Center is a direct geographic rival** moving faster on site acquisition in Vestland. Kitebrook risks losing first-mover advantage at Bordalen if they don't accelerate.

2. **Grid capacity is the binding constraint.** All 1,200 MW in Bergen area is reserved. Kitebrook's 130 MW is secured, but the physical grid upgrades (Samnanger transformer, new power lines) will determine actual delivery timeline. This is not something more PM can fix — it's infrastructure dependency.

3. **Construction cost escalation punishes delay.** With wages rising 5.7% annually, Kitebrook's prolonged pre-development phase (2019→2026 with no construction) has a real financial cost. Professional PM can compress the construction timeline once started.

4. **Every comparable that succeeded had significant PM capability** — Bulk has a large internal team, Lefdal partnered with ABB from the start, Google uses major EPC contractors. The "lean developer with no PM" model has no successful precedent at 100+ MW scale.
