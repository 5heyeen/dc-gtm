## 1. Company Snapshot

Kitebrook is the new data center platform of **J. Byrne Murphy** and **Bill Conway** — the duo who co-founded DigiPlex in 2001 and built it into Scandinavia's largest DC operator before selling to IPI Partners (now STACK Infrastructure) in August 2021. Kitebrook Partners LLC, based in Washington DC, launched its Nordic DC platform in 2024 targeting AI-enabled data centers powered by Norwegian hydropower in "100+ MW tranches."

The Norwegian operating entity, **Kitebrook AS** (org. 917 810 214, Nesttun/Bergen), was originally founded in 2016 as REGN Datacenters by Per Espen Edvardsen. It holds 3-4 subsidiaries including project vehicles for Bordalen (Samnanger, 100-200 MW) and Matre (Masfjorden, 30 MW). A third entity, Kitebrook Sogn Datasenter AS, was established in 2020 for a Sogn region project. Total pipeline: **130 MW of grid capacity** reserved across 2 confirmed sites. Estimated project value for Bordalen alone: **~NOK 1 billion**.

Despite this ambitious pipeline, Kitebrook has **no operational data centers, no sites under construction, and only 1-4 employees in Norway** as of April 2026. The company is at the critical inflection point between land banking and active development — ground surveys were completed at Matre in mid-2025, and Bordalen has been zoned since 2019 but awaits construction.

## 3. Funding & Investor Context

### Capital Structure
- **Total funding:** Undisclosed. Self-funded by principals (Murphy/Conway), likely including DigiPlex exit proceeds.
- **Key investors:** J. Byrne Murphy and Bill Conway (personal/family capital). No external PE or institutional investors publicly identified.
- **Fund type:** Principal-led investment vehicle — NOT a fund. No LP obligations, no fund lifecycle.
- **JV structure:** Delaware entity "Kitebrook Energy Norway 1 JV LLC" suggests possible co-investors, but none identified.
- **Largest shareholder of Kitebrook AS:** Enrec Ltd (UK company 15775391, incorporated June 2024) — likely a Murphy-controlled holding vehicle.

### Investor-Driven Constraints
- **Timeline pressure:** LOW. No PE exit horizon. DigiPlex was held 20 years. Patient capital.
- **Governance overhead:** LOW. Board controlled by principals. No external institutional board seats.
- **ESG/sustainability mandates:** Self-imposed (hydropower, sustainability as commercial positioning), not LP-driven.
- **Milestone requirements:** No evidence of externally gated capital.

### What This Means for Metier
Unlike PE-backed operators, Kitebrook does NOT have funding-driven urgency. The pitch cannot rely on "your investors need milestone reporting" or "your PE timeline demands fast delivery." Instead, the pitch must focus on **execution capability gaps** and **competitive urgency** (ASP Data Center is moving faster in the same geography). The lack of external constraints is both an opportunity (no competing advisors imposed by investors) and a challenge (less urgency to engage external PM).

## 4. Strategic Context

**Segment:** Hyperscale/HPC data centers powered by sustainable hydropower. Targeting AI workloads.

**Strategy:** "DigiPlex 2.0" — Murphy is repeating the playbook that built Scandinavia's largest DC operator, but at larger scale (100+ MW tranches vs DigiPlex's more modest beginnings) and focused on AI-driven demand. The 2024 platform launch was timed to the AI infrastructure boom.

**Geographic focus:** Western Norway (Vestland county) — all 3 sites are within ~1 hour of Bergen. Hydropower-rich area. Website mentions UK and Sweden but no concrete non-Norwegian activity found.

**Timeline drivers:** Market-driven, not investor-driven. The AI demand surge creates commercial urgency — every month of delay is lost revenue opportunity. ASP Data Center's faster site acquisition in Vestland adds competitive pressure. The proposed Norwegian DC concession system (parliament debate March 2026) adds regulatory uncertainty.

## 6. Pain Points — Ranked by Urgency

### Pain Point 1: No Project Execution Capability for Imminent Construction
- **Evidence:** Zero job postings, only 4 identifiable people, no PM/engineering/construction staff, no EPC or engineering firm engaged, no Doffin tenders. Ground surveys at Matre completed 2025; Bordalen zoned since 2019. Transitioning from land banking to development with no execution team.
- **Urgency:** HIGH — Construction trigger (tenant commitment or board decision) could come at any time. Without PM capability, they cannot deliver.
- **Metier service:** Project Execution & PM (PRIMARY), Owner's Engineer / PMC (PRIMARY)
- **Confidence:** HIGH — Multiple independent signals confirm the gap conclusively.

### Pain Point 2: EPC/Contractor Procurement Without Internal Procurement Function
- **Evidence:** No procurement/contracts team, no EPC relationships established, no Doffin/TED tenders, ~NOK 1 billion project value at Bordalen. When construction begins, they must select and manage EPC contractors, design consultants, and equipment suppliers.
- **Urgency:** HIGH — Procurement decisions are the most consequential in DC construction. Contract packaging determines risk, schedule, and cost. Must happen before construction starts.
- **Metier service:** Contract, Procurement & Equipment Planning (PRIMARY)
- **Confidence:** HIGH — Confirmed by complete absence of procurement activity or personnel.

### Pain Point 3: Multi-Site Pre-Development Coordination
- **Evidence:** 3 project entities across 3 municipalities (Samnanger, Masfjorden, Sogn) with different grid operators, regulatory contexts, and stakeholders. Bordalen NVE application pending since 2020. Proposed DC concession system adds complexity. 1-4 employees managing it all.
- **Urgency:** MEDIUM-HIGH — Already relevant. Each site has different permitting status and grid timeline.
- **Metier service:** Early-Phase Advisory (PRIMARY), Portfolio & Project Owner Governance (SECONDARY)
- **Confidence:** MEDIUM — Multi-site complexity is clear; whether they're coping or struggling is not visible publicly.

## 7. Recommended Pitch

**Lead with: Owner's Engineer / PMC + Project Execution & PM**

The strongest opening angle is NOT "you need project managers" — Murphy built DigiPlex and knows what PM looks like. Instead, lead with **Owner's Engineer**: "As you move from development to construction, you need a trusted technical representative who can manage the interface with EPC contractors, review design deliverables, and protect your interests during construction — without building a large permanent team."

This resonates because:
1. It respects their expertise (they're the owner, we represent their interests)
2. It fits their lean model (they clearly intend to stay small — no hiring signals)
3. It addresses the most imminent gap (when construction starts, they need someone on their side of the table)
4. It's how DigiPlex operated in early days — Murphy will recognize the model

**Secondary lead:** Contract & Procurement advisory — frame as "getting the EPC procurement right the first time, because at NOK 1 billion, the cost of getting contract packaging wrong is enormous."

## 8. Talking Points

1. "We see you're at an exciting inflection point — zoned sites, secured grid capacity, and the AI demand wave hitting at exactly the right time. The next phase — going from approved sites to operating data centers — is where the execution complexity jumps. That's where we live."

2. "Your Bordalen project at ~NOK 1 billion is at the scale where contract packaging — how you split scopes between EPC contractors — fundamentally determines your risk profile. We help owners make those structuring decisions before going to tender."

3. "With 3 sites at different stages across different municipalities and grid contexts, you're effectively managing a portfolio. We help operators set up governance frameworks that give visibility across parallel development tracks — so nothing falls through the cracks."

4. "We understand you're not looking to build a 50-person internal team. Many of our DC clients use us as their Owner's Engineer — we provide the technical oversight and PM backbone so they can stay lean while still having full control over quality and schedule."

5. "ASP Data Center just acquired Dale Fabrikker in Vaksdal and land in Suldal. The Bergen area grid capacity is fully reserved. The competitive window for getting your sites to market is real — and professional PM is what compresses construction timelines."

## 9. Questions to Ask

1. "What's your current timeline expectation for breaking ground at Bordalen? What needs to happen before you start construction?" *(Validates urgency and identifies the construction trigger)*

2. "How are you thinking about the delivery model — full EPC turnkey, EPCM, or a multi-contract approach with an owner's representative managing the interfaces?" *(Reveals procurement strategy and where Metier fits)*

3. "With 3 sites at different stages, how are you prioritizing? Is Bordalen first, or could Matre move faster given the completed ground surveys?" *(Uncovers portfolio sequencing and where the immediate need is)*

4. "Are you working with any engineering or technical consultants yet for concept design or pre-FEED work?" *(Validates the partner gap finding — if they say no, the opportunity is confirmed)*

5. "How is Kjetil Ertnaes involved with Kitebrook? Is DLE/Grezya advising on the commercial or technical side?" *(Directly addresses the Ertnaes connection and maps the advisor landscape)*

6. "What's your view on the proposed DC concession system being debated in the Storting? Does that change your development timeline?" *(Shows awareness of regulatory landscape and opens a conversation about permitting support)*

7. "When you were building DigiPlex, what did the project delivery organization look like in the early years? Are you planning something similar here?" *(Leverages Murphy's experience to understand his preferred model)*

8. "The Bordalen NVE application was submitted in late 2020 — what's the status? Is permitting the main bottleneck?" *(Probes whether the delay is by choice or due to obstacles)*

## 10. Risks & Objections

- **"We know how to build data centers — we built DigiPlex."** This is the #1 objection. Response: "Absolutely — and that's exactly why we'd be effective. You know what good delivery looks like, which means you'll get more value from a professional PM partner than someone learning on the job. We're not here to teach you about data centers — we're here to provide the execution capacity your current team can't."

- **"We're not ready yet — we don't have a tenant/FID."** Possible. If they're deliberately staying lean until a tenant signs, Metier's pitch is premature. Counter: "Pre-construction advisory is most valuable before FID — it's cheaper to optimize the concept and procurement strategy now than to fix problems during construction."

- **"We prefer to keep things small and informal."** The zero-hiring signal suggests a philosophical commitment to staying lean. Metier must frame itself as enabling that model, not threatening it.

- **"We already have advisors."** Unknown advisors may exist that aren't publicly visible. Kjetil Ertnaes/DLE may already be advising. Ask before assuming a vacuum.

- **"We're self-funded — we don't need to justify PM costs to investors."** True. Without investor pressure, the decision to engage PM is purely value-driven. The pitch must demonstrate ROI: "Professional PM saves 10-15% on construction costs through better procurement and schedule management."
