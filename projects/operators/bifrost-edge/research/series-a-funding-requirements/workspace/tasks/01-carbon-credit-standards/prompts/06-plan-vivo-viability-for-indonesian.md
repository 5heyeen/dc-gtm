# Prompt 06: plan-vivo-viability-for-indonesian

> **Prompt:** What is Plan Vivo and is it a viable certification pathway for a small-to-medium reforestation startup in Indonesia?

---

## What Plan Vivo Is and What It Certifies

Plan Vivo, established in 1994, is one of the oldest voluntary carbon standards. Its flagship carbon programme — **PV Climate** — is explicitly designed for community-based and smallholder-led nature-based projects. Unlike VCS or Gold Standard, which accommodate large industrial or utility-scale projects, Plan Vivo's entire model is built around small-scale community stewards as the project implementers.

Eligible activities include: avoided deforestation, reforestation, afforestation, agroforestry, and blue carbon. The standard emphasises restoring native forest and reintroducing native species on degraded land. It does **not** currently certify projects outside forestry and blue carbon.

A defining structural requirement: **at least 60% of income from Plan Vivo Certificate (PVC) sales must flow back to the communities on the ground** — making it a community benefit-sharing standard as much as a carbon standard.

## Eligibility and Process

Eligibility for PV Climate requires demonstrating: (1) secure land tenure and user rights; (2) genuine community engagement and inclusion; (3) project activities that provide long-term increases in carbon storage and positive community impacts; and (4) third-party validation and ongoing verification. Projects must submit a **Project Idea Note (PIN)** as the first formal step, followed by development of a full **Project Design Document (PDD)** under the Plan Vivo Standard. All certificates are issued onto the **SPG Global registry** and are publicly visible.

The standard is designed for smaller, often fragmented landholdings aggregated across community members — making it a natural fit for Indonesia's smallholder reforestation context where individual plots are small but collectively significant.

## Scale and Minimum Size

Plan Vivo is explicitly designed for **small-to-medium community-scale projects**. There are no large minimum hectare thresholds that would exclude a startup working with smallholder communities. Projects as small as a few hundred hectares have been certified. This is a significant advantage over VCS, where the overhead of the PDD process typically demands scale (thousands of hectares or tens of thousands of tonnes of CO₂e per year) to be cost-effective.

## Plan Vivo vs. VCS vs. Gold Standard

| Dimension | Plan Vivo | VCS (Verra) | Gold Standard |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Market share** | ~0.5% of voluntary credits issued | ~71% | ~17% |
| **Target project type** | Community/smallholder, forestry & blue carbon only | All sectors, all scales | All sectors, SDG co-benefits focus |
| **Credit price range** | €15–€40/tonne (depending on project, vintage, buyer) | Broad range; REDD+ typically $5–$15 | Higher price point; strict co-benefit premium |
| **Buyer market depth** | Niche but growing; mission-aligned buyers (corporates with community narrative requirements) | Deepest liquidity; broadest buyer base | Strong among premium buyers; well-known brand |
| **Timeline to first issuance** | Typically 18–30 months from PIN to first PVC issuance (community engagement adds time upfront but validation is lighter than VCS AFOLU) | 24–48 months for new AFOLU projects | 24–36 months |
| **Cost** | Lower absolute cost than VCS AFOLU; validation and verification fees are lower due to smaller scale tolerance; no large registry fee per issuance | High: PDD development ($50K–$200K+), auditor fees, registry fees | Moderate-to-high; similar to VCS |
| **Community benefit requirement** | Mandatory 60% revenue sharing | No mandatory floor | Required co-benefits but no revenue floor |

**Key trade-off:** Plan Vivo has shallower market liquidity and lower total credit volume, meaning a startup may face harder buyer discovery. However, for buyers seeking narrative-rich, community-first credits with high integrity, Plan Vivo PVCs can command premium prices in that segment.

## Indonesia-Specific Relevance

Plan Vivo has **three certified projects operating in Indonesia** (in Sumatra and Kalimantan), with additional projects under review for certification in Sulawesi and Papua. One active project is the **West Sumatra Singkarak basin** initiative, restoring degraded land while boosting local income. Plan Vivo has also conducted biodiversity monitoring training with community peatland projects in Ketapang, Kalimantan.

Critically, in 2024 Plan Vivo signed a **Mutual Recognition Agreement (MRA) with the Indonesian government**, formally recognising PVCs within Indonesia's national carbon framework — specifically within the **Greenhouse Gas Emission Reduction Certification System (SPEI)**. This resolves a previous uncertainty about whether Plan Vivo credits would be compliant with Indonesia's domestic carbon regulations, which had been a risk factor for international standards operating in-country.

## Viability Assessment for a Small-to-Medium Indonesian Reforestation Startup

Plan Vivo is a **viable and potentially well-suited** certification pathway for a startup focused on community reforestation in Indonesia, provided:
- The project is genuinely community-driven (not plantation-style)
- The team can absorb 18–30 months before first revenue from credits
- The startup is targeting mission-aligned buyers (impact corporates, NGO offtakers) rather than the commoditised bulk market
- Scale is small-to-medium (Plan Vivo does not penalise small projects the way VCS economics do)

The MRA with Indonesia removes regulatory risk. The lower cost relative to VCS AFOLU is favourable for capital-constrained early-stage teams. The trade-off is shallower liquidity — a startup using Plan Vivo should plan for direct buyer relationships rather than relying on spot market trading.
