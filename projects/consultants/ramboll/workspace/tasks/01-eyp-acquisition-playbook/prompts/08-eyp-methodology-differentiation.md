# Prompt 08: eyp-methodology-differentiation

> **Prompt:** Given the components above, which aspects of EYP's methodology are most differentiated from what generalist engineering firms (Arup, WSP, Arcadis) offer?

---

*[Sequential — builds on Prompts 05-07 framework components + Prompts 26-27 playbook findings]*

Based on the research gathered, EYP MCF's most differentiated elements vs. generalist firms are:

## 1. Integrated Strategy-to-Commissioning Scope (End-to-End Single Firm)

EYP MCF explicitly markets a capability covering "strategic planning and roadmaps through complete engineering design and integration to commissioning, operations, and energy consulting." For a firm of ~50-100 people, this is a remarkably complete scope. Generalist firms (Arup, WSP) typically require hand-offs between strategy/advisory and design teams, or between design and commissioning agents. EYP's self-described "holistic set of offerings" under a single brand and team is a genuine differentiator — if the staffing depth actually supports it.

## 2. Sourcing and Demand Analysis Methodology

EYP developed a named, proprietary **multistep Sourcing and Demand Analysis methodology** — a comparative analysis for multiyear data center sourcing solutions covering management of application portfolios and investment options over time. This is a strategic advisory capability that most pure engineering firms (Arup Building Engineering, WSP's design teams) do not offer as an integrated service. It positions EYP as a business advisor before it is an engineering firm — a stronger entry point than a design-only pitch.

## 3. Probability Risk Analysis (PRA) for Existing Infrastructure

EYP uses **Probability Risk Analysis** for site auditing and existing infrastructure assessment. This is an actuarial/quantitative approach to reliability risk — not just "walk the site and rate the equipment" but a structured methodology for determining where limited capital should go to reduce risk. Few generalist engineering firms offer this as a named service with a defined methodology. It is particularly relevant for enterprise clients with legacy data centers making retrofit vs. replace decisions.

## 4. IUE Metric Co-Development

EYP (through Yigit Bulut) co-developed the **IUE (Infrastructure Usage Effectiveness)** metric with The Green Grid/TGGC. This is not just thought leadership — it demonstrates that EYP's practitioners were at the table when an industry measurement standard was defined. It signals practitioner-level influence on how the sector measures performance.

## 5. Modular and Containerized Deployment Expertise

EYP has documented experience designing massively modular facilities (700,000 sq ft colo, 45 containerized units deployed in 6 months). This is a capability that most traditional design firms do not have, as modular data center delivery requires a different design philosophy — designing to factory manufacturing tolerances, coordinating with module manufacturers, and integrating prefabricated units into site infrastructure. Generalist firms that primarily do traditional stick-build designs typically lack this expertise.

## 6. HPC and Supercomputing Design Experience

EYP led the design of the NCAR-Wyoming Supercomputing Center (at the time, among the world's most powerful supercomputers) and the NCSA Petascale Computing Building. This HPC track record is directly relevant to AI campus design (which is structurally similar — extreme power density, specialized cooling, mission-critical uptime). Generalist firms that have primarily designed commercial colo facilities may not have this reference base.

## What Is NOT Differentiated

- Basic MEP engineering for standard Tier III data centers: commodity capability available from dozens of firms
- LEED certification expertise: standard at all major A/E firms
- BIM-based design delivery: industry standard
- General sustainability consulting: Ramboll has strong capability but it is not differentiated within the data center segment
