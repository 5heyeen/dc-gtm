# Prompt 20: competitor-claims-vs-ramboll-eyp

> **Prompt:** What could a competitor (Arup, WSP, Arcadis) argue makes their data center capabilities equal or superior to Ramboll/EYP?

---

**Arup**
Arup would argue it has been designing mission-critical and high-technology facilities longer than EYP, with deeper global reach and more diverse technical specializations. Its structural engineering and acoustic practices are widely regarded as world-class. It would point to internal R&D and peer-reviewed work on thermal management, structural performance, and sustainability. Arup's integrated multi-discipline practice (no sub-consultants required for most disciplines) reduces coordination risk. Arup's counterargument weakness: selective about projects, perceived as expensive, less commercially aggressive than EYP in the colo/hyperscale segment.

**WSP**
WSP has made significant data center acquisitions of its own and would argue it has a purpose-built data center practice with global scale, dedicated account management for hyperscaler clients, and a track record across North America, Europe, and Asia-Pacific that Ramboll/EYP cannot match outside North America. WSP's global data center practice is large enough to staff multiple large programs simultaneously. WSP's counterargument weakness: large-scale acquisition integration creates similar dilution risks; some acquired capability may be thin in a firm of WSP's size.

**Arcadis**
Arcadis would argue its strength is in the program management and owner's representative dimension — it knows how to deliver large, complex capital programs and its playbook covers not just design but procurement strategy, contractor management, and cost control. For operators who see engineering design as a commodity and program delivery as the risk, this is compelling. Arcadis's counterargument weakness: design engineering depth for data centers is thinner than Arup or WSP.

**The common competitor argument against Ramboll/EYP**
All three would argue that Ramboll/EYP's playbook is geographically and culturally concentrated (strong in North American enterprise/HPC/colo, less proven in European hyperscale), and that a global data center program requires a more globally distributed specialist practice. Ramboll's acquisition of i3 Solutions (2024) partially addresses the European gap, but the combined practice is still young.
