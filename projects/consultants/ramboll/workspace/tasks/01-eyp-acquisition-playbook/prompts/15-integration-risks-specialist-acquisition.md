# Prompt 15: integration-risks-specialist-acquisition

> **Prompt:** What commonly goes wrong when a large generalist engineering firm acquires a specialist boutique — what integration risks are most common?

---

**Key person departure**
The most immediate and common risk. Boutique firms are built around a small number of practitioners with deep expertise and client relationships. If those individuals are not retained — through earn-out structures, equity participation, or cultural accommodation — they leave, often within 18 months, and take clients with them. EYP MCF's founding and senior principals were critical assets; the brand preservation strategy (keeping the EYP name) is evidence Ramboll understood this risk.

**Cultural collision**
Boutiques operate with flat hierarchies, fast decision-making, and direct client access. Large firms operate with governance layers, utilization targets, billing approval chains, and practice area politics. Specialists who are accustomed to calling a client directly and resolving a design issue in an afternoon find themselves navigating approval processes and rate cards.

**Rate and billing mismatch**
Large firms typically have higher overhead rates and billing structures than boutiques. This can make the acquired practice uncompetitive on smaller or mid-market projects that formed the boutique's bread and butter.

**Knowledge codification failure**
If the acquiring firm does not invest in systematically extracting and codifying the boutique's tacit knowledge, that knowledge remains person-dependent and leaves with the people.

**Brand dilution**
Rebranding too fast eliminates the market signal that the capability exists. Clients who trusted "EYP MCF" for data center work may not find "Ramboll's data center practice" without active marketing.

**Integration distraction**
The energy consumed by integration — IT systems, HR processes, insurance, liability structures, office consolidation — diverts senior practitioners from client work, reducing revenue precisely when the acquisition premium needs to be justified.
