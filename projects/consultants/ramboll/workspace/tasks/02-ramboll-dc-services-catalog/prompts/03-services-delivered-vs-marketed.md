# Prompt 03: services-delivered-vs-marketed

> **Prompt:** What is the difference between services a firm actually delivers vs. services it markets in the data center engineering and consulting sector — what signals in a service catalog indicate genuine vs. aspirational capability?

---

The gap between marketed and delivered capability is one of the most important judgment calls in competitive intelligence. In the data center engineering and consulting sector, it is especially pronounced because the market rewards comprehensive-looking catalogs, and because clients often cannot evaluate depth until they are mid-project.

**The core distinction**

A genuinely delivered service has operational infrastructure behind it: dedicated staff with relevant credentials, repeatable methodologies, reference projects with named clients or at least named geographies, and internal tools or frameworks that are not generic. A marketed service has none of those things — it exists because a partner or a business development team decided the firm should be able to say it does that thing.

**Signals of genuine capability**

**Specificity** is the strongest signal. A firm that actually delivers critical facility commissioning will describe Cx phases (ICA, L4, L5, integrated systems testing), cite specific failure modes they look for, and name the standards they work to (ASHRAE Guideline 1.7, Uptime Institute M&O Stamp methodology, TIA-942). A firm that is checking a box writes "we support commissioning activities to ensure your facility is ready for operations."

**Reference project depth** is the second signal. Real capability produces references with MW scale, tier classification, named hyperscaler or colo clients (or at least "major hyperscaler, confidential"), and specific deliverable types.

**Personnel indicators** matter enormously. Job ads, LinkedIn profiles, and team bios reveal whether the firm has hired people who have actually done the work — engineers with backgrounds at Turner & Townsend DC, CBRE DCS, WSP's critical environments group, or operators like Equinix and Digital Realty.

**Methodology artifacts** are a third signal. Firms that do the work produce proprietary checklists, calculation tools, cost databases, and decision frameworks. These get mentioned in proposals, conference talks, and technical papers.

**Signals of aspirational capability**

- **Vague action verbs**: "support," "advise," "assist," "facilitate," "coordinate." Compare to capability language that describes outputs: "we deliver the Basis of Design document," "we prepare the 100% CD set."
- **Catalog breadth without depth**: 12 distinct services on a single page with 40 words each — at least half are likely checkbox items.
- **Generalism framing**: "our engineering expertise transfers seamlessly to the data center environment" signals applying generic capability and hoping it is sufficient.
- **Absence from industry discourse**: Firms with genuine DC capability show up at DCD, Uptime Institute, in technical working groups. No footprint = treat catalog with skepticism.

**The Ramboll-specific implication**

For Ramboll, the analytical question is which parts of their data center catalog are genuinely built out versus extended from adjacent practices.
- **Likely genuine**: Sustainability and energy analysis (heat reuse, carbon reporting, water efficiency) given environmental engineering depth; structural and building services engineering given core practice
- **Worth pressure-testing**: Owner's engineer, program management, and technology advisory for hyperscale construction — these require DC-specific operational experience that a generalist firm does not automatically have
