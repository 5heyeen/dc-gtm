## 3. SITE POSITION ("Land") & POWERED LAND ACQUISITION STRATEGIES

**Development Pipeline:**
- **11 AI factory sites** in Norway listed on website, totaling **385+ MW**
- Additional sites in Sweden (number undisclosed)
- **400+ MW** under active development across Nordics
- Capacity range per site: 6 MW to several hundred MW

### Known Sites:

**3a. Fyresdal, Telemark — ACQUIRED (First AI Factory)**
- Acquired from Norsk Data AS (Fossefall's predecessor entity)
- **Operational facility** — already running
- Current capacity: **5.5 MW**, expansion target: **18 MW**
- Grid capacity already reserved — no additional power/grid licence needed
- Cold inland climate enables natural cooling
- Originally purchased by Norsk Data from Fyresdal kommune in April 2021
- **Powered land strategy:** Brownfield acquisition of existing DC with grid reservation

**3b. Harpefoss, Sor-Fron (Innlandet) — IN DEVELOPMENT**
- ~240 km north of Oslo
- Developing entity: **Fossefall SPV 1**
- Proposed site: former Harpefoss childcare center (set to close)
- Municipal council decision pending
- **Powered land strategy:** Repurposing municipal building with existing grid

**3c. Rollag, Buskerud — IN DISCUSSIONS**
- Numedal Naeringspark and Fossefall in discussions
- Plan: buy old factory, repurpose into **15 MW** data center
- Former Kongsberg Vapenfabrikk facility (1975), later Kongsberg Automotive (closed 2017)
- ~50 km west of Oslo
- **Powered land strategy:** Industrial brownfield with existing grid infrastructure

**Acquisition Pattern:**
Fossefall is systematically acquiring small-to-medium brownfield sites — former industrial facilities, municipal buildings, existing DCs — with existing grid connections. This avoids the 5-10 year Statnett grid reservation queue. Strategy: aggregate many small sites into a distributed AI factory network rather than a single mega-campus.

## 4. POWER POSITION ("Grid")

**Grid Strategy:**
- Leveraging existing grid connections at brownfield sites — avoids Statnett queue
- Fyresdal: grid capacity already reserved (5.5 MW, expandable to 18 MW without new licence)
- Rollag: existing industrial grid infrastructure from Kongsberg Automotive era
- Harpefoss: existing municipal connection, capacity TBD

**Power Sourcing:**
- Almost entirely renewable hydropower
- Northern Norway NO4 region: average spot price ~10 ore/kWh (~0.009 USD/kWh) in 2025
- Cost advantage is the core business thesis — "refine the energy, not export it"

**Grid Risk:**
- Individual sites are small (5-50 MW). Aggregating to 500 MW across 11+ sites requires securing grid at each location independently. Lower risk per site but higher operational complexity at portfolio level.
- No evidence of large-scale (100+ MW) grid reservations with Statnett

## 5. FIBER & CONNECTIVITY POSITION

**No public information found** on fiber connectivity strategy. Given the distributed, rural/semi-rural site strategy (Fyresdal, Harpefoss, Rollag), connectivity could be a constraint — these are not Oslo-area sites with dense fiber.

## 7. CORPORATE STRATEGY IN NORWAY

**What they're trying to achieve:**
- Build a distributed network of AI factories across Norway (and Sweden) powered by cheap hydropower
- Position as European AI infrastructure provider — "convert Norway's renewable energy advantage into digital infrastructure"
- Sell AI compute capacity (GPU-as-a-Service) via Seekr and Done.ai platforms, not traditional colocation
- Reach 500 MW by 2030

**Timeline Pressure:**
- Moderate. First projects operational by end 2026. No PE fund deployment deadline. But AI compute demand is moving fast — every month of delay is competitive ground lost to Nscale/Aker, Bitdeer, and international AI cloud providers.

**How Norway Fits:**
- Norway IS the entire strategy. Hydropower cost advantage is the business thesis. Sweden is secondary.
- Distributed Norwegian sites (not Oslo-centric) — rural/semi-rural locations where land is cheap and grid available.

**Differentiation Claim:**
- "We either sell the electricity cheaply as a raw material and buy back AI capacity at a high cost, or we use the power ourselves to build value." (CEO Vesterdal)

## 10. SPECIFIC PAIN POINTS

1. **Scaling from startup to multi-site operator** — 11 sites simultaneously with a small team. No evidence of program management capability or delivery framework.

2. **Brownfield conversion engineering** — converting former factories, childcare centers, and small DCs into AI-grade facilities requires specialized power, cooling, and fire engineering. AI workloads demand 50-100+ kW/rack density — fundamentally different from standard IT.

3. **Capital raising** — need significant CAPEX to develop 400+ MW. Current investors are strategic but small-scale. Will need PE, infrastructure fund, or project finance to scale. Financial close in 2026 is critical milestone.

4. **Grid aggregation risk** — relying on many small existing grid connections rather than one large reservation. Each site has its own grid constraints, utility relationships, and expansion limits. Portfolio-level power management is complex.

5. **Fiber/connectivity at rural sites** — AI workloads need high-bandwidth, low-latency connectivity. Rural Norwegian sites may lack adequate fiber infrastructure.

6. **Norwegian regulatory compliance at scale** — building permits, electrical codes (NEK 400), fire safety for AI density, environmental assessments across 11 municipalities simultaneously. Each kommune has its own process.

7. **No established advisory relationships** — no publicly identified PM, engineering, or construction advisors. This is the white space.

8. **Investor-grade governance** — when Fossefall raises institutional capital, investors will demand execution assurance, independent monitoring, and professional project governance. The startup currently has none of this visible infrastructure.
